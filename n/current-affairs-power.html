1 Import dependence

🛢️ How Pakistan's energy sector operates

the setup

The single sentence to remember: Pakistan is a major consumer of energy, not a major producer, so it has to import the difference.

Across oil, gas and coal, local production covers only a fraction of what the country burns, and the rest is bought from abroad in dollars. The scale is large: hydrocarbon imports are more than 25% of all imports (as high as 36% in some years), the annual hydrocarbon bill runs to roughly $17 to $23 billion, and energy makes up more than 50% of the trade deficit.

📌 The core fact for any answer

Local production cannot meet demand for any of the three fuels, so Pakistan must import, in dollars, and that single dependence drives every problem in the rest of this topic.

2 Why it hurts

📉 Why it is a crisis: three problems

bill · rupee · inflation

Import dependence turns into a crisis through three linked problems. Keep them separate in an answer.

Problem 1 · A large, volatile import bill

More than 25% of all imports are hydrocarbons, so the whole external account rises and falls with global fuel prices. Energy alone is more than half of the trade deficit, which means any price spike abroad immediately widens Pakistan's deficit.

Problem 2 · Price shocks plus a falling rupee

Prices rise for two reasons. First, international demand and supply and geopolitics: if Iran closes the Strait of Hormuz, or a Middle East war shuts refineries, the fuel Pakistan imports (most oil from Saudi Arabia, gas from Qatar, the majority of hydrocarbons from the Middle East) jumps in price. Second, rupee devaluation: fuel is bought in dollars, and the rupee has weakened sharply. In 2021, 1$ = 178 PKR and a $1 billion oil import cost 178 billion PKR; today 1$ is around 290 PKR and the same import costs more than 290 billion PKR. Same oil, far higher rupee cost.

Problem 3 · Inflation

Energy is the key determinant of the price of almost everything else: transport, electricity generation (about 60% of power comes from hydrocarbons), gas and electricity bills, and industrial and agricultural goods. When the fuel price rises, all of these rise with it. The Pakistan Bureau of Statistics recorded an 8%+ rise in prices driven mainly by energy.

✍️ A line worth memorising

"Energy is the better determinant of the prices of other commodities." If you can explain why that is true (transport, generation, bills, goods), you have Problem 3.

3 The way out

🌱 The solutions: indigenous generation

nuclear · hydro · solar · Thar

The strategic answer is to generate more electricity at home so that energy imports fall. Four indigenous routes, plus Thar coal.

☢️ Nuclear

Civil nuclear technology gives cheaper, carbon-free power. Chashma units I to IV are operational (about 340 MW each) and Karachi's K-2 and K-3 add about 1,100 MW each. The plan is to build more civil reactors.

💧 Hydro and dams

A pipeline of large dams: Dasu and Diamer-Bhasha are the giants (roughly 4,300 and 4,800 MW), with Mohmand, Suki Kinari (complete), Karot (complete) and Kohala (under construction) adding thousands more. The stated target is around 10,000 MW of hydel by 2025, though several projects have slipped.

☀️ Solar

The Quaid-e-Azam Solar Park in Bahawalpur is the flagship. Beyond it, net metering (the "green meter", where a household feeds power to the grid by day and draws from WAPDA at night) has documented about 2,600 MW, but there is a far larger undocumented rooftop boom across Balochistan, interior Sindh, and southern KP and Punjab. Solarisation is the fastest-moving trend.

⛏️ Thar coal

Thar holds one of the world's 4th or 5th largest coal reserves. Only Block 1 is currently producing power, with more blocks planned, so the potential is far larger than today's output. On the environmental objection, the lecturer's point: the US, China (the world's largest coal burner) and India all still burn coal, so indigenous coal is a realistic bridge for Pakistan.

4 The structural core

🏭 IPPs & capacity payments (the trap)

circular debt

The deepest part of the crisis is not fuel; it is the contracts. This is the point that separates a good answer from an average one.

Definition · Independent Power Producers (IPPs)

Private-sector companies that generate electricity and sell it to the state. IPPs supply about 60% of Pakistan's generation; WAPDA supplies roughly 30% and nuclear about 7 to 8%.

Why they were installed, in two waves. The 1994, 2005 and 2011 bunches were built because supply was short of demand. The larger 2015-onward wave was built to escape expensive furnace oil and diesel by shifting to coal and LNG, and to feed the industrialisation and urbanisation expected under CPEC (the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor).

⚠️ The trap · take-or-pay & capacity payments

IPP contracts are take-or-pay: the government must pay a capacity payment on the plant's installed capacity whether or not it uses the electricity. Because Pakistan added roughly 18,000 MW after 2015, installed capacity now exceeds demand (peak summer demand is around 17,000 to 18,000 MW, well below total capacity). So the country pays for idle plants. Many Chinese (CPEC) IPPs are paid in dollars, adding currency pressure, and the unpaid bills have built a large circular debt (into the trillions of rupees). In 2021 the government renegotiated some contracts to cut capacity payments.

🎯 Exam keywords

Take-or-pay, capacity payment, installed capacity greater than demand, and circular debt. Name all four and you have shown you understand the structural problem, not just the fuel bill.

5 The symptoms

🔌 Load shedding & expensive power

losses · theft

Two symptoms the public feels directly: the power still goes out, and it is among the most expensive on earth.

🕯️ Load shedding despite surplus capacity

Even with capacity above demand, load shedding persists, with a peak shortfall of up to about 8,000 MW. Two causes: to save dollars the government cuts fuel imports, so less power is generated; and the distribution companies (DISCOs) lose a large share of what they do deliver. Load shedding runs higher where recovery is worse (roughly 4 to 5 hours a day in cities, up to 12 hours in some rural areas).

💸 The third-most-expensive electricity in the world

Pakistan has the 3rd most expensive electricity in the world and the most expensive in Asia, roughly 40%+ above regional competitors. The drivers stack up: capacity payments, imported fuel priced in dollars, taxes, and transmission and distribution losses.

⚠️ Losses and theft (the DISCO problem)

The grid leaks value at every stage. Transmission line losses ~3%; distribution losses ~16%; and outright theft ~9%, heaviest in urban areas. High-loss distribution companies (TESCO, HESCO and others) consume units that are never billed, and honest consumers pay for it through higher tariffs.

6 Fixing it

🛠️ Reforms & the way forward

T&D · IMF

The reform agenda follows straight from the problems: generate at home, stop the leaks, and fix the contracts.

  • Cut transmission and distribution (T&D) losses. The single highest-return fix. Even a 3% loss reduction pays back a new transmission line's cost within about 8 to 9 years.
  • Build new transmission lines. For example the Matiari-to-Lahore line (around $1.3 billion); the sector needs roughly $45 billion+ of investment overall, much of it expected from China.
  • Renegotiate IPP contracts and resolve the circular debt (the 2021 capacity-payment cuts were a first step).
  • Meet IMF conditions, which push tariff increases and broader structural reforms of the power sector.
  • Shift to indigenous and renewable generation (Section 3): nuclear, hydro, solar, wind and Thar coal, to cut the dollar import bill at its source.
🎯 Likely exam question

"Critically analyse Pakistan's energy crisis and suggest reforms." Structure: the import dependence with figures, the three problems, the IPP and circular-debt trap, then reforms (indigenous generation, cutting T&D losses, renegotiating IPPs, IMF-driven structural change). Reference

📖 Glossary

IPP (Independent Power Producer)
A private company that generates electricity and sells it to the state; IPPs supply about 60% of Pakistan's power.
Capacity payment
A fixed payment the government owes an IPP for its available (installed) capacity, regardless of how much electricity is actually taken.
Take-or-pay
The contract type that forces those capacity payments: you pay for the plant's output whether you use it or not.
Circular debt
The chain of unpaid dues across the power sector (into the trillions of rupees), driven largely by capacity payments the state cannot cover.
Hydrocarbons (HC)
Oil, gas and coal; more than 25% of Pakistan's imports and the source of most of its electricity.
Trade deficit
Imports minus exports; energy alone is more than half of Pakistan's.
DISCO
Distribution company (e.g. TESCO, HESCO); high losses and theft in DISCOs raise everyone's tariff.
Net metering / green meter
A rooftop-solar arrangement: the household feeds power to the grid by day and draws from WAPDA at night.
Thar coal
One of the world's largest coal reserves, in Sindh; only Block 1 is producing power so far.
Strait of Hormuz
The Gulf chokepoint for oil and gas shipments; closing it spikes the prices Pakistan pays.
WAPDA
Water and Power Development Authority; supplies roughly 30% of generation and buys IPP power.
CPEC
China-Pakistan Economic Corridor; drove the 2015-onward wave of (often dollar-paid) IPPs and rising demand.
Load shedding
Deliberate power cuts; persists despite surplus capacity because of import cuts and DISCO losses.

Before the paper

🎯 Exam focus

  • Always quantify. This is a data topic. Use the numbers: >25% of imports, 500,000 barrels/day, $1bn = 178 vs 290 billion PKR, ~60% from IPPs, ~18,000 MW added after 2015, 3rd priciest power, ~16% distribution losses.
  • Keep the three problems distinct (import bill, rupee, inflation), and explain the causal chain for each rather than listing them.
  • Make the IPP / circular-debt point the structural core of your answer. Most candidates stop at "we import fuel"; the stronger answer explains take-or-pay capacity payments and installed capacity exceeding demand.
  • Pair every problem with a reform (import dependence → indigenous generation; losses → T&D reform; contracts → IPP renegotiation; tariffs → IMF-driven structural reform).
  • Ground it in named projects and bodies such as Chashma, Diamer-Bhasha, Quaid-e-Azam Solar, Thar, the Matiari-Lahore line, WAPDA, CPEC and the IMF, to show command of the sector.
