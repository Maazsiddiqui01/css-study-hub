⭐ The five-minute version

  • The subject. Environmental Sciences is a Group V CSS optional worth 100 marks, added in 2016 when the syllabus and subject-selection scheme were revised. It is not a pure science paper. The instructor's own definition, set out on one of his slides: it is an amalgamation of the political, social, economic and scientific paradigms of the environment, which is exactly why a business or arts graduate can score well in it.
  • One thing to check before you commit. Group V allows only one subject, so opting for Environmental Sciences rules out Botany, Zoology, Gender Studies, English Literature and Urdu Literature. Note also that the FPSC now runs a Preliminary Test (MPT) before the written exam, a change that post-dates this recording. Both are explained in section 1.
  • The three kinds of question. Scientific (the mechanism of atmospheric SO₂ removal), practical (the applicability of renewable energy in Pakistan), and political, social and economic (the fallout of the US pulling out of the Paris Accord). Even the scientific ones want the mechanism and its impact, not laboratory chemistry.
  • The six recurring themes. Climate change · environmental pollution · environmental assessment and management (EIA and SEA) · the interdisciplinary nature of the subject · global initiatives and treaties · sustainable development. Almost every past paper is built from these six.
  • The three zones. The physical environment splits into the atmosphere (air), the hydrosphere (all water) and the lithosphere (land). Together they make the biosphere, the thin shell where life exists.
  • Air. Dry air is 78.08% nitrogen, 20.95% oxygen, 0.93% argon and a sliver of carbon dioxide. It is a mixture, not a compound, and pollutants are not counted as part of it. Of the five layers, this course cares about the troposphere (weather, smog, SOₓ, NOₓ) plus the ozone layer, which sits in the stratosphere above it.
  • Water. Earth holds 1.386 billion km³. Only 2.5% is fresh, and of that 68.7% is locked in glaciers and 30.1% is groundwater. Rivers, the resource we lean on hardest, are 0.49% of the surface-water slice, which is itself only 1.2% of the fresh 2.5%. That vanishingly small number is the whole argument for conservation and recycling.
  • Why the subject exists. The Industrial Revolution is the hinge. It produced urbanisation, a shift from agrarian to industrial work, resource depletion and deforestation; then war multiplied the damage; then a run of disasters (London's killer fogs, Love Canal, Bhopal, Chernobyl, Exxon Valdez) made environmental consciousness unavoidable.
  • How to answer it. Bring tangible data. The examiner rewards applied research, so quote real figures from UNEP, the IPCC, WWF and Pakistan's climate ministry rather than writing in generalities.
1 Orientation

🎓 The subject and the paper

lecture 1.2

Environmental Sciences sits in Group V of the CSS optional subjects, alongside Gender Studies, Agriculture and Forestry, Zoology, English Literature and Urdu Literature 1.2 · 00:29. The slide dates its arrival precisely: it was introduced in 2016, following the revision of the CSS syllabus and the subject-selection scheme, and the FPSC's own records confirm it: the revised scheme is dated July 2015 and took effect for CE-2016, which is also the first year Environmental Science past papers exist.

Correction, and the most consequential one in this chapter: you may take only ONE subject from Group V. The lecture lists the group's other subjects but does not mention the constraint that governs them. Group V contains seven subjects, each worth 100 marks: Gender Studies (31), Environmental Sciences (32), Agriculture and Forestry (33), Botany (34), Zoology (35), English Literature (36) and Urdu Literature (37). The lecturer omits Botany.

The FPSC rule reads: one subject carrying 100 marks from codes 31 to 37 can be opted. Only Groups I and II allow a candidate to take 200 marks. So choosing Environmental Sciences excludes Gender Studies, Botany, Zoology, English Literature and Urdu Literature completely. If you were planning to pair Environmental Sciences with English Literature, you cannot. Plan the other 500 optional marks from the remaining groups.

Being new is not the same as being soft. The instructor is blunt about this: it is a genuinely competitive subject with real depth and many dimensions 1.2 · 00:55. The syllabus runs to about eight modules, and this course concentrates on the ones that recur in past papers while still touching the rest 1.2 · 01:24.

The definition to memorise. A later slide in this same lecture, in its green box, gives the cleanest one-line answer to "what is environmental science?": an amalgamation of the political, social, economic and scientific paradigms of the environment 1.2 · 05:52. Everything below is a consequence of that sentence.

The paper's shape

It is a 100-mark paper, the same weight as the other optionals in its bracket 1.2 · 02:38. The pattern splits it: 20 marks of MCQs, and the remainder subjective questions drawn from the recurring themes 1.2 · 03:04. That is exactly right, and the official paper confirms the mechanics:

  • Total time 3 hours.
  • Part I, the MCQs, is 20 marks (20 questions of 1 mark), to be attempted in a maximum of 30 minutes, on an OMR answer sheet.
  • Part II carries the remaining 80 marks as subjective questions.
  • There is no negative marking, so never leave an MCQ blank.

Update the lecture does not have: there is now a screening test before the written exam. This course was recorded before the change. The FPSC now runs a mandatory MCQ-based Preliminary Test (MPT) that you must clear before you may sit the written papers. It was applied for the first time to CE-2026, held on 9 November 2025: 200 MCQs, 200 marks, pass mark 33% (66 out of 200).

Three things to know about it. The MPT score does not count toward your final merit. It is valid for that year only. And appearing in it does not consume one of your attempts. It continues for CE-2027. Everything else is unchanged: 600 optional marks plus 600 compulsory, and the syllabus itself has not been revised since CE-2016. Always check the FPSC's current public notice for dates rather than relying on a recorded lecture.

The three paradigms of question

This is the most useful idea in the lecture. Every question you will meet falls into one of three families, and knowing which one you are looking at tells you how to answer it.

ParadigmExample given in the lectureWhat the examiner wants
Scientific"What are the mechanisms involved in the removal of atmospheric sulphur dioxide?"The mechanism and its effect, not laboratory-grade chemistry
Practical"The applicability of renewable energy in Pakistan"; "the impact of climate change on the food system"A reasoned, nuanced argument. Subjectivity is allowed and expected
Political, social & economicThe events leading to the UNFCCC 1992; the repercussions of the US reversal on the Paris AccordAnalysis of actors, events and consequences

On the scientific family, the instructor makes a distinction worth holding onto. A question about the removal of atmospheric SO₂ focuses on the mechanism, rather than the science of the mechanism 1.2 · 04:23. No prerequisite science degree is assumed anywhere in this paper 1.2 · 09:38.

On the political family, the worked example is the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change, 1992: what were its political implications, and what events led up to that landmark convention 1.2 · 06:11? Then the 2015 Paris Climate Accord, the American reversal under Donald Trump, and the follow-on question of what that does to the global environmental movement 1.2 · 06:39.

The rule that generates political questions. "Every phenomenon has three tangents: political, social and economic" 1.2 · 08:15. Climate change is an environmental phenomenon, so it has social repercussions across every class and group, and economic ones too. The same trick generates questions on the economic impact of acid rain or of smog 1.2 · 08:44. If you can name the three tangents of any environmental issue, you can attempt almost any essay-style question on it.

Who should take this subject. The conclusion the lecture draws: this subject is for aspirants from all domains. You do not need an engineering or medical background. A business student can opt for it and score well, because two of the three question families are analytical rather than technical, and the third does not go deep enough to require a science degree 1.2 · 10:08.

2 Paper analysis

🧭 The six recurring themes

lecture 1.3

The lecture is built on a past-paper analysis: six themes account for almost everything the examiner asks 1.3 · 00:00. Learn the themes and you know the syllabus that matters.

Theme 1 · Climate change

The one theme that appears in every paper. The instructor's own audit runs it through 2016, 2017, 2018, 2019 and 2020, and finds a question on some dimension of climate change in each 1.3 · 01:00. It is treated as a universal phenomenon that essentially nobody disputes 1.3 · 00:32. Four dimensions recur, and they are the four bullets on the slide:

  1. Theoretical perspective. Why is climate change happening, what are its sources, what is the phenomenon? The most scientific of the four, but still basic 1.3 · 01:29.
  2. Reasons, with special emphasis on anthropogenic causes. The enhancing factors, and specifically the human hand in catalysing and accelerating it 1.3 · 01:57.
  3. Social and political impact. For example, the impact of climate change on women in Pakistan. On the political side, the Kyoto Protocol and the Paris Agreement 1.3 · 02:56.
  4. Remedies. Having laid out causes, sources and catalysts, what can be done 1.3 · 03:25?

Theme 2 · Environmental pollution

The slide breaks it into four sub-headings, which double as a checklist for any pollution question 1.3 · 03:52:

Sub-themeWhat is examined
WasteMunicipal and hazardous waste: sources, causes of mismanagement, and how to manage them 1.3 · 04:25
AtmosphereSmog, acid rain and industrial emissions. Smog is the priority; acid rain is the older, more clichéd topic 1.3 · 05:21
WaterEutrophication and algal blooms, plus water logging and salinity 1.3 · 06:13
Industrial pollutionNOₓ and SOₓ emissions and the technology used to control them 1.3 · 05:51

On smog the lecture is specific: since 2016, October, November and December have been Pakistan's smog-hit months, with health, environmental and economic repercussions 1.3 · 04:52.

Themes 3 and 4 · Assessment, management, and the interdisciplinary core

Environmental assessment and management is the bureaucratic side. Once an issue exists, there are processes and programmes that address it, and by law a development plan must be modified to satisfy them. That is done through EIAs and SEAs 1.3 · 07:45. Expect questions on what an environmental impact assessment is, how EIA and SEA differ, and where each applies. An EIA question came up in 2019. Older papers, roughly five years back, asked about ISO standards and OSHA safety and health standards 1.3 · 08:15.

Correction: what these acronyms actually stand for. The lecture expands ISO as the "International Safety Organization". That is wrong on both words. ISO is the International Organization for Standardization. It is deliberately not an acronym, since the initials would change with the language; the body says it comes from the Greek isos, meaning equal. The series that matters here is ISO 14000 (environmental management), with ISO 14001 as the certifiable standard, first published in 1996 and currently in its 2015 edition.

OSHA is not an international standard at all: it is the Occupational Safety and Health Administration, a United States federal agency inside the US Department of Labor, created by the Occupational Safety and Health Act of 1970 and operating from April 1971. Its standards are US regulations. It is not ISO's counterpart, and calling it international would be an error.

One wrinkle worth knowing. The FPSC syllabus text itself still refers to OHSAS 18000 for occupational health and safety, which has been superseded: ISO 45001 was published on 12 March 2018 and OHSAS 18001 was withdrawn in March 2021. Since examiners draw on both the syllabus wording and current practice, the safe move is to name ISO 45001 and note that it replaced OHSAS 18001.

Interdisciplinary nature is the smaller theme but a reliable one, often carrying about five marks in notes-style questions 1.3 · 09:14. The point is that environmental science is not a single subject but an amalgamation: environmental sociology and feminism, environmental economics, biotechnology, and others.

Correction: the term is ecofeminism. The slide reads "Environmental Feminium" and the lecturer says "environmental feminism". The accepted academic term is ecofeminism, sometimes "ecological feminism", coined as éco-féminisme by the French feminist Françoise d'Eaubonne in 1974, in the conclusion of her book Le Féminisme ou la Mort (Feminism or Death). It argues that the domination of nature and the domination of women share a common structure. Use "ecofeminism" in an answer, and you may add "environmental feminism" as the looser synonym.

Themes 5 and 6 · Global initiatives and sustainable development

Global initiatives is the political spine of the paper 1.3 · 09:45. The slide names three clusters:

  • UNFCCC, the Kyoto Protocol and the CDM (the clean development mechanism), with implementation problems a favourite angle. This is the climate-change cluster.
  • The Vienna Convention and the Montreal Protocol, which is the ozone-depletion cluster.
  • REDD+, plus the tertiary conventions on desertification and biological diversity 1.3 · 10:16.

Reading the acronyms correctly. The slide is right where the spoken audio garbles them. UNFCCC is the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (four Cs, not three). It is the Vienna Convention for the Protection of the Ozone Layer, 1985, with the Montreal Protocol, 1987, sitting under it as the operative instrument. REDD+ stands for Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation, with the plus covering conservation, sustainable forest management and enhancement of forest carbon stocks; its rulebook is the Warsaw Framework for REDD+, agreed at COP19 in 2013. All of these are covered in full in the separate Global Environmental Agreements chapter.

Sustainable development is described as the fourth wave of environmentalism, and it is where the subject currently sits 1.3 · 10:43. The idea is not new, but the framing is: we cannot stop developing, so development has to carry the environment with it 1.3 · 11:06. The slide's issue list is SDGs, renewable energy, deforestation, desertification and rural-urban migration, then remedies 1.3 · 11:35.

The Venn diagram on the right of the slide is the standard sustainability model and is worth reproducing in an answer. Three circles, social, economic and environment, overlap in pairs to give bearable (social + environment), equitable (social + economic) and viable (economic + environment). Only where all three meet do you get sustainable. As the lecture puts it, sustainable development is the confluence of the three 1.3 · 12:05.

Use the six themes as a revision map. Write the six headings on one page: climate change, pollution, assessment and management, interdisciplinary nature, global initiatives, sustainable development. Under each, list the sub-points from these slides. That page is a complete index of what the paper has historically asked, and it tells you where your gaps are.

lecture 1.3

The closing argument of the introduction is about how to prepare, and it turns on one word: tangible data. The examiner is rewarding applied research, meaning you have real figures and you apply them 1.3 · 15:26.

For building understanding, the instructor prefers the internet over textbooks, on the reasoning that we live in a digital world and information is easier to extract from video than from a book 1.3 · 12:40. The channels named on the slide are TEDx, Zentouro, Our Changing Planet, UNEP and Crash Course, the last recommended for its topic-by-topic series 1.3 · 13:21.

For the numbers themselves, go to the organisations that produce the research 1.3 · 13:49:

Correction: two of these are misdescribed. The lecture calls GIZ a local or international NGO. It is not an NGO. Deutsche Gesellschaft für Internationale Zusammenarbeit (GIZ) GmbH is a German federal enterprise, owned by the German government and working mainly for its development ministry. Calling it an NGO in an answer would be a real error. Second, the IPCC is not simply "the United Nations": it was established in 1988 jointly by the World Meteorological Organization (WMO) and UNEP, and endorsed by the UN General Assembly. It assesses published science; it does not conduct its own research.

Editorial: get the ministry's current name right. The lecture says "Ministry of Climate Change", which was correct when it was established in 2011. It was renamed in 2023, and the correct current title is the Ministry of Climate Change and Environmental Coordination. Use the full name once, then MOCC.

What "tangible data" looks like in an answer. Not "Pakistan is highly vulnerable to climate change" but "Pakistan contributes under 1% of global greenhouse gas emissions yet ranks among the most climate-vulnerable countries, and the 2022 floods displaced roughly 8 million people". One sentence with a source-backed number is worth a paragraph of assertion. Keep a running page of figures as you revise, organised by the six themes.

The definition to write down. The three amalgamated together are called the biosphere 2.3 · 01:54. In the lecture's own phrasing: the Earth is one big biosphere, constituted of the hydrosphere, the atmosphere and the lithosphere. Why this frame is worth marks. It gives you a structure for any "impact of X" question. Take a pollutant or a policy and walk it through all three zones: what does it do to the air, to the water, to the land? Most answers that feel thin are answers that only discussed one zone. The lecture also keeps returning to the fact that the physical, biological and social environments are interdependent, so a fourth move is available: what does the change in those three zones then do to people 2.3 · 05:12?

5 Atmosphere

💨 What the air is made of

lecture 2.3

The atmosphere is a mixture of gases, not a chemical compound 2.3 · 02:50. The principal ones are nitrogen, oxygen, argon, carbon dioxide and water vapour 2.3 · 02:23.

Correction: nitrogen is 78.08%, not 78.8%. The lecturer reads the figure aloud as "78.8%" 2.3 · 03:20, but his own pie chart says 78.08%, and that is the correct value. The standard composition of dry air is nitrogen 78.084%, oxygen 20.946%, argon 0.934%. Those three alone make up 99.96% of the air. Trust the slide here, not the audio.

Editorial update: the CO₂ figure on the slide is out of date. The slide shows carbon dioxide at 0.038%, which is about 380 parts per million and reflects roughly the mid-2000s. Carbon dioxide has risen steadily since. NOAA's Mauna Loa record put it at about 431 ppm in June 2026, which is roughly 0.043%. If you quote a CO₂ concentration in an answer, use the current figure and say which year it is from. The rise itself is the point of the climate-change theme.

Why pollutants are not "part of" the atmosphere

A conceptual distinction the instructor stresses. Because the atmosphere is a mixture of gases rather than a fixed compound, particulate matter is not counted as a constituent of it 2.3 · 02:50. The slide's own caption says the same thing: the listed gases together make up the total volume of the atmosphere and this does not include the pollutants that are present. Principally, the atmosphere consists only of what is on that pie chart 2.3 · 03:20.

Carbon dioxide and the greenhouse effect

Oxygen matters because of haemoglobin. Carbon dioxide matters for a different reason: without it, the lecture argues, the planet's average temperature would collapse and almost nothing could live here 2.3 · 03:47. He is careful about what "average" means, noting it does not describe the poles or a desert but the global mean 2.3 · 04:46. The takeaway he wants is that although CO₂ is the villain of the climate debate, it is also a precondition for life 2.3 · 05:12.

Correction: the temperature figure, and what causes it. The lecture says that without carbon dioxide the world's average temperature would be minus 15°C 2.3 · 04:16. Two things are wrong with that.

First, the number. Earth's actual mean surface temperature is about +15°C (288 K). Without any greenhouse effect it would be about −18°C (255 K). The lecturer appears to have taken the "with" figure and flipped its sign. The difference the greenhouse effect makes is roughly 33°C.

Second, the attribution. That 33°C is the work of all greenhouse substances, not CO₂ alone. NASA's Goddard Institute apportions it roughly as water vapour ~50%, clouds ~25%, carbon dioxide ~20%, other gases ~5%. Water vapour, not CO₂, is the largest single contributor.

The lecturer's underlying instinct is still defensible, and it is worth knowing why: Lacis and colleagues (Science, 2010) showed that CO₂ and the other non-condensing gases supply about a quarter of the effect but control the other three quarters, because water vapour and cloud cover adjust to whatever temperature those gases set. Remove the CO₂ and the water vapour rains out behind it, and the planet does freeze. So the right sentence is: remove all greenhouse gases and Earth averages about −18°C rather than +15°C; carbon dioxide is the control knob that holds the rest of the system up.

6 Atmosphere

🪂 The layers, and the two that matter

lecture 2.3

Working outward from the ground, the layers are the troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, thermosphere and exosphere 2.3 · 05:44. The slide also marks two boundaries: the Kármán line, the conventional edge of space at about 100 km, and the exobase at the base of the exosphere.

Correction: there are five layers, not seven. The lecture says "the atmosphere classifications are also 7" and then lists five 2.3 · 05:44. The standard scheme has five thermal layers, and the five he names are exactly right. The count is the only error. If you want to be exhaustive you can add the four pauses between and above them (tropopause, stratopause, mesopause, thermopause), but do not present a seven-layer atmosphere as the accepted model.

Why only the troposphere matters here

For this course the instructor narrows the field hard: our concern is the troposphere, and beyond it only the ozone layer 2.3 · 07:12. That is because the troposphere is where the syllabus lives: smog, SOₓ and NOₓ are all tropospheric problems 2.3 · 06:14. The mesosphere, thermosphere and exosphere have no place in the paper.

Correction: the ozone layer is inside the stratosphere. The lecture describes the ozone layer as a shield sitting "on top of the troposphere" 2.3 · 06:46, then says the stratosphere is not our concern. That leaves the ozone layer floating without a home, and it matters because ozone is examined from both directions.

The ozone layer is a stratospheric feature, concentrated at 15 to 35 km, and about 90% of all atmospheric ozone is up there. It absorbs the incoming ultraviolet radiation, which is exactly the protective role the lecture describes, and it is the subject of the Vienna Convention and the Montreal Protocol.

The distinction to hold: stratospheric ozone is good, tropospheric ozone is bad. Down at ground level, ozone is not a shield but a pollutant, formed when NOₓ and volatile organic compounds react in sunlight, and it is a major component of photochemical smog. The instructor's own annotation actually captures this correctly: he writes "OZONE" up at the stratosphere and then writes "ozone" again down inside the troposphere next to "SMOG, SOₓ, NOₓ". The words on the slide are right even where the sentence is loose.

What flies where

The lecture uses vehicles to fix the layers in memory: satellites in the thermosphere, spacecraft in the exosphere, and jet planes flying above the stratosphere 2.3 · 07:42.

Correction: airliners do not fly above the stratosphere. Satellites in low Earth orbit really are in the thermosphere, so that part holds: LEO runs from about 160 to 2,000 km and the International Space Station orbits at roughly 400 km. But commercial jets cruise at about 9 to 13 km (30,000 to 42,000 feet), which puts them in the upper troposphere and the very bottom of the stratosphere, straddling the tropopause. To be above the stratosphere you would need to be beyond 50 km. Concorde, at about 18 km, was genuinely stratospheric; ordinary airliners are not.

7 Hydrosphere

🌊 The ocean, the largest habitat there is

lecture 2.3

Water is the most abundant resource the Earth has to offer 2.3 · 08:12. The oceans cover approximately 71%, and glaciers and ice caps cover additional areas.

Say what the 71% is of. The slide's wording, "the oceans cover approximately 71% water of the planet", is ambiguous, and the ambiguity is a classic exam trap. The 71% refers to Earth's surface area, not to its water. Of Earth's water, the oceans hold 96.5%. Two different numbers, two different denominators. Mixing them up is a common and very visible mistake.

The claims on the slide

  • An estimated 50 to 80% of all life on Earth is found under the ocean surface 2.3 · 08:42.
  • The oceans contain 99% of the living space on the planet, meaning the volume in which organisms can actually live 2.3 · 09:11.
  • Less than 10% of that space has been explored by humans.
  • 85% of the area and 90% of the volume is the dark, cold environment we call the deep sea 2.3 · 09:41.

The instructor's explanation for why so little is known is sound: there is no light down there, and the pressure becomes unbearable, so the deep ocean remains unknown territory even in a scientific age 2.3 · 09:41.

Correction: two of these four figures do not hold up. All four trace to a single uncited page on the MarineBio Conservation Society website, not to NOAA or any scientific agency, and two of them fail on inspection.

The 50 to 80% claim is contradicted by the evidence. The definitive biomass census (Bar-On, Phillips and Milo, PNAS 2018) puts the whole biosphere at about 550 gigatonnes of carbon, of which plants are about 450 Gt and all marine life together is only about 6 Gt, roughly 1%. Terrestrial life exceeds marine life by two orders of magnitude. Counting described species gives the same answer, because land insects dominate. Do not write "50 to 80% of life is in the ocean" as a fact. The defensible version is NOAA's qualitative one: the ocean is the largest livable space on the planet.

The "less than 10% explored" figure is out of date, and it conflates two different things. As of June 2026 the Seabed 2030 project reports 28.7% of the ocean floor mapped to modern standards, up from 27.3% a year earlier. But mapped is not seen: NOAA puts the share of the deep seafloor that has been visually observed at under 0.001%. Quote whichever you mean and say which it is.

What does hold. The deep sea, meaning below 200 m, really is about 90% of ocean volume and the largest biome on Earth; that is supported by the peer-reviewed literature (Ramirez-Llodra and colleagues, Biogeosciences, 2010), and the 85% area figure is consistent with continental shelves being only 7 to 8% of ocean area. The "99% of living space" claim is an order-of-magnitude estimate by volume, broadly reasonable but not agency-sourced. Attribute the first to the literature and hedge the second.

How to use this in an answer. The examinable point survives the corrections intact: the ocean is the planet's largest habitat by volume, it is overwhelmingly deep, cold and dark, and it is poorly explored. That is the setup for questions on marine pollution, deep-sea mining and the difficulty of governing what nobody has surveyed. Just do not attach the shakiest number to it.

The argument the table is making

Aquifers, and Pakistan's

The instructor defines an aquifer with a homely image: think of a glass buried under the earth, holding water 2.3 · 12:09. He then notes that the Indus basin aquifer is one of the world's big aquifers, and tells students to write that down as a Pakistan-specific fact.

Editorial: sharpen that into a scoring fact. The Indus is not merely large, it is in trouble, and the specific figure is worth quoting. A NASA study using GRACE satellite gravity data (Richey and colleagues, Water Resources Research, 2015) assessed the world's 37 largest aquifers and found 13 in distress, of which 8 are "overstressed", meaning almost no natural recharge offsets what is withdrawn. The Indus Basin aquifer ranks as the second-most overstressed aquifer in the world, behind only the Arabian Aquifer System. In a question on Pakistan's water crisis, that single sentence does a lot of work.

9 Hydrosphere

🔄 The water cycle

lecture 2.3

The water cycle is how water sustains itself in the environment 2.3 · 15:27. The instructor traces it by drawing on the diagram, starting at a river.

  1. Evaporation. Water leaves the river surface as vapour 2.3 · 15:55.
  2. Condensation. The vapour rises and forms clouds.
  3. Precipitation. It falls back to Earth as rain or snow.
  4. Surface runoff. What is not absorbed runs across the land, and in the end the rivers join it 2.3 · 16:21.
  5. Infiltration. As the river flows, water seeps down through the ground into the groundwater or aquifer, which is how surface water replenishes the aquifers below 2.3 · 16:51.
  6. Transpiration. Groundwater is drawn up, whether by a tubewell or by plant roots, and plants release it from their leaves back into the air, closing a second loop 2.3 · 17:20.

The two loops. Notice that the lecture describes the cycle as more than one circuit. There is the big atmospheric loop (evaporation, condensation, precipitation, runoff) and a second, slower one through the ground and through living things (infiltration, groundwater, uptake, transpiration). Lakes sit inside this too: most are fed by rivers, and the example given is the Kunhar River, which the lecture links to Lulusar Lake and Saiful Malook 2.3 · 16:51.

Editorial: the Kunhar example checks out. The Kunhar River rises at Lulusar Lake, at about 3,410 m near the Babusar Pass in the Kaghan Valley, and runs roughly 177 km through Naran, Kaghan and Balakot before joining the Jhelum. Lake Saiful Malook, at about 3,224 m and some 9 km north of Naran, drains through a stream that is a tributary of the Kunhar, so it does feed the river. It is a good local illustration of surface water and lakes belonging to the same system.

Why examiners like this diagram. The water cycle is the connective tissue between several syllabus topics. Water logging and salinity are failures of infiltration and drainage. Eutrophication is what runoff carries. Acid rain is precipitation with the chemistry changed. Groundwater depletion is extraction outrunning recharge. If you can draw this cycle and label the six steps, you can locate any water topic on it.

10 Module 3

🏭 Industrialisation and urbanisation

lecture 3.1

This is where the history of environmental thought begins. The question the lecture asks is how environmental consciousness developed in humans, and in which period 3.1 · 00:56. The answer starts with the Industrial Revolution, because it is the reason today's world can be linked back to the environment at all 3.1 · 01:28.

The benefits are not in dispute. What surprises students is the scale of the environmental cost 3.1 · 01:53. The slide's four bullets are a causal chain, and the lecture works through them in order.

  1. Transition from an agrarian society to an industrial society. Before industrialisation, the first world was as agrarian, or feudal, as the third: economies everywhere rested on land and its exploitation rather than on industry 3.1 · 02:22.
  2. A paradigm shift in the nature of work. The new industrial centres did not need field workers, they needed factory labour. Workers who had been on the land moved into industry, producing finished goods, and that movement toward the cities is what urbanisation means 3.1 · 03:17.
  3. Exponential increase in urban population, and a resultant increase in resource depletion. As cities spread, that population has to be supported: shelter, resources, entertainment. The lecture's own logic is that population growth in cities forces resource extraction 3.1 · 04:13.
  4. Enhanced deforestation. Deforestation follows urbanisation directly 3.1 · 04:41, and the lecture calls it "one of the major crimes that we have committed" 3.1 · 05:29.

The consumerism link. A step worth adding to the chain. As the product range expands, more raw material comes out of the earth. Construction alone requires steel and cement, and both come from the ground, which means mining 3.1 · 05:02. Urbanisation means more than people moving: it is a permanent increase in the material throughput of the economy.

On the deforestation figure. The slide states that enhanced deforestation rates "may cause the loss of up to 7.4 million acres of prime agricultural land each year", and credits Greenbiz. Note carefully what it claims: this is agricultural land degraded, not forest cleared, and it is a media citation rather than an agency statistic. The lecturer reads it aloud slightly differently, as 7.5 million acres 3.1 · 06:26. Quote it as the slide has it, with the attribution attached.

Editorial: the authoritative forest numbers. If you want a figure you can defend, use the FAO's Global Forest Resources Assessment instead. The current assessment (FRA 2025) reports gross deforestation of about 10.9 million hectares a year (roughly 27 million acres) and a net forest loss of about 4.12 million hectares a year (roughly 10 million acres) over 2015 to 2025, the gap between them being new planting and natural regrowth. Forests cover about 4.14 billion hectares. The trend is genuinely improving: gross deforestation has fallen from 17.6 to 13.6 to 10.9 million hectares a year across the periods 1990 to 2000, 2000 to 2015 and 2015 to 2025.

Two cautions. Do not mix the FRA 2020 and FRA 2025 series, because FRA 2025 revised the historical baselines. And keep gross deforestation and net loss distinct: blurring the two is the single commonest error in writing at this level, and it is exactly what the slide's figure does.

11 Module 3

⚔️ Wars and the environment

lecture 3.1

Industrialisation has a direct and an indirect link with war 3.1 · 06:53. Countries industrialised, economic terms were settled by force, and the two World Wars followed the two waves of industrialisation. The environmental cost of a war, the lecture says plainly, is immense 3.1 · 07:56.

Editorial: the dates, cleanly stated. The audio garbles the industrial waves. The standard periodisation is the First Industrial Revolution, roughly 1760 to 1840 (steam, coal, textiles, iron), and the Second Industrial Revolution, roughly 1870 to 1914 (steel, chemicals, electricity, the internal combustion engine, mass production). The World Wars ran 1914 to 1918 and 1939 to 1945. The second wave is what made industrial-scale warfare possible.

Reconstruction as an environmental event

The first case in point is Europe. It was destroyed across the two wars, then rebuilt through the Marshall Plan and other American doctrines 3.1 · 08:19. The lecture's argument is that this reconstruction was itself a period of unbridled development, so the environmental loss from a war continues long after the fighting stops.

Vietnam

The enemy was hiding in the jungle, so the United States used chemical agents to strip the forests away 3.1 · 08:49. The lecture offers it as the clearest single example of the environment being treated as a target.

Editorial: name the programme. The operation was Operation Ranch Hand, authorised in late 1961, flying its first mission in January 1962 and running to 1971. The agents were the "rainbow herbicides", of which the best known is Agent Orange. Note that the dioxin TCDD was not an ingredient: it was an unwanted by-product of manufacturing the herbicide 2,4,5-T.

The scale, using the authoritative figures: roughly 77 million litres (about 20 million gallons) of herbicide were sprayed, of which Agent Orange was about 11 to 12 million gallons. The area sprayed at least once was 3.6 million acres, about 1.5 million hectares, or nearly 10% of the land area of South Vietnam (US National Academies). For mangroves specifically, the FAO records that 105,000 of southern Vietnam's 291,000 hectares, some 36%, were sprayed, with the heaviest destruction on the Ca Mau Peninsula.

On terminology: these were herbicides and defoliants, not anti-personnel chemical warfare agents, and the accurate phrase is "chemical defoliants" or "herbicidal warfare". The United States held that the 1925 Geneva Protocol did not cover herbicides; the UN General Assembly disagreed in Resolution 2603 A (XXIV) of 1969; President Ford renounced first use by executive order in 1975. The Chemical Weapons Convention, whose preamble expressly recognises the prohibition on using herbicides as a method of warfare, only entered into force in 1997. So the correct formulation is that this is prohibited today but was not banned as a chemical weapon when it was carried out. The human health consequences of dioxin exposure were severe and are still litigated, and the episode gave rise to the term ecocide, coined in 1970 by the American biologist Arthur Galston in direct response to it.

The Gulf

The slide's fourth bullet reads "Gulf war 1990 - 600 oil wells set on fire", and the lecture repeats it, adding that the intensity of 600 burning wells is hard to imagine 3.1 · 09:16.

Correction: the fires were in 1991, not 1990. Iraq invaded Kuwait on 2 August 1990, but the wells were set alight by retreating Iraqi forces, with systematic ignition on 15 to 17 February 1991. The last well was capped on 6 November 1991, so they burned for roughly nine to ten months. The US Department of Defense counted 605 burning wells with about 750 of Kuwait's 943 wells damaged, so the slide's "600" is accurate. Extinguishing them took over 10,000 workers from 37 countries.

The accompanying spill is the bigger environmental fact. From 19 January 1991, between 6 and 8 million barrels were deliberately released into the Persian Gulf from tankers and the Sea Island terminal. NOAA still records it as the largest oil spill in history, roughly two to two and a half times Deepwater Horizon, and notes pointedly that it was not an accident. On land, more than 100 oil lakes held a further 25 to 40 million barrels.

One corrective on the atmospheric side, because it is often overstated: the pre-war predictions of a "nuclear winter" effect did not materialise. Hobbs and Radke found the smoke absorbed 75 to 80% of solar radiation regionally, a severe local impact, but the global climate effect was insignificant. Date the event 1991, quote the spill, and keep the climate claim regional.

Turning this into an answer. A question on war and the environment wants three mechanisms, and this lecture supplies all three: direct destruction (defoliation, burning oil wells), the industrial mobilisation that precedes the war, and the reconstruction boom that follows it. Give one named example per mechanism and the structure does the work.

12 Module 3

☠️ The disasters that woke the world up

lecture 3.1

Pollution arrives at mass scale with industrialisation 3.1 · 09:46. The production chain is simple: extract raw materials, run a factory process, ship an end product, and pollute at every step. There are three major areas of pollution: land, air and water 3.1 · 10:22, and the lecture illustrates them with five disasters where the impact ran to thousands of people.

1 · London's killer fogs

The slide lists the killer fog of 1880 and 1892, killing 700 people, and then the Great Smog of 1952 3.1 · 10:50. Coal-burning factories and domestic fires produced the smoke; the lecture attributes the underlying cause to unbridled capitalism, resources used without regard for the harm those activities caused to the environment or to people.

Correction: the 700 belongs to a year the slide does not mention. The slide pins "700 people" to 1880 and 1892. Checked against the Registrar-General's weekly returns and the figures cited in Parliament, the 700 actually belongs to the December 1873 fog, an episode the deck leaves out entirely. The Victorian killer fogs were a recurring event, not two isolated ones, and the real tolls were considerably worse:

EpisodeExcess deaths
December 1873Over 700. London's death rate ran about 40% above normal
January to February 18802,994, the deadliest Victorian episode
December 1891Attested as notable, but no reliable figure survives. Do not quote a number
December 1892About 1,484 in the fog week

Editorial: the 1952 event, which is the one to know in detail. Note first that it is the Great Smog, not the "great fog" as the lecture calls it; the slide has it right. It ran from 5 to 9 December 1952. Contemporary estimates put the excess deaths at about 4,000: weekly deaths went from roughly 945 in a normal week to 2,484 during the smog and 1,523 the week after. Later analysis by Bell and Davis, following the mortality through to February 1953 and rejecting the influenza explanation offered at the time, raises the total to about 12,000, with around 100,000 made ill.

Its real importance is legislative. It produced the Clean Air Act 1956, one of the first modern air-quality statutes anywhere, which created smoke-control areas and moved households off raw coal. It was preceded by the Beaver Committee of 1953 to 1954 and extended by a second Clean Air Act in 1968. If you are asked when air pollution became a matter of law, this is the answer.

2 · Love Canal

Love Canal was an area at Niagara Falls in the United States 3.1 · 11:20. From 1940 onward, by the lecture's account, a chemical company bought it and used it as a dumping zone for chemical waste. Once it was full it was covered over and developed, and homes and a school were built on top. Residents were exposed to carcinogens, and the scale of it was not understood until the 1970s, by which time many had become leukaemia and cancer patients 3.1 · 12:13.

Correction: the 21,000 is tons of waste, not people. The slide reads "Love canal tragedy - 1940 - 21000", a bare number with no unit, and the lecturer interprets it as 21,000 people seriously infected 3.1 · 12:13. That is not what the number refers to.

The Hooker Electrochemical Company, now Occidental Chemical, buried about 21,800 short tons of chemical waste in the canal between 1942 and 1953, not from 1940. The US Environmental Protection Agency's own site record says "over 21,000 tons of hazardous chemicals", and 82 compounds were later identified there, 11 of them suspected carcinogens.

On 28 April 1953 the company covered the site with earth and deeded it to the Niagara Falls Board of Education for one dollar, attaching a seventeen-line caveat that warned of the buried waste and purported to release Hooker from future liability. The 99th Street School opened on it in 1955, and housing followed. After chemicals surfaced in basements and gardens, President Carter declared a federal emergency in August 1978, covering 239 families, and a second in May 1980 covering about 700 more. In total roughly 950 of the area's 1,050-odd families were relocated, and the homes were demolished by 1982.

The health record is also more equivocal than "21,000 infected" suggests. What triggered the alarm in 1978 was a cluster of miscarriages, birth defects and elevated white-blood-cell counts. New York State's long-term follow-up studies found no overall excess of cancer and the same life expectancy as the rest of upstate New York, but they did find a significant increase in bladder and kidney cancer among those exposed as children, along with more preterm births and low birth weights. Present it that way and you are both accurate and more persuasive.

The tragedy's real significance is legal: Love Canal is the case that produced the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act of 1980, known as Superfund, the American law that makes polluters pay for cleaning up contaminated sites. That is the examinable point, and it is a stronger one than a casualty count.

3 · Bhopal, 1984

The slide records Bhopal, 1984, 40 tons of toxic gas. The lecture describes thousands upon thousands of people affected, in an area that became dangerous to access 3.1 · 12:41.

Correction: it was a pesticide plant, not a fertiliser plant. The lecturer calls it "a factory on fertilizers". The Union Carbide India Limited plant at Bhopal, built in 1969, manufactured pesticides, principally the insecticide carbaryl sold as Sevin. The confusion is understandable, since Union Carbide had agricultural-chemical interests and the Indian ministry with jurisdiction is Chemicals and Fertilizers, but the distinction is exactly the kind of detail an examiner notices.

The gas released on the night of 2 to 3 December 1984 was about 40 tonnes of methyl isocyanate (MIC) from tank 610, an intermediate in that process, so the slide's tonnage is right. The immediate official toll was about 2,259, with the confirmed government figure at 3,787 deaths. Over the following two decades premature deaths reached 15,000 to 20,000, and Amnesty International puts it above 22,000. Roughly 500,000 people were exposed. It remains the worst industrial disaster in history.

4 · Chernobyl, 1986

The slide records Chernobyl 1986, radiation, 20,000 km². The lecture adds that the contamination did not stay inside Ukraine and that other countries were affected too 3.1 · 13:10.

Correction: 20,000 km² matches no recognised threshold, and it understates the disaster. Contaminated area depends entirely on the contamination level you are counting, so a bare figure means nothing without the threshold attached. Checked against the OECD Nuclear Energy Agency and the IAEA's Chernobyl Forum, the slide's number does not correspond to any of the standard measures. It is six to ten times too small for the usual one, roughly a third short of the next, and about double the strictest.

MeasureArea

Around 116,000 people were evacuated in 1986, Pripyat's 49,000 of them within roughly 20 hours, and over 300,000 were eventually evacuated or resettled. Always pair an area with the threshold it refers to. The lecture's wider point, that the radiation crossed borders and made this a transboundary problem, is correct and is precisely why Chernobyl belongs in a history of environmental politics.

5 · Exxon Valdez, 1989

The final case is the Exxon Valdez oil tanker spill of 1989 in Prince William Sound, Alaska, which the lecture calls one of the most serious environmental disasters ever to take place 3.1 · 13:35. The slide records the two consequences: an estimated 250,000 sea birds died in the months after the spill, and 14 members of the 36-strong local Prince William Sound killer whale pod had disappeared by 1990.

Correction: 250,000 seabirds, not 1.5 million. Reading this bullet aloud, the lecturer says "about 1.5 million seabirds" 3.1 · 14:06. His own slide says 250,000, and the slide is right: 250,000 is the figure published by the Exxon Valdez Oil Spill Trustee Council. As with the nitrogen percentage earlier, where the audio and the slide disagree, the slide is the reliable one. The other headline losses were roughly 2,800 sea otters, 300 harbour seals and 250 bald eagles.

And it was not the biggest spill in the world. The lecture calls it "probably the biggest". About 37,000 tonnes (11 million gallons, roughly 257,000 barrels) went into the water on 24 March 1989. That made it the largest spill in United States waters until Deepwater Horizon, but on the international register it sits around 36th among tanker spills. Larger ones include the Gulf War spill of 1991 at 6 to 8 million barrels, which NOAA records as the largest ever, Deepwater Horizon (2010) at about 4.9 million barrels, Ixtoc I (1979), and several tanker losses such as the Atlantic Empress and the Amoco Cadiz.

What made Exxon Valdez a landmark was not volume but location: a remote, pristine, biologically rich sound with no infrastructure to respond, and more than 1,300 miles of shoreline oiled. It produced the Oil Pollution Act of 1990 and the requirement for double-hulled tankers. Use the ecological framing, not the volumetric one.

The killer whale figure, by contrast, is correct. Worth saying, since the rest of this bullet needed fixing. The Exxon Valdez Oil Spill Trustee Council records that the AB pod numbered 36 animals at the time of the spill and that 14 disappeared across 1989 and 1990, with no calves recruited. Mortality ran at 19% in 1989 and 21% in 1990 against an expected natural rate of about 2%.

Two refinements. AB is a resident pod, so "the local pod" is loose. And the lecture misses the group that fared worse: the AT1 transient group was stable at 22 animals from 1984 to 1989, lost nine shortly after the spill, and has since fallen to seven. It has had no recorded birth since 1984, was designated a depleted stock under the US Marine Mammal Protection Act in 2004, and is considered not recovering. If you want a single line on the long-term cost of an oil spill, that is it.

Editorial: pair each disaster with the law it produced. This is the highest-value way to revise this section, because it converts a list of tragedies into a narrative of how environmental regulation was built. London 1952 gave the Clean Air Act 1956. Love Canal gave Superfund (CERCLA) in 1980. Bhopal drove the US Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act of 1986 and reformed chemical-plant safety worldwide. Chernobyl produced the IAEA conventions on early notification and assistance, both 1986. Exxon Valdez gave the Oil Pollution Act of 1990. The through-line for an essay: environmental law is almost entirely reactive, written after the disaster rather than before it.

Which CSS group is Environmental Sciences in, and when was it introduced?

Group V, introduced in 2016 with the revision of the syllabus and subject-selection scheme. A 100-mark paper: Part I is 20 marks of MCQs in 30 minutes, Part II is 80 marks subjective, 3 hours total, no negative marking.

How many subjects may you take from Group V?

Exactly one, for 100 marks. The group has seven subjects (Gender Studies, Environmental Sciences, Agriculture and Forestry, Botany, Zoology, English Literature, Urdu Literature), and choosing one excludes the rest. Only Groups I and II allow 200 marks.

Name the eight parts of the official syllabus.

History of Environmental Thought; Sustainable Development Issues; Interdisciplinary Nature of Environmental Science; Environmental Pollution; Climate Change; Environmental Governance; Global Initiatives; Environmental Assessment and Management.

Define environmental science in one line.

An amalgamation of the political, social, economic and scientific paradigms of the environment.

Name the six recurring themes of the paper.

Climate change; environmental pollution; environmental assessment and management; the interdisciplinary nature of the subject; global initiatives; sustainable development.

What are the three tangents of any environmental phenomenon?

Political, social and economic. Naming them turns any topic into an essay structure.

What are the three zones of the physical environment, and what do they make together?

Atmosphere, hydrosphere and lithosphere. Together they form the biosphere.

Give the composition of dry air.

Nitrogen 78.08%, oxygen 20.95%, argon 0.93%, then carbon dioxide and traces. Those first three are 99.96% of it.

What would Earth's average temperature be without the greenhouse effect?

About −18°C, against an actual +15°C, so the greenhouse effect is worth roughly 33°C. Water vapour contributes about half of it, clouds a quarter, CO₂ about a fifth.

Name the five layers of the atmosphere in order from the ground.

Troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, thermosphere, exosphere.

Where is the ozone layer, and what is the good-ozone/bad-ozone distinction?

In the stratosphere, 15 to 35 km, holding about 90% of atmospheric ozone. Stratospheric ozone shields us from UV; tropospheric ozone is a pollutant and a smog component.

How much water is on Earth, and how much of it is fresh?

1.386 billion km³. Only 2.5% is fresh; 96.5% is ocean and a further 0.9% other saline water.

Break down that 2.5% of fresh water.

Glaciers and ice caps 68.7%, groundwater 30.1%, surface and other freshwater 1.2%.

What share of fresh water is in rivers?

0.49% of the surface-and-other category, itself only 1.2% of fresh water. That fraction is the whole case for conservation and recycling.

Where does the Indus Basin aquifer rank globally?

Second-most overstressed aquifer in the world, per NASA GRACE satellite data (Richey et al., 2015), behind only the Arabian Aquifer System.

List the steps of the water cycle.

Evaporation, condensation, precipitation, surface runoff, infiltration into groundwater, and transpiration from plants.

What was the first environmental consequence of the Industrial Revolution?

Urbanisation: the shift from an agrarian to an industrial society, a change in the nature of work, exponential urban growth, then resource depletion and deforestation.

Which programme defoliated Vietnam's forests, and with what?

Operation Ranch Hand, 1961 to 1971, using herbicides including Agent Orange. About 20 million gallons sprayed, roughly 2 million hectares defoliated. Herbicides, not chemical weapons in the CWC sense.

When were the Kuwaiti oil wells set on fire, and how many?

January and February 1991, by retreating Iraqi forces, with the last capped in November 1991. Between 600 and over 700 wells. The slide's "1990" is the invasion year, not the fires.

Which London fog killed 700, and what did the others kill?

December 1873 killed over 700. January to February 1880 was the deadliest Victorian episode at 2,994. December 1892 killed about 1,484. The Great Smog of 5 to 9 December 1952 killed about 4,000 at the time, revised to roughly 12,000, and produced the Clean Air Act 1956.

What does the 21,000 at Love Canal actually refer to?

About 21,800 tons of chemical waste buried by Hooker Electrochemical between 1942 and 1953, not 21,000 people. The site was deeded to the school board for one dollar in 1953. Roughly 950 families were relocated. It produced Superfund (CERCLA) in 1980.

How much land did Chernobyl contaminate?

Depends on the threshold, which must always be stated. Above 37 kBq/m² of caesium-137: over 200,000 km² across Europe, about 150,000 km² of it in Belarus, Russia and Ukraine. Above 555 kBq/m²: about 10,300 km². The exclusion zone itself is about 2,600 km².

What kind of plant was Bhopal, and what gas escaped?

A Union Carbide pesticide plant making carbaryl (Sevin), not a fertiliser plant. About 40 tonnes of methyl isocyanate escaped on the night of 2 to 3 December 1984.

How many seabirds died in the Exxon Valdez spill?

About 250,000, per the Exxon Valdez Oil Spill Trustee Council. Also around 2,800 sea otters, 300 harbour seals and 250 bald eagles. It was the largest spill in US waters at the time, not the largest in the world.

Pair four disasters with the laws they produced.

London 1952 → Clean Air Act 1956. Love Canal → CERCLA/Superfund 1980. Chernobyl 1986 → IAEA early notification and assistance conventions. Exxon Valdez → Oil Pollution Act 1990.

Question types this chapter prepares you for

  • "Discuss the interdisciplinary nature of environmental science." Use the one-line definition from section 1, then the three question paradigms, then the disciplines from the slide: environmental sociology and ecofeminism, environmental economics, biotechnology.
  • "Water scarcity in Pakistan." Open with the water budget: 2.5% fresh, 68.7% of that frozen, rivers at 0.49%. Then the Indus aquifer as the second-most overstressed in the world. Then conservation and recycling as the conclusion the arithmetic forces.
  • "Trace the evolution of environmental consciousness." Industrial Revolution → urbanisation and resource depletion → the two World Wars and their reconstruction → the disaster sequence from London 1952 to Exxon Valdez 1989 → the legislative response each one produced.
  • "Explain the greenhouse effect." Give the 33°C figure, apportion it correctly across water vapour, clouds and CO₂, and then make the control-knob argument: the non-condensing gases set the temperature the water vapour responds to.
