1 The method

📐 The comparative frame

00:01 – 00:03

The lecture is built on one move: take the UK as the yardstick and compare every other system to it 00:01:41. The countries on the board are the UK, Pakistan, India, Malaysia and Germany, and each institution is run across all five in turn.

The reason the UK is the yardstick is that these are all parliamentary systems descended from it, so the same parts appear in each: a ceremonial head of state, a strong or weak upper house, a powerful directly elected lower house, and a prime minister who actually governs. Once you can place any one country's institution on the UK template, you can answer a question on any of them. The lecturer's promise is exactly that: learn the power and functions of the UK prime minister, and you can apply the same frame to Pakistan's 00:50:36.

How comparative-government questions are set. They rarely ask for one country in isolation. They ask you to compare, so build the habit this lecture builds: pick the institution (head of state, upper house, lower house), then give all the countries on it in a row. The tables in the next three sections are laid out to be reproduced exactly that way.

Queen Elizabeth II died on 8 September 2022, and the head of state is now King Charles III. The substance is unchanged (hereditary, for life, no real power), but write King in 2026.

Pakistan's electoral college

Pakistan's president is indirectly elected 00:05:40, for a five-year term, by an electoral college made up of the members of the National Assembly, the members of the Senate, and the members of the four provincial assemblies 00:06:23. Indirect election is the mark of a figurehead: the people do not choose the president, the assemblies do.

India, Malaysia, Germany

  • India: the president is indirectly elected for five years by the members of both houses of the central legislature plus the members of the state assemblies 00:09:05.
  • Malaysia: before independence Malaysia was made up of nine sovereign states, each with its own ruler 00:09:42. Its king is chosen from among those nine rulers for a five-year term, so the crown rotates among them.
  • Germany: the president is elected for five years by the members of both houses 00:14:59, and like the others is a figurehead. (Editorial precision: the actual electing body is the Federal Convention, the Bundestag plus an equal number of state delegates, rather than the Bundesrat sitting as such.)

Correction: India has 28 states, not 29. The lecture says the Indian president is elected by "the 29 state assembly members." In 2019, Jammu and Kashmir was reorganised into two union territories (Jammu & Kashmir and Ladakh), reducing the count from 29 to 28 states. Write 28.

3 The second chamber

🏛️ Upper houses compared

00:15 – 00:33

Upper houses divide sharply into powerful and weak, and the test is simple: can an ordinary bill become law without the upper house's consent? Where it cannot, the house is powerful.

The UK House of Lords: a showpiece

The Lords are appointed by the monarch and form the house of nobles 00:16:45. Once this was a powerful house that checked the crown, but in the UK's conservative, status-quo settlement it has become just a showpiece 00:18:01. The lecturer's proof is the quorum, the minimum members needed for the house to do business. For Pakistan's Senate, and for any house, the usual quorum is a quarter of the members, but for the House of Lords, with over a thousand members historically, the quorum is just three 00:19:09. A house whose business can proceed with three members present is a house that is not expected to decide anything.

Correction on the Lords' size. The lecture says the House of Lords has "more than a thousand members." It did historically, but the House of Lords Act 1999 removed most hereditary peers, and its membership is now around 800. The quorum of three for general business is still correct (a division on legislation needs 30).

Pakistan's Senate: powerful, and equal representation

Unlike the Lords, Pakistan's Senate is very powerful 00:23:36. No law, ordinary or constitutional, is passed until the Senate passes it, with one exception, the budget (a money bill) 00:23:36. Senators serve six-year terms, half retire every three years, and they are indirectly elected by the provincial assemblies, with each province returning the same number of senators whether it is large or small 00:22:10. That equal-per-province representation is the point of a senate: it protects the small provinces against being outvoted by the large ones.

Correction: the Senate now has 96 members, not 104. The lecture gives 104 and 23 senators per province. After the 25th Amendment (2018) merged FATA into Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, FATA's 8 Senate seats were abolished, phased out to bring the total from 104 down to 96 by 2024. Each of the four provinces still returns 23 senators (4 × 23 = 92), plus 4 for the Islamabad Capital Territory.

India, Malaysia, Germany

  • India's Rajya Sabha is indirectly elected by the state legislatures on a population basis, larger states sending more, and the lecturer gives it the same power he gave Pakistan's Senate 00:26:12.
  • Malaysia's Dewan Negara has 70 members 00:27:02: with 13 states each sending 2, that is 26 elected by the state assemblies, and the remaining members are appointed by the king at his discretion 00:28:04. Selected, not elected 00:30:08.
  • Germany's Bundesrat is the middle case 00:31:03: an ordinary law can pass without it, but a constitutional law cannot 00:32:28.

Two corrections here. First, the lecture treats the Rajya Sabha as equal in power to Pakistan's Senate, saying no bill passes without it except the budget. That overstates it: in a deadlock India can call a joint sitting of both houses, where the far larger Lok Sabha usually prevails, and on a money bill the Rajya Sabha can only delay 14 days. The Rajya Sabha is strong but not co-equal. Second, the Bundesrat's members are delegates of the state (Land) governments, not members "elected for a four-year term"; they change whenever a state government changes.

4 Where power sits

🗳️ Lower houses compared

00:33 – 00:39

The real power in every one of these systems sits in the directly elected lower house. The lecturer's test for why is crisp: the UK House of Commons is powerful because the House of Lords cannot stop it 00:38:15.

† UK Commons figures added as current reference; the lecture gives the Commons its principle (it cannot be stopped by the Lords) rather than a seat count. The Pakistan and Malaysia figures are the lecturer's, with Pakistan's updated per the correction below.

Pakistan's National Assembly

The National Assembly is directly elected for five years. General seats are contested at the election, and the reserved seats, 60 for women and 10 for minorities, are then allocated to parties in proportion to the general seats each won 00:35:04. This is the house that matters: the prime minister sits here, the cabinet is drawn from here, and the budget is passed here 00:36:35.

Correction: the National Assembly now has 336 seats, not 342. The lecture gives 272 general seats (342 total with the 60 women and 10 minority seats). After the 25th Amendment (2018) merged FATA into Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, FATA's 12 seats were halved and then folded in, cutting the general seats from 272 to 266 and the total from 342 to 336 (266 + 60 + 10). The reserved-seat rules are unchanged.

Why the Commons cannot be stopped

The lecturer states the principle, that the Lords cannot stop the Commons, without naming the instrument. Worth adding for a stronger answer: the instrument is the Parliament Acts of 1911 and 1949, which stripped the Lords of the power to veto legislation and left them only the power to delay it. This is exactly why the directly elected house is supreme: the appointed house was legally subordinated to it.

5 One state or many

🗺️ Unitary vs federal

00:41 – 00:51

This is the sharpest single distinction in the lecture, and it turns on where a region's authority comes from 00:41:38.

Unitary (UK)Federal (Pakistan)
Source of local authorityDelegated by the central governmentGiven by the constitution
Can the centre withdraw it?Yes, at will, "within seconds", because it is the centre's own to give 00:45:21No. The centre did not grant it, so the centre cannot take it back
What protects the regionNothing but the centre's convenience (efficiency)The constitution itself

In the unitary UK, local government has authority only because the central government delegated it, and what the centre delegates the centre can pull back whenever it chooses, because it was the centre's in the first place 00:45:21. In federal Pakistan the provinces' authority was given by the constitution, not handed down by the centre, so the centre cannot simply withdraw it 00:45:21.

The deeper point: the value of a constitution. The lecturer keeps returning to it. An ordinance is not permanent and carries no real weight; a constitution does, because breaking it carries a political cost 00:48:12. The UK is the interesting case: it has no single written constitution, so in effect its constitution is the House of Commons, and that parliamentary supremacy is why a unitary centre can reshape local government at will 00:46:47.

Editorial balance for a top answer. The unitary/federal contrast is sound as a principle, but do not draw it too hard. The UK has since devolved real powers to Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland, and although Westminster remains legally supreme, withdrawing those powers would now be a serious constitutional step, not a matter of seconds. On the other side, Pakistan's 18th Amendment (2010) is what most strongly entrenched provincial autonomy in the constitution, which is the modern anchor for the lecturer's point.

6 The army and the state

🎖️ The military in Turkish politics

00:51 – 01:17

This is a set past-paper topic: the role of the military in Turkish politics 00:51:27. The lecturer's method is to open with the historical background, and the background explains everything that follows.

Why the military was powerful: it built the state twice

Before 1924 Turkey was the Ottoman Empire 00:53:28. An empire is built by expansion, and expansion is done by the army, so the army made the Ottoman Empire 00:54:18. When that empire was destroyed in the First World War, modern Turkey was rescued by a military general, Mustafa Kemal Ataturk, its founder and saviour 00:55:47. So the military made modern Turkey too, and an institution that founds a state writes itself deep into that state's constitution.

Correction on dates and the "guardian" clause. Turkey's key dates: the sultanate was abolished in 1922, the Republic was declared in 1923, and the caliphate was abolished in 1924, so "before 1924" is a reasonable shorthand. But the lecture then says the military's role as guardian of the state comes from a constitution written "in the 1930s (32, 33, 34)". That is not right. Turkey's constitutions are 1921, 1924, 1961 and 1982; there is no 1930s constitution. The military's guardian role comes from the 1961 Internal Service Law (Article 35) and is reinforced by the 1961 and 1982 constitutions and the National Security Council. Attribute the guardianship to 1961 onward, not the 1930s.

How powerful the institution was

The signs of that power, as the lecturer lists them:

  • The military was the constitutional guardian of the state 00:56:33.
  • The National Security Council, responsible for every important internal and external policy of Turkey, was headed by the army chief 00:58:25.
  • Military courts tried civilians, which the lecturer flags as the clearest sign that a democracy is not real 00:59:47.
  • Retired military generals headed civilian institutions (a steel mill, PIA and the like) 01:01:00.
  • There was no civilian audit of the military budget, unlike in Western democracies 01:01:42.

The reforms that pushed the army back

Then the state rolled the military out of politics, and this is the part an answer must reach, because the topic is really about that transition 01:04:14:

ReformWhat changed
Guardianship removedThe guardian role and martial-law provisions were taken out of the framework, and the military is no longer the guardian of the state 01:04:14
National Security CouncilStill exists and still handles key policy, but is now chaired by the civilian President, not the army chief 01:05:02
Military courtsThe trial of civilians in military courts was ended 01:05:02
Civilian institutionsFrom 2007 onward, no military general could head a civilian institution 01:05:02

Correction on the reform dates. The lecture puts the removal of the guardian clause at "1987", which is not right. The military's political power was rolled back mainly by the EU-harmonisation reforms of 2001 to 2004 (which gave the National Security Council a civilian majority and advisory status), and the guardianship wording of Article 35 of the Internal Service Law was amended in 2013. Use "the EU reform decade, roughly 2001 to 2013" rather than 1987.

Why the military accepted the retreat

Three reasons in the lecture 01:14:04:

  • The EU membership bid. Civilian control of the military is an EU condition, and Turkey wanted in 01:09:13. (Turkey is a candidate, and has been since 1999, but is still not a member 01:09:57.)
  • A changed threat perception. Turkey no longer felt an imminent external threat from Europe, which reduced the case for a politically dominant army 01:10:21.
  • Democratic consolidation. Turkey stabilised and grew in standing, which the lecturer credits to Erdogan's early period 01:14:04.

The honest coda. The lecturer does not leave Erdogan as a hero. He notes that a leader once praised for consolidating democracy shifted into reverse gear toward authoritarianism, with opponents jailed 02:01:52. So the arc has two faces: the military left politics, but civilian strongman rule is its own danger. That balance is exactly what a strong essay on Turkish civil-military relations should carry.

7 The presidential turn

🗳️ Turkey's presidential system & France

01:21 – 02:06

The second half of the Turkey story is constitutional: a 2017 referendum converted Turkey from a parliamentary to a presidential system, and the lecturer uses it to teach the two-round election that France also uses 01:24:19.

Before and after the referendum

FeatureBefore (parliamentary)After 2017 (presidential)
Grand National Assembly550 members 01:22:17600 members 01:25:08
Term4 years5 years 01:25:08
Prime MinisterHead of government, cabinet drawn from the GNA 01:22:37Abolished 01:25:56
PresidentElected indirectly by the GNA, 5 years, no real authority, acted on the PM's advice 01:23:29Directly elected, head of state and government

Correction: the referendum was April 2017, and it was 18 amendments. The lecture says "September 2017" and "72 articles changed". The Turkish constitutional referendum was held on 16 April 2017, on a package of 18 constitutional amendments. The 550-to-600, four-to-five-year and prime-minister-abolished changes are all correct; the date and the "72 articles" figure are not.

The two-round system (Turkey and France)

The president is now directly elected by the people, on a two-round system 01:26:51. The rule 01:29:24:

  1. Every party may field a candidate. Suppose five candidates run.
  2. Round one: if any candidate wins more than 50% of the vote, there is no second round; that candidate is president.
  3. If no one clears 50%, a second round is held between the top two candidates from round one, and whoever wins that runoff becomes president.

The lecturer's real example: Erdogan took about 53% in the first round, so no second round was needed 01:31:51. And the French president is elected by exactly this system 01:26:51, which is why it matters. The 2016 constitutional-law paper asked how the French president is elected 01:31:51, and this two-round answer is the answer.

Why "more than 50%", not "51%". The lecture says 51% in places; the actual rule is an absolute majority, meaning more than half (50% + 1). A candidate on exactly 50% has not cleared the bar; a candidate on 50.1% has. Write "more than 50%" or "absolute majority".

How strong the new president is

The lecturer calls it "more than presidential", stronger even than the US president 01:33:44. The checks and powers he draws out:

  • No vote of no confidence. The assembly cannot remove the president by a no-confidence vote 01:34:33.
  • Impeachment only. For gross misconduct or corruption the GNA can move to impeach, requiring a two-thirds majority, and impeachment is a separate, harder process 01:34:33.
  • The real check: simultaneous elections. If the president dissolves the assembly, presidential and parliamentary elections are held together, so dissolving the assembly forces the president to face re-election too 01:36:33. That mutual risk is what restrains him.
  • Legislation. The president can refuse to sign a law; the GNA can then override him by passing it again with a majority of its total members, and the lecturer stresses this is not a US-style veto 01:39:19. His worked example used a 400-member house needing 201 01:39:48; for today's 600-member assembly that majority is 301.

France: the same family, with a prime minister

France runs the same two-round presidential election, but is not a pure presidential system, because it also has a prime minister sitting alongside the president 01:45:30. The French president can dissolve the National Assembly, but there is a check: not twice in one year 01:46:00. And bills can be referred for a judicial review of their constitutionality to the Constitutional Council, which the president refers 01:42:43.

Correction: unitary states do have high courts. The lecture says Turkey and France are unitary and that "a unitary state has no supreme court", only a constitutional council. Take care with this. It is true that France uses a Conseil constitutionnel for constitutional review (and separately a Conseil d'État and Cour de cassation as its top courts), but the general claim that a unitary state has no supreme court is not a rule: unitary states can and do have supreme or constitutional courts. Turkey itself has a Constitutional Court (Anayasa Mahkemesi), not merely a council. Present it as France's particular arrangement, not a law of unitary states.

Reference

📖 Glossary

Head of state vs head of government
The head of state is the ceremonial figurehead (a monarch or president in these systems); the head of government is the prime minister, who actually governs.
Paper tiger / rubber stamp
The lecturer's phrase for a head of state with power in theory but not in practice.
Indirect election
Election by an assembly or electoral college rather than by the people directly; the mark of a figurehead office.
Electoral college (Pakistan president)
The National Assembly, the Senate and the four provincial assemblies, who together elect the president for 5 years.
Quorum
The minimum number of members present for a house to conduct business. Usually a quarter; three for the UK House of Lords, which shows how little it now decides.
House of Lords
The UK upper house: appointed peers, no power to veto legislation, a showpiece.
Senate (Pakistan)
The powerful upper house: equal representation per province, 6-year terms with half retiring every 3 years, and consent needed for every bill except the budget. 96 members today.
Rajya Sabha
India's upper house, elected by state legislatures on population; strong but not co-equal with the Lok Sabha.
Dewan Negara / Dewan Rakyat
Malaysia's upper house (part elected, part royally appointed) and lower house (222 directly elected members).
Bundesrat
Germany's upper house, made of delegates of the state governments; ordinary law can pass without it, constitutional law cannot.
Parliament Acts 1911 & 1949
The UK laws that removed the Lords' veto and left them only delay, making the elected Commons supreme.
Unitary state
One where regional authority is delegated by the centre and can be withdrawn by it (the UK).
Federal state
One where regional authority is granted by the constitution and cannot be withdrawn by the centre (Pakistan).
Ordinance
A temporary executive order, not permanent and without the standing of constitutional law.
Guardian role (Turkish military)
The military's constitutional self-definition as protector of the republic, from the 1961 Internal Service Law, used to justify its dominance until the EU-era reforms.
National Security Council (Turkey)
The body handling key internal and external policy; once chaired by the army chief, now by the civilian President.
Grand National Assembly (GNA)
Turkey's parliament: 550 members before the 2017 referendum, 600 after.
Two-round system
A presidential election where a candidate needs more than 50% in round one to win outright, otherwise the top two contest a runoff. Used by Turkey and France.
Constitutional Council (France)
The body that reviews a bill's constitutionality on the president's referral. France's arrangement, not a rule for all unitary states.
Judicial review
A court or council checking a law against the constitution and striking it down if it violates fundamental rights.
Self-test

🎴 Flashcards

What is the comparative method of this lecture?

Take the UK as the yardstick and run each institution (head of state, upper house, lower house, unitary vs federal) across the UK, Pakistan, India, Malaysia and Germany.

What is a head of state in a parliamentary system?

A figurehead: power in theory but not in practice. The lecturer's phrase is "paper tiger, rubber stamp". Real power is with the PM and the elected lower house.

Who is the UK head of state now?

King Charles III. Queen Elizabeth II died on 8 September 2022. Hereditary, for life, no real power. (The lecture says "Queen".)

Who elects Pakistan's president, and for how long?

An electoral college of the National Assembly, the Senate and the four provincial assemblies, for a 5-year term. Indirectly elected.

How many states does India have?

28, since Jammu & Kashmir became two union territories in 2019. (The lecture says 29.)

How is Malaysia's king chosen?

From among the 9 royal state rulers, for a 5-year term. The crown rotates among them.

What makes an upper house powerful?

Whether an ordinary bill can become law without it. If it cannot, the house is powerful (Pakistan's Senate); if it can, the house is weak (UK Lords).

Why is the House of Lords called a showpiece?

It has no power to veto legislation and its quorum for general business is just three, out of a house of around 800.

How powerful is Pakistan's Senate, and how many members?

Very powerful: no bill, ordinary or constitutional, passes without it except the budget. 96 members today (was 104 before the FATA merger under the 25th Amendment).

Senate term and rotation?

6-year terms, with half the senators retiring every 3 years. Indirectly elected by the provincial assemblies, equal number per province.

Is India's Rajya Sabha equal to Pakistan's Senate?

No. It is strong but not co-equal: a deadlock can go to a joint sitting where the larger Lok Sabha prevails, and it can only delay a money bill 14 days.

Describe Malaysia's Dewan Negara.

70 members: 26 elected by the 13 state assemblies (2 each), the rest appointed by the king at his discretion.

Where does the Bundesrat sit on power?

In the middle: an ordinary law can pass without it, but a constitutional law cannot. Its members are delegates of the state governments.

Why is the House of Commons powerful?

Because the Lords cannot stop it. The Parliament Acts of 1911 and 1949 removed the Lords' veto, leaving only delay.

Pakistan's National Assembly composition?

336 seats: 266 general, 60 reserved for women, 10 for minorities. Reserved seats are allocated in proportion to general seats won. (The lecture's 272 general / 342 total predates the FATA merger.)

Unitary vs federal, in one line?

Unitary: regional authority is delegated by the centre and can be withdrawn (UK). Federal: regional authority is given by the constitution and cannot (Pakistan).

In the UK, what is the constitution in effect?

The House of Commons. With no single written constitution, parliamentary supremacy lets a unitary centre reshape local government at will.

Why was the Turkish military so powerful?

It built the Ottoman Empire and modern Turkey (Ataturk was a general), so it was written into the state as its guardian.

Where does the military's "guardian" role actually come from?

The 1961 Internal Service Law (Article 35) and the 1961/1982 constitutions, not a 1930s constitution. (The lecture says 1930s.)

Name the signs of the Turkish military's old dominance.

Constitutional guardian, National Security Council chaired by the army chief, military courts trying civilians, generals heading civilian bodies, and no civilian audit of the defence budget.

How was the military rolled back?

The guardian role went, the NSC is now chaired by the civilian President, military courts stopped trying civilians, and from 2007 no general could head a civilian institution. (Mainly the EU reforms of 2001 to 2013, not 1987.)

Three reasons the military accepted the retreat?

The EU membership bid (civilian control is an EU condition), a reduced external threat perception, and democratic consolidation under early Erdogan.

What did Turkey's 2017 referendum change?

It switched Turkey to a presidential system: GNA 550 to 600, term 4 to 5 years, prime minister abolished, president directly elected. Held 16 April 2017 (not September), 18 amendments.

Explain the two-round presidential system.

If a candidate wins more than 50% in round one, they win outright; otherwise the top two contest a runoff. Used by Turkey and France. Erdogan took about 53% in round one.

Why "more than 50%", not "51%"?

The rule is an absolute majority, more than half (50% + 1). The 51% figure is a loose approximation.

Why is Turkey's president "more than presidential"?

No vote of no confidence against him; removal only by a two-thirds impeachment. He is stronger than even the US president in that respect.

What is the real check on Turkey's president?

Simultaneous elections: if he dissolves the assembly, presidential and parliamentary elections are held together, so he must face re-election too.

How does the GNA override a president who won't sign a law?

By passing it again with a majority of its total members. The lecturer's worked example was 201 of a 400-seat house; for today's 600 seats that is 301. It is not a US-style veto.

How does France differ from a pure presidential system?

It also has a prime minister alongside the president (a semi-presidential system). The president can dissolve the National Assembly, but not twice in one year.

Do unitary states have no supreme court?

No, that is not a rule. France uses a Constitutional Council for review, but Turkey has a Constitutional Court, and unitary states can have supreme courts. Present it as France's particular arrangement.

Before the paper

🎯 Exam focus

  • Use the current figures, not the lecture's. This lecture predates 2018, so update every number: India 28 states, Pakistan Senate 96, National Assembly 336 (266 general), UK head of state King Charles III. Writing the old figures is the easiest way to lose a mark on this topic.
  • Answer by institution across countries, not country by country. Head of state, then upper house, then lower house, then unitary vs federal, giving all five countries on each. That is what a comparative-government question rewards, and it is how the three tables here are built.
  • Lead every institution with its power test. Head of state: figurehead or real. Upper house: can an ordinary bill pass without it. Lower house: it cannot be stopped by the upper house. Region: is its authority delegated or constitutional.
  • For unitary vs federal, name the source of authority. Delegated by the centre (unitary, UK) versus given by the constitution (federal, Pakistan). Add the 18th Amendment for Pakistan and devolution for the UK to show it is not absolute.
  • Turkish civil-military relations is a two-part answer. First why the army was dominant (it founded the state, hence guardian), then how it was rolled back (NSC civilianised, military courts ended, generals barred from civilian posts, driven by the EU bid). Finish with the honest coda: the army left, but Erdogan's authoritarian turn is the new concern.
  • The two-round system is the reusable payoff. The 2016 paper asked how the French president is elected; the answer is this system, and Turkey now uses it too. Say "more than 50%", not 51%.
  • Keep Turkey's presidency and France apart. Turkey is a pure presidential system with no prime minister; France is semi-presidential, keeping a prime minister beside the president.
  • Do not over-claim on courts. "Unitary states have no supreme court" is wrong as a rule. France's Constitutional Council is France's arrangement; Turkey has a Constitutional Court.
