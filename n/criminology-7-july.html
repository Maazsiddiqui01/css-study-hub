⭐ The five-minute version

  • Why this topic exists. Crime has evolved. Classical criminology studied street crime, theft, robbery, assault. The 21st century added terrorism, cybercrime, money laundering and organized networks, so the FPSC now expects you to analyse these modern crimes as a criminologist, not just describe them.
  • The security-threat chain. Radicalization (a process of adopting extreme beliefs) → Violent Extremism (a mindset that accepts violence as legitimate) → Terrorism (the violent act). Four theories (Social Learning, Differential Association, Social Control, and Strain) explain why an ordinary person travels this path.
  • The modern economic crimes. White-collar crime (Sutherland, non-violent, from a professional position), organized crime (structured, hierarchical syndicates), and money laundering (placement → layering → integration). Each paired with the Pakistani law that governs it.
  • The newest frontiers. Cybercrime and cyber-warfare (PECA 2016, NCCIA), and gender-based violence, which looks very different in urban versus rural Pakistan.
  • What earns marks. Precise definitions (radicalization vs extremism vs terrorism; tax avoidance vs evasion; gang vs organized crime), Pakistani laws and bodies named with their year, a "why + prevention" lens, and no factually wrong claims. Past papers ask this topic every year (see Section 08).
1 Contemporary criminology

🧭 Why "contemporary" criminology?

00:00 – 05:00

The lecture opens with the examiner's implicit question: why must a candidate learn modern concepts in contemporary criminology at all?

The answer is set up as a logical flow from earlier in the course. Section 3 dealt with the criminal-justice system (interrogations, interviews and investigative techniques) and stressed how significant those techniques are. This part now asks: significant for what kinds of crime? Because the crimes themselves have changed.

The governing idea, which recurs all lecture, is that the nature of crime changes with time and space. What counts as crime, and how it is committed, is not fixed; it shifts as societies change (this links back to the Section 1 definitions of deviance and crime). "Contemporary" therefore means the examiner wants you to read crime through a 21st-century lens and understand modern crimes as a criminologist.

By the 21st century, criminals are no longer confined to local thefts and conventional acts of violence. Today law-enforcement agencies face terrorism, cybercrime, money laundering, organized criminal networks and finance-related crimes. Crime has grown in scale and become sophisticated, using modern technology in ways classical criminology never anticipated. Crime has evolved in three directions at once: in its nature, in its technology, and in its variety.

💡 The core argument

Because crime is evolving on all three fronts, ordinary policing is no longer enough. The modern response needs community-based policing, public–private partnership, and specialized authorities. This is exactly why the FPSC syllabus shifts from traditional criminal investigation toward contemporary criminology, bringing in intelligence, technology, media and community as tools of analysis and prevention.

The opening slide lays out the whole territory. Under Modern Concepts in Contemporary Criminology: terrorism, radicalism and the war on terror; media's representation of crime; modern law enforcement and crime prevention; intelligence-led policing; community policing; and public–private partnership. Alongside these sit gender and crime; crime and urbanization; organized and white-collar crime; human-rights protection; and money-laundering and cybercrime with the roles of NAB, FIA and ANF. The lecture works through these in turn, and so do these notes.

2 The security-threat chain

🧠 Radicalization → Violent Extremism → Terrorism

05:00 – 51:00

This is the spine of the whole topic, and, as Section 08 shows, the part the examiner asks about every year. The lecturer builds it as a chain of three stages, then explains the journey with four theories. Keep the three stages distinct; that boundary-drawing is exactly what is tested.

Definition · Radicalization

A process by which an individual or group adopts extreme beliefs that reject mainstream values. Radicalism is not simply holding a view different from the average. The danger is that radicals seek to justify their extreme beliefs, and some, to prove "what we think is the right belief," will resort to violence. That readiness to carry a belief to a violent conclusion is what makes it a subject for criminologists.

Two features matter. First, radicalization is a process, not an event. Second, it is deeply society- and culture-specific, "not one model fits all." Pakistan's radicalism is largely religious; American and European societies show different forms, because every culture has a different set of beliefs and standards. So radicalization must be studied through region-specific case studies: America, Libya and Pakistan each need their own evidence.

Definition · Violent Extremism

A mindset that accepts violence as a legitimate or necessary means, "the only way through which I can propagate or prove my beliefs." It sits between radicalization and terrorism: radicalization supplies the extreme belief; violent extremism is the willingness to use violence for it.

Definition · Terrorism

The practical manifestation, the act itself. From the word terror: the deliberate use of violence against civilians or non-combatants to achieve a political, religious or ideological objective, spreading fear in the process. It has many definitions, but the fear-plus-objective core is constant. Extremism is mostly a mindset; terrorism is that mindset's practical manifestation, a thin line between them.

⚠️ Watch out · Terrorism is not mob lynching

A likely trap. In a mob lynching you punish one specific person. You are already "two steps ahead," aiming to harm that individual, and you are not spreading fear to promote a definable extremist ideology. Terrorism deliberately spreads terror to promote and entrench an extremist ideology. Test it: which extremist ideology is the mob promoting? If you cannot name one, it is not terrorism.

How the concept emerged 21:00

Terrorism is not an old concept. Before the 1980s, the assumption was that a state's enemy is another state, states fight states, or at most a civilian group in a civil war. After the 1990s and 9/11, the world recognised a distinct new challenge and began to trace out where radicalism spreads from. The lecturer notes, explicitly without taking sides, how the West framed Islam within this: ask someone in the West to free-associate on "terrorism" and, in the lecturer's rhetorical illustration (his words, not survey data), roughly 90% answer "Muslims / Islam." He stressed he was not taking sides, only giving an example; the analytical point is that a criminologist must read each society's radicalization on its own terms.

🔎 Worked example · ISKP

Islamic State of Khorasan Province (ISKP) shows an extreme ideology in operation: it holds that only Sharia is the righteous law, that existing (Western) laws are illegitimate because "created by infidels," and pursues a jihadist objective of imposing its system across the Middle East, Afghanistan and Pakistan, rejecting democracy, secular governance and nation-state borders. Ground any radical movement you discuss in its own stated ideology.

🧩 Why an ordinary person turns, the four theories 30:00

The blue box on the slide asks: "Why does an ordinary individual become radicalized, embrace violent extremism, and eventually commit an act of terrorism?" The lecturer answers with four frameworks (studied earlier), used together, not as rivals. A handy way to remember them (an editorial memory aid, not the lecturer's own ordering) is environment → association → vulnerability → trigger.

1 · Social Learning Theory (Albert Bandura). People learn by observing and imitating others, and behaviour that appears rewarded is reinforced. Radicalization comes through repeated exposure, family and home, educational institutions, religious institutions, religious/political leaders, and social-media glorification of extremist content. "Brainwashing" is not a single day; it is cumulative social learning absorbed from childhood.

2 · Differential Association Theory (Edwin Sutherland). Crime is learned through close interactions; a person turns criminal when exposed to an excess of definitions favourable to crime. Close association with a group tied to an extremist outfit (joining its channels, websites, discussion groups, then physical meet-ups) teaches you its favourable definitions of violence. The classic case: a university student who joins an extremist discussion group and is gradually drawn in.

3 · Social Control Theory. When social bonds are weak, criminal behaviour rises. A socially isolated young person (poor family ties, no employment, no standing in institutions) has little holding them back, and extremist organizations deliberately pick such people, offering what was missing: "Welcome to the brotherhood. You are not alone. You are made for a higher purpose." They manufacture identity, belonging and purpose. Note the exam-worthy twist: many recruits are highly educated (Bachelors, Masters); the belonging mechanism, not ignorance, explains it.

4 · Strain Theory (Robert Merton). When society imposes strain (you cannot reach accepted goals through legitimate means) the frustration and marginalization can push people toward radical ideologies. Someone who feels suppressed, and believes peaceful political participation changes nothing, may conclude that only an armed struggle can change their status. Strain converts social/political frustration into violent extremism.

🎯 Likely exam question

"Why does an ordinary individual become radicalized and eventually commit an act of terrorism? Explain using criminological theories." Give the chain, then apply the four theories (environment → association → vulnerability → trigger), each with a Pakistani illustration, and close on the criminologist's goal: prevention.

3 Crime in a suit

💼 White-collar crime

51:00 – 1:07:00

The slide's question: why did criminology expand from street crime to crimes committed by professionals, corporations and governments?

The answer is historical. As societies advanced through industrialization, urbanization and specialization, the most damaging crimes stopped happening only on the street and started happening inside offices. Classical criminology focused on conventional crimes (murder, theft, robbery, assault) but criminologists realised the horizon had to expand: even people who achieve society's goals through legitimate positions commit crime.

Definition · White-collar crime

Crime committed by people of the middle-and-above socioeconomic classes in the course of their occupation, exploiting their professional position. Introduced by Edwin Sutherland to challenge the traditional belief that criminals are necessarily poor. The offender here is often "sitting in the higher office, in the AC." Its defining trait: it is non-violent, and committed by exploiting one's professional position, not separately from the job.

The slide's provocation makes the stakes vivid: which is more damaging, a Rs.50,000 theft, or a Rs.5 million embezzlement by a bank manager? White-collar crime, though non-violent, is frequently far more damaging than street crime, precisely because it is done quietly from a position of trust, which is why its study became "a very important thing."

📋 Characteristics

Committed by someone in a professional position; exploits that standing; requires an active employment status (you must hold the position to abuse it); and is non-violent. Tie every type below back to "abuse of a professional position."

🗂️ Types

Corporate fraud (manipulating financial statements to defraud), embezzlement (misappropriating entrusted funds), tax evasion, insider trading (confidentially trading shares on non-public company information), bank frauds, fake accounts, and financial-record manipulation.

⚠️ Watch out · Tax avoidance vs tax evasion

Not the same, and confusing them is a marked error. Tax avoidance is legal, using lawful provisions to reduce tax. Tax evasion is illegal. Only evasion is a white-collar crime.

🎯 Likely exam question

"Define white-collar crime and discuss why it can be more damaging than conventional street crime, with examples." Anchor on Sutherland, the non-violent + professional-position characteristics, and the Rs.50,000-vs-Rs.5-million contrast. (Illustrative film: The Accountant.)

4 Crime as a system

🕸️ Organized crime

1:09:00 – 1:46:00

Where white-collar crime is about an individual professional, organized crime is about a structure.

Sometimes it is not individuals acting alone but well-structured criminal organizations, syndicates. When people work through a proper syndicate, you must "think about an entire system," not a person. A widely accepted definition comes from the United Nations, and organized crime is studied in the modern context precisely because it keeps evolving.

Characteristics of organized crime

1 · Structured & hierarchical, a boss, a defined hierarchy, and specified job descriptions. 2 · Continuous, not a one-time offense; it runs generation to generation. 3 · Profit-oriented, material and financial gain is the main motive. 4 · Violence & intimidation, threats, extortion, kidnapping, even assassinations, to run operations and eliminate competition. 5 · Wide-scale operations with genuine planning and coordination.

Famous syndicates: the Italian Sicilian Mafia (the world of Al Pacino's films), the Russian Bratva, the Japanese Yakuza (so embedded it ran its own casinos and restaurants), and the Cali Cartels (famous for drug trafficking). Forms today: drug trafficking, human trafficking, migrant smuggling and arms trafficking.

🇵🇰 Pakistan context

Pakistan has proper networks for irregular migration, the "donkey" route behind the boat-capsizing tragedies of Pakistanis reaching Europe. These are full networks with distinct specialists: front men, finance managers, on-ground facilitators, drivers and boat operators are all different people. There are generational organized-crime circles on the Balochistan/KP borders running drug and arms trafficking, with money laundering as the end use of the proceeds.

⚠️ Watch out · Two accuracy traps

Gang ≠ organized crime. A gang can be four people; it lacks hierarchy, job descriptions, wide-scale operations and continuity. A gang may be a subset of organized crime but is not itself organized crime (e.g. club/biker gangs bound by affinity, doing petty crimes like bar fights). Map the five characteristics; only if all qualify is it organized crime.
Transgender is a social construct, not a crime. The claim that "transgender strength has increased to commit crime" is factually incorrect. Avoid such statements; one wrong assertion damages your whole answer.

Why criminologists study it: organized crime taps a broad spectrum (from white-collar to petty to the most heinous crime) under one umbrella, and the standing goal is prevention: understanding where syndicates form, how hierarchies build, and how weak social bonds and strain (poverty, the pull of gain) feed recruitment.

🎯 Likely exam question

"What is organized crime? Discuss its characteristics and distinguish it from a gang, with examples." Lead with the five characteristics, name the syndicates, use the irregular-migration network as the Pakistani case, and close with the gang-vs-syndicate distinction.

5 Cleaning dirty money

💸 Money laundering

1:46:00 – 2:15:00

Money laundering follows straight from the last two topics: white-collar and organized crime both generate enormous illegal profits, and the criminal's problem is spending them.

Dirty money cannot be used openly: the moment a public official who took millions in bribes, or a drug syndicate that earned billions, deposits or spends that cash, financial institutions and law enforcement notice ("their ears stand up"). Earning illegal money is hard, but spending it without getting caught is even harder. That is the problem money laundering solves for the criminal, and why a criminologist studies it.

Definition · Money laundering

Washing away the criminal origin of money, turning "black" (illegally earned) money into "white" (legitimate-looking) money through a defined process, so it appears to come from a legitimate source. The metaphor is literal: as you launder clothes, here you launder the origin of the money.

The three stages

  1. Placement. Introduce the black money into the financial system. The classic technique is smurfing (structuring), depositing small amounts across many accounts, dividing the money below reporting thresholds so no single transaction raises suspicion. The money is "still dirty," but now inside the system.
  2. Layering. Create a long trail of transactions (A → B → C → … → K), so many that the original source A can never be traced. Cryptocurrency and the dark web are heavily used. In the lecturer's image, you "cannot get to the core of that onion," the true origin. (Accuracy note for the paper: public blockchains are actually a permanent, traceable ledger; what really defeats tracing is pseudonymous wallets, mixers/tumblers and privacy coins, not an absence of a ledger. Phrase it that way rather than "crypto can't be traced.")
  3. Integration. Reintroduce the now-clean money as legitimate, buy property or assets with proper receipts. The asset is legal, so the money is finally usable. Placement → layering → integration is the full cycle.
🇵🇰 Pakistan · Legal framework & institutions

Pakistan has taken this seriously under both a national and an international (FATF) push. Anti-Money Laundering Act, 2010, defines money laundering as an offense and prescribes penalties. National AML & CFT Authority Act, 2023, establishes the National Anti-Money-Laundering & Counter-Financing-of-Terrorism Authority, created under Pakistan's FATF grey-list action plans. The Financial Monitoring Unit (FMU), Pakistan's autonomous Financial Intelligence Unit, established under the AML Act 2010 and operating under the Ministry of Finance (it was a State Bank department before 2010), collects and analyses Suspicious Transaction Reports (STRs), flagging, say, an account showing tens of millions in transactions its profile can't explain.

🎯 Likely exam question

"Explain the stages of money laundering and Pakistan's legal framework to counter it." Give placement → layering → integration (with smurfing and crypto), then name the AML Act 2010, the AML & CFT Authority Act 2023, FATF, and the FMU. (Illustrative series: Ozark.)

6 Crime in cyberspace

💻 Cybercrime & cyber-warfare

2:15:00 – 2:27:00

The newest crime frontier: crime committed using a digital device within a digital network. Cyberspace is vast, and crimes proliferate inside it.

Categorization · two angles

The lecturer's two axes were the role of the device and the action performed. In the standard academic taxonomy this maps to computer as a tool, the device is used to commit the act (viruses, hacking, malware, denial-of-service, DoS/DDoS attacks, unauthorized access, phishing), and computer as a target, where the device or system is itself attacked. Name both the device's role and the action.

Types (a fast-growing list): hacking, phishing, cyberbullying, AI-enabled image-based abuse (AI-made fake faces / deepfakes, a very recent crime), and email spoofing. You need the idea and the major categories; the specific techniques keep multiplying.

⚔️ Cyber-warfare · a 5th-generation-warfare concept

Beyond individual cybercrime lies cyber-warfare, states attacking states through cyberspace, part of fifth-generation warfare. The classic example is the malware attack on Iran's nuclear facilities. Extend cyber-crime answers into cyber-warfare and 5GW, a very relevant, exam-worthy dimension.

✅ Correction · name it precisely

In the lecture the Iran-nuclear cyber-attack was called "Sputnik" (the lecturer began to correct himself mid-sentence, "Sputnik… stuck next"). The correct name is Stuxnet, the malware (widely attributed to a US–Israeli operation) that sabotaged centrifuges at Iran's Natanz enrichment facility around 2010. Sputnik was the 1957 Soviet satellite. Use Stuxnet in the exam.

🇵🇰 Pakistan · Legal framework & institutions

Prevention of Electronic Crimes Act (PECA), 2016, Pakistan's core cyber statute, covering electronic fraud, forgery, identity theft and cyber terrorism. The National Cyber Crime Investigation Agency (NCCIA), formerly the FIA's cyber-crime wing, since made a standalone agency. The State Bank of Pakistan also monitors cyber-enabled financial crime. Pakistan is cognizant of cybercrime and has both legal and institutional frameworks.

🎯 Likely exam question

"Discuss cybercrime as an emerging threat, its major types, and Pakistan's response." Use the tool-vs-target categorization, list the types (incl. AI image-based abuse), add cyber-warfare/Stuxnet, and name PECA 2016 and NCCIA.

7 Gender & crime

⚖️ Gender & crime: urban vs rural Pakistan

2:27:00 – 2:45:00

The final topic, flagged as highly likely on the paper. The core concept is gender-based violence (GBV), read differently across urban and rural settings.

Definition · Gender-based violence

Any act of violence, physical, sexual or psychological, directed at a particular gender that harms the victim's public or private life. Historically focused on women; the modern spectrum is broader. Three components: (1) it targets a specific gender; (2) through physical/sexual/psychological violence; (3) harming private or public life. Learn it by its components.

Urban characteristicsRural characteristics
Workplace harassmentPatriarchal social structures
Cyber harassment & online blackmailingDomestic violence (limited economic independence)
Human traffickingHonor killings
Domestic violenceDenial of education, employment & mobility
Greater use of digital technology for GBVCase study: Mukhtaran Mai

In urban centres, the digital dimension dominates: workplace and cyber harassment, online blackmailing, human trafficking. In rural areas, GBV grows out of patriarchal structures: domestic violence (linked to limited economic independence), honor killings, and the denial of education, employment and mobility, itself a form of violence, since it removes the right to earn and to freedom. The rural case study is Mukhtaran Mai.

📊 Data & reports to cite

National Commission on the Status of Women, 40% of Pakistani women report being cyber-bullied or harassed online; thousands of cybercrime complaints (mostly by women) are filed with the FIA yearly, sometimes severe enough that women delete or limit their accounts. Law & Justice Commission of Pakistan Report 2023, category-wise GBV data (the slide table lists: sexual violence, abduction/kidnapping/selling of persons, gender-based killing, gender-based hurt, offences relating to marriage, economic violence, gender-based intimidation, and gender-based electronic crimes, most under the PPC, the electronic-crimes row under PECA). Named commissions and years are what earn marks.

🇵🇰 Pakistan · Laws

Article 34 of the Constitution, full participation of women in national life. Protection against Harassment of Women at the Workplace Act, 2010, the major statute against workplace and economic violence. (Use the full title, including "Protection against", exact naming earns the mark.)

🎯 Exam approach

Define GBV by its three components → split urban vs rural → cite the National Commission on the Status of Women and the Law & Justice Commission 2023 → name Article 34 and the Protection against Harassment of Women at the Workplace Act 2010 → close with the Mukhtaran Mai case study.

8 Straight from the paper

📝 Past-paper questions (Section IV)

exam · 2023–2025

The lecturer closed on this slide, and it is the single most useful thing for revision: radicalization and terrorism have appeared in Section IV every year, 2023–2025. If you master Section 2 of these notes, you can answer all three. (The questions below are transcribed from the slide; the "how to answer" notes are our suggested approach, not the lecturer's words.)

🗓️ 2023 (Section IV)

"Terrorism and radicalization continue to pose threat to global peace, economy, and stability. Discuss?"
How to answer: define the radicalization → violent extremism → terrorism chain, then discuss the threat across three axes: peace (violence, instability), economy (deterred investment, security costs), and stability (weakened institutions), with global and Pakistani examples.

🗓️ 2024 (Section IV)

"Define radicalization. What factors contribute to individuals becoming radicalized, and how can societies address these influences?"
How to answer: give the definition, then use the four theories as your "factors" (social learning, differential association, social control, strain); for "how societies address them," bring in community-based policing, public–private partnership, specialized authorities, and counter-radicalization / de-radicalization.

🗓️ 2025 (Section IV)

"Is radicalism present in Pakistan society? How does it escalate into terrorism?"
How to answer: yes, and stress that it is society-specific (largely religious in Pakistan). Trace the escalation along the chain, apply strain and social-control theory to Pakistani conditions, and use a case (e.g. ISKP), closing on prevention.

🎯 The pattern

Three years, one theme: radicalization → terrorism. Prepare Section 2 thoroughly (definitions, the chain, the four theories, and Pakistani examples) and you are covered for the most predictable question on the paper.

Self-test

🎴 Flashcards

Distinguish radicalization, violent extremism and terrorism.

Radicalization = the process of adopting extreme beliefs; violent extremism = the mindset accepting violence; terrorism = the violent act. A chain, each leading to the next.

Why is terrorism not the same as a mob lynching?

Terrorism spreads fear to promote an extremist ideology; a lynching targets one person to harm them and promotes no definable extremist ideology.

Which four theories explain the path to terrorism?

Social Learning (Bandura), Differential Association (Sutherland), Social Control, and Strain (Merton), remember as environment → association → vulnerability → trigger.

Social Control Theory, who is recruited, and how?

Socially isolated people with weak bonds; extremist groups offer brotherhood, identity and purpose. Many recruits are surprisingly well-educated.

Who introduced white-collar crime, and what belief did it challenge?

Edwin Sutherland, challenging the idea that criminals are only the poor. It is non-violent crime committed from a professional position.

Tax avoidance vs tax evasion?

Avoidance is legal (lawful reduction of tax); evasion is illegal and is the white-collar crime.

Five characteristics of organized crime?

Structured/hierarchical (with job descriptions); continuous (generation to generation); profit-oriented; uses violence/intimidation; wide-scale planned operations.

Is a gang the same as organized crime?

No. A gang lacks hierarchy, job descriptions, wide-scale operations and continuity; it can be a subset of organized crime but is not itself organized crime.

Name the three stages of money laundering.

Placement (get dirty money in, e.g. smurfing) → Layering (obscure the trail, incl. crypto/dark web) → Integration (spend it as legitimate assets).

Pakistan's money-laundering laws & bodies?

AML Act 2010; National AML & CFT Authority Act 2023 (FATF-driven); Financial Monitoring Unit (autonomous FIU, Ministry of Finance) collecting STRs.

Two categorizations of cybercrime?

Computer as a tool (hacking, malware, DDoS, phishing) and computer as a target. Name both the device role and the action.

Cyber-warfare example, and the name to get right?

Stuxnet, the worm that sabotaged Iran's Natanz centrifuges (~2010). Not "Sputnik." A 5th-generation-warfare case.

Pakistan's cybercrime law & agency?

PECA 2016 (electronic fraud, forgery, identity theft, cyber terrorism); NCCIA, formerly the FIA cyber-crime wing, now standalone.

GBV, two reports and two laws to cite?

Reports: National Commission on the Status of Women (40% cyber-harassed) & Law & Justice Commission 2023. Laws: Article 34 & Protection against Harassment of Women at the Workplace Act 2010.

Before the paper

🎯 Exam focus

  • The safest bet: radicalization → terrorism (Section 2) has been asked in Section IV in 2023, 2024 and 2025. Prepare it cold.
  • Definitions must be exact. Separate radicalization / extremism / terrorism, tax avoidance / evasion, and gang / organized crime. Learn multi-definition terms (terrorism, GBV) by their components.
  • Name Pakistani laws with year and enforcing body, AML Act 2010, AML & CFT Authority Act 2023, FMU; PECA 2016, NCCIA; Protection against Harassment of Women at the Workplace Act 2010, Article 34.
  • Always add the criminologist's lens. Theories explain why; the standing goal is prevention, say so.
  • Read every modern crime as context-specific. "Not one model fits all", use region-specific case studies.
  • Never write a factually wrong claim (e.g. "transgender people commit organized crime," or calling Stuxnet "Sputnik"). It undermines the whole answer.
  • Ground each topic in a case, ISKP, the boat-capsizing migration networks, Mukhtaran Mai, Stuxnet.
