Governance: What It Is & the Theories Behind It

The meaning of governance, and the lenses through which the subject reads it: the decentred and interpretive view, the Marxist critique, the neoliberal case for a small state, and regulation theory.

Governance is not government. It is how a whole society, its many actors and networks, is actually steered. Each theory is a different answer to the question: steered by whom, and for whom? 🏛️

⭐ The five-minute version

  • Governance is not government. Government is the institution; governance is the process of steering a society, the making and implementing of public policy across many actors, of which the state is only one. The UNDP frames it as the exercise of authority to manage a country's affairs at every level.
  • The decentred (interpretive) theory (Bevir and Rhodes) says governance has no single control room. It happens through networks and many centres (polycentric governance), and it turns on situated agency: people act from their own beliefs, traditions and historical and cultural context, so no one policy fits everyone.
  • The Marxist approach reads governance as a contest between the bourgeoisie (owners of capital and land) and the proletariat (labour). Left unchecked, policy is captured by the rich (policy capture, elite capture), and the resulting inequality drives revolution or separatism. The remedy is to build balance and include the masses in policy.
  • Libertarianism and neoliberalism argue the opposite: keep the state out of the economy. Minimum intervention, a free market, a strong private sector, ease of doing business, and democracy rather than dictatorship.
  • Regulation theory looks at the whole system: if one part of an institution is flawed (wrong people, wrong policy, wrong legal framework), the whole institution malfunctions and destabilises society. The job is to identify the faulty parts and correct them before instability spreads.
  • How to use this in the exam. Each theory is a lens. A good answer names the theory, states its core claim, and applies it to a real Pakistani example (a fuel-price policy read through Marxism, provincial autonomy read through polycentric governance, privatisation read through neoliberalism).
  • The recurring instruction from the teacher: no subject stands alone. The Marxism you met in Political Science or Sociology is the same idea; what changes is how you apply it to governance and policy.
1 The concept

🧭 What governance means

Class 2

Start by separating two words students constantly merge. Government is the institution, the set of offices and people. Governance is the activity: the actual steering of a society, the making and carrying out of public policy.

The working definition to remember, from the deck: governance is the implementation of public policy, the management of the socio-economic development process, by attending to the meanings and beliefs that inform the actions of different actors Class 2 · 11:05. The slide adds that governance is a multi-dimensional concept with no single universally agreed definition, which is why the theories below each define it differently.

The UNDP definition. The standard formulation the paper expects, from the United Nations Development Programme, is that governance is the exercise of economic, political and administrative authority to manage a country's affairs at all levels. It works through the rules, institutions and processes by which citizens and groups voice their interests, exercise their rights and mediate their differences. The lecture slide's own shorthand, which cites UNDP for "rules of the political system to resolve conflicts between actors through legality and legitimacy", is a compressed version of that idea; use the fuller UNDP wording above in an answer.

Who the "actors" are

Governance is done by many hands, and naming them is the first move in almost any answer. The actors, or stakeholders, are: the public (the largest actor, the one governance affects), the government, the civil servants who are the tool of governance, and then politicians, the business community, the education and healthcare communities, the media, NGOs, and others Class 2 · 12:12. Each carries its own beliefs, demands and mindset, and good governance means understanding those before you implement anything.

Why the actor list matters. The whole subject rests on the idea that governance is a negotiation among actors with different interests. If a question asks about any policy problem, listing the relevant actors and what each wants is a reliable, high-value opening. It also sets up every theory below, because each theory is really a claim about which actors hold the power and whose interests the policy ends up serving.

2 Theory one

🕸️ The decentred, interpretive theory

Class 2

The decentred theory of governance, associated with Mark Bevir and R.A.W. Rhodes, is the interpretive lens. Its claim: governance has no single centre and cannot be understood from the top down. It emerges from the beliefs and actions of many actors, read in their own context.

Situated agency

The foundation is situated agency. "Agency" means autonomy, the capacity to decide and act. "Situated" means that capacity is exercised inside a context Class 2 · 06:18. Institutions and social norms shape people but do not fully determine them; their personal choices and character make a difference, always against the background of their history, culture and relationships Class 2 · 37:18.

A nuance worth getting right. The lecture's slide says "individuals' actions are not impacted by the institutions and social norms", which, taken literally, overstates the theory. Bevir and Rhodes do not claim people are unaffected by their surroundings. Their actual point is subtler and stronger for an answer: individuals inherit traditions and norms, but they can reason about them and modify them, so their agency is real but situated within, not free of, that inherited background. The teacher's own examples make this clearer than the slide: a person raised amid corrupt practices who still chooses integrity, or a family whose belief that "girls do not work" is revised when economic hardship forces a rethink Class 2 · 08:46. The tradition is there; the agent works on it.

Networks and polycentric governance

If agency is situated, then power is spread out. The theory therefore emphasises network governance and polycentric governance: policy is not made only in Islamabad (or Washington, or Delhi), but across many centres, communities, provinces, the media, civil service, business, the army, NGOs Class 2 · 26:39. The idea is to empower communities to manage their own problems, and to keep the many centres of decision-making in view when a policy is made Class 2 · 27:17. These networks interact through negotiation, cooperation and conflict, and the theory's prescription is that power should be distributed across them rather than concentrated at the centre Class 2 · 35:49.

History, culture, and evolution

Because agency is situated, the theory insists that governance cannot be understood without the historical and cultural context of the actors involved Class 2 · 30:57. A person shaped by famine is sensitive to food insecurity; a naturalised community brings the culture of its origin into its policy preferences. And governance evolves over time: you cannot apply the policies of an earlier age unchanged, because needs, values and resources have moved on, though past practices, institutional legacies and cultural norms still shape what is possible Class 2 · 32:42.

The single sentence that captures it. "No one policy can satisfy an entire population" Class 2 · 26:39. That is the decentred theory in one line, and it justifies devolution, local government, community empowerment and context-sensitive policy. Use it whenever a question assumes a single national policy can fix a diverse country.

3 Theory two

⚒️ The Marxist approach to governance

Class 2

The Marxist lens reads governance as a struggle between classes, and reads most policy as serving the powerful. The teacher stresses that this is the same Marxism from Political Science and Sociology, applied here to policy Class 2 · 50:44.

ClassWho they are
Bourgeoisie (capitalist class)Owners of capital, investment and large land, who employ and, in the Marxist account, exploit labour Class 2 · 45:15
Proletariat (working class)The labour that produces the value but does not own the means of production

Applied to governance, the argument runs: when the capitalist class controls policy-making, policies come out pro-rich rather than pro-public Class 2 · 46:34. The masses, who are not rich, feel the difference in the price of fuel, the weight of a roti, the cost of vegetables and kitchen essentials, while the benefits accrue upward Class 2 · 47:07. Excluded from planning, the public eventually revolts.

Where the theory points: revolution and separatism. The lecture's throughline is that unaddressed inequality and class conflict do not stay quiet. They produce revolution, the Russian and French Revolutions being the stock examples, where people toppled and replaced governments Class 2 · 48:22; or they produce separatism, when a group whose needs are ignored seeks to break away. The teacher uses Balochistan as the live Pakistani illustration of grievance feeding separatist sentiment Class 2 · 49:23. The Marxist prescription is to build balance and equality into the economy and the policy process so the pressure never reaches that point. Contemporary examples of the balance-seeking mindset offered: the USSR of the past, and China and Cuba.

Two jargon terms to bank: policy capture and elite capture. The teacher flags both as the terms to attach to the Marxist reading Class 2 · 51:08. Policy capture is when policy is shaped to serve a narrow interest rather than the public. Elite capture is when a privileged few divert public resources and benefits to themselves. Naming these in an answer signals you can move from the theory to its concrete mechanism, which is what turns a definition into analysis.

4 Theory three

🏪 Libertarianism and neoliberalism

Class 2

Where Marxism wants the state to correct the market, libertarianism and neoliberalism want the state to step back from it. The teacher treats the two together, since in a governance context their message is much the same, while noting they are formally distinct theories Class 2 · 52:10.

Core messageWhat it means
Minimum government interventionThe state stays out of economic affairs as far as possible Class 2 · 52:10
Adoption of democracyGovernance improves under democracy, not dictatorship or theocracy Class 2 · 01:02:02
Empowerment of the private sectorBusiness, not the state, drives the economy
Ease of doing businessThe state's role narrows to improving conditions for enterprise Class 2 · 54:47
Adoption of capitalism / free marketPrices set by market principles, not by the state

The distinction, for a careful answer. The teacher's own gloss: the framing leans toward a capitalist, free-market school of thought, with neoliberalism emphasising the free-market economy, hyper-individualism, capitalism and limited government intervention, while libertarianism shades toward the more strongly liberal, minimal-state position Class 2 · 01:02:02. For most governance answers, treating them as one pro-market, small-state family is fine; if a question demands the difference, libertarianism is the more absolute anti-state stance, neoliberalism the market-first economic-policy programme.

Editorial: connect it to real policy. This is the lens behind privatisation, deregulation, IMF-style structural adjustment, and the "ease of doing business" reforms Pakistan pursues. When a question asks about privatising state enterprises or cutting subsidies, the neoliberal case (efficiency, private-sector dynamism, a leaner state) is one half of the answer, and the Marxist objection (policy capture, the burden falling on the masses) is the other. Setting the two lenses against each other is a strong essay structure.

5 Theory four

🔧 Regulation theory

Class 2

The last theory of the chapter is regulation theory, and the teacher's version is a systems view: a governance system is only as sound as its weakest part, so the task is to find the faulty parts and correct them.

The core claim as taught: if any one part of an institution is flawed, the whole institution is flawed Class 2 · 01:03:43. Recruit the wrong people and the institution's whole working fails; write the wrong policy and even good implementers cannot save it; leave a legal framework broken and the institution starts to destabilise the society around it Class 2 · 01:06:16. The consequence the teacher stresses is a loss of citizenship in the sense of belonging: when institutions malfunction, people stop feeling the country is theirs and look to leave Class 2 · 01:05:15.

What the theory asks you to do. Use the system to identify problematic features and correct them before instability compounds, because in a capitalist society, the teacher warns, imbalance spreads fast Class 2 · 01:06:47. The specific target is any system that promotes socio-economic inequality and class conflict: left uncorrected, these breed the revolution or separatism the Marxist section described Class 2 · 01:07:14. The teacher's homely image: regulation is like a remote-controlled air conditioner, a way to keep checking and adjusting the system to the setting you want Class 2 · 01:08:20.

Editorial: know the academic term, and note where the lecture drifts. In the scholarly literature, Regulation Theory (the French régulation school of Michel Aglietta, Robert Boyer and Alain Lipietz) is a Marx-influenced political economy, and its central question is the opposite of what the slide implies: it studies how a set of institutions and norms lets capitalism achieve temporary stability despite its built-in contradictions, and why those arrangements eventually break down. So the theory is about the institutions that stabilise capitalism, not ones that "cause instability". Two more precision points: its acknowledged roots are structural Marxism, institutionalism and the Annales school (and Polanyi), not systems theory, so treat the slide's "roots in Marxism and systems theory" as a loose gloss rather than a citable fact. And the lecture's "a flaw in one part fails the whole institution" is really a general systems-thinking account of institutional failure. For a CSS answer, present the identify-and-correct systemic idea the way the teacher does, but if you name Regulation Theory as a school, attribute it to Aglietta and the régulation tradition and describe it as explaining capitalism's periods of stability.

The chapter's takeaway. Four lenses, four different answers to "who steers, and for whom". The decentred theory says many centres steer, so devolve power. Marxism says the rich steer, so rebalance. Neoliberalism says the market should steer, so shrink the state. Regulation theory says the system steers, so fix its faulty parts. A strong answer rarely picks just one; it reads the same policy problem through two or three and shows what each reveals.

As class conflict between bourgeoisie and proletariat; unchecked, policy is captured by the rich, and the resulting inequality drives revolution or separatism.

Policy capture vs elite capture?

Policy capture: policy serves a narrow interest not the public. Elite capture: a privileged few divert public resources and benefits to themselves.

What do neoliberalism and libertarianism argue?

Keep the state out of the economy: minimum intervention, free market, private-sector empowerment, ease of doing business, democracy over dictatorship.

State regulation theory as taught, and how the academic school differs.

As taught: a flaw in one part of an institution fails the whole, so systemic faults must be found and corrected. The academic régulation school (Aglietta, Boyer, Lipietz; Marxist roots) actually studies how institutions let capitalism achieve temporary stability, not what causes instability.

Each is a lens on "who steers and for whom". Read one policy problem through two or three (Marxist vs neoliberal, plus polycentric) to build an analytical answer.

How to write a theory answer

  • Name the theory and its theorist where there is one (decentred → Bevir and Rhodes).
  • State the core claim in one sentence before elaborating.
  • Apply it to a real example. A fuel-price or subsidy decision read through Marxism; provincial autonomy or local government read through polycentric governance; privatisation read through neoliberalism; a failing institution read through regulation theory.
  • Set two lenses against each other for essay-style questions. The neoliberal case for privatisation versus the Marxist warning of elite capture is a ready-made structure.
  • Bank the jargon: situated agency, polycentric/network governance, policy capture, elite capture, ease of doing business. Correct terms signal command of the subject.

The teacher's standing advice. No subject is learned in isolation. The Marxism, neoliberalism and interpretive theory here are the same ideas you met in Political Science, Sociology or International Relations; what earns marks in this paper is applying them to governance and public policy specifically, with a Pakistani example attached. Build your notes so each theory carries at least one such example you can deploy on demand.
