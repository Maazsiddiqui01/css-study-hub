⭐ The five-minute version

  • Interpret before researching. Define each keyword, divide the prompt into components, question each part, and then read the parts together.
  • Move from words to abstraction. The essay needs a broad principle, debate or concept that explains what the complete prompt is really asking.
  • Do not force one formula onto every prompt. Some topics yield to questions, some to a contrast, and some to a debate such as reality versus an ideal.
  • Brainstorm twice. First collect ideas freely. Then organise them through parts, categories, opposing views and a collective reading.
  • Turn the governing idea into thesis questions. Each main heading should open a substantial inquiry and remain visibly connected to the prompt.
  • Develop fewer claims more deeply. A heading can contain several paragraphs, with reasons, examples, counterviews and a concluding principle.
  • Build the introduction towards the thesis. The class recommends a relevant anecdote, question, current event or verified fact, followed by a final thesis that states topic, stance and reasons.
  • Keep every worked example provisional. The classroom outlines demonstrate interpretation and structure. Their substantive claims still need evidence before use in an exam.
  • Audit unity at the end. The abstraction, headings, paragraphs, thesis and conclusion should form one connected discourse rather than separate lists.

Three miniature readings

“Wisdom finds truth”

Separate wisdom from truth. Ask how each is acquired, whether wisdom differs from knowledge, how truth is found, and whether the relationship can be challenged Part 1 · 00:08:50 Part 1 · 00:10:44.

“Society gets the values it rewards”

The individual words are simple, so the useful move is collective reading: what system of rewards produces a society's working value system Part 1 · 00:13:22 Part 1 · 00:14:45?

“Values are not what humanity is...”

Split the real condition from what humanity ought to be. The course reading locates a debate about actual conduct, ideals and a standard of goodness Part 1 · 00:15:15 Part 1 · 00:16:11.

Interpretive boundary. The claim that an ideal is always unattainable and becomes “real” once achieved is presented as one classroom framing Part 1 · 00:16:39. It is not a settled definition of ideals. An essay may test that premise, qualify it, or adopt a different defensible account.

The practical lesson is flexibility. The teacher says there is no single “pet formula”: one prompt may invite component questions, another a collective reading, and another a real-versus-ideal debate Part 1 · 00:16:39 Part 1 · 00:17:12.

3 Make the parts mean something together

🧩 Find the governing abstraction

Part 2 · 03:35-57:37

Part 2 turns interpretation into an exam workflow. First, write down whatever the prompt activates. Next, structure that material by analysing the parts. Finally, seek the broader concept that lets those parts participate in one argument Part 2 · 00:03:35 Part 2 · 00:06:10.

Two kinds of brainstorming

Unstructured thinking

Capture ideas, examples, quotations, current events, contrary views and positive or negative implications without deciding the final order Part 2 · 00:04:36 Part 2 · 00:05:51.

Structured thinking

Divide the wording into parts, examine each part, compare their strengths and limits, and then combine them into a collective reading Part 2 · 00:06:10 Part 2 · 00:06:41.

Use categories only as idea prompts

When content stalls, the class suggests category pairs such as short term and long term, individual and societal, or political, technological and social Part 2 · 00:16:48 Part 2 · 00:19:38. These categories generate possibilities; they do not become the essay's final structure unless they serve the governing abstraction.

The abstraction test

  1. Can the idea explain why the prompt's parts belong together?
  2. Can it produce several connected questions rather than two isolated lists?
  3. Can it appear briefly in exposition as context for the body?
  4. Can every main heading be phrased so the reader sees its connection to the original prompt?

The teacher rejects headings that sound interesting in isolation but do not reveal their relation to the given topic Part 2 · 00:43:49 Part 2 · 00:46:47. The abstraction can frame the exposition, while the later headings connect directly to the essay topic rather than mechanically to the first heading Part 2 · 00:51:17 Part 2 · 00:51:43.

One-sentence checkpoint. Write: “This prompt is ultimately an inquiry into...” If the completion merely repeats one keyword, the collective reading is still incomplete.

4 Divide by inquiry

❓ Build thesis-question headings

Part 1 · 18:09-23:22 · Part 2 · 01:23-01:36

The chief organisational problem is finding dimensions. In this course framework, those dimensions are main headings framed as thesis questions: substantial questions the essay raises and answers through several arguments or paragraphs Part 1 · 00:18:09 Part 1 · 00:21:01.

What qualifies as a thesis question?

Weak headingProblemStronger direction
One abstract wordIt names a theme but does not announce an inquiry.Turn the theme into a question tied to the prompt.
A five-line statementIt tries to write the paragraph inside the outline.Compress it into a concise question Part 2 · 01:27:45.
A generic social questionIts relevance to the prompt is invisible.Reuse the prompt's relationship, contrast or destination.
A question with only one point beneath itIt lacks the depth expected of a major dimension.Merge it, deepen it, or find a more productive question.

The lecture expects a thesis question to sustain several arguments or paragraphs Part 1 · 00:21:31. In the second class, the teacher reiterates that all main body headings perform this role; there is no need to mark one separate heading as “the thesis question” Part 2 · 01:24:04.

A coherence chain

Prompt

Supplies the exact words and relationship that must be answered.

Abstraction

Provides the principle that makes a whole essay possible.

Thesis questions

Divide that principle into connected, answerable dimensions.

Course technique, not an FPSC rule. The lecture says that three headings are insufficient and recommends increasing the number for depth and length Part 2 · 01:23:36 Part 2 · 01:36:23. FPSC does not prescribe a fixed heading count. Use enough dimensions to answer the prompt fully, and give each one meaningful development.

Counterviews can be woven into these inquiries without creating the formal refutation section used in the course's argumentative-essay model Part 1 · 00:21:53 Part 1 · 00:22:24.

5 Make each answer earn its conclusion

🔗 Reason through the paragraph

Part 1 · 22:24-37:32

Within each dimension, the paragraph begins with an argument, explains why it should be accepted, uses examples where useful, and closes with a conclusion that advances the essay Part 1 · 00:36:38 Part 1 · 00:37:05.

The paragraph sequence taught in class

  1. Argument: make the claim in the topic sentence.
  2. Reasons: state the connected premises that explain why the claim follows.
  3. Example or explanation: clarify and support the reasoning.
  4. Counterview, where useful: recognise a relevant complication without forcing a formal rebuttal block.
  5. Conclusion: draw out the result without repeating the topic sentence.

From paragraph result to principle

The teacher encourages a philosophical paragraph to generalise its conclusion into a principle or maxim where the material genuinely permits it Part 1 · 00:24:16 Part 1 · 00:26:45. This is not a licence for a grand slogan after every example. The generalisation must be earned by the paragraph's reasoning.

Correction: reasons alone do not make a deduction. The lecture calls a sequence of supporting reasons “deductive reasoning” Part 1 · 00:35:40 Part 1 · 00:36:12. Formally, a deductive inference is valid only when its structure makes the conclusion necessarily follow if the premises are true. A paragraph can be reasoned and persuasive without being deductive. Use “premise” and “deduction” precisely.

Depth over accumulation

The course allows several examples to support one argument and recommends fewer, deeper arguments rather than a long list with thin explanation Part 1 · 00:27:16 Part 1 · 00:28:15. The working standard is quality of support, not a numerical quota.

Paragraph audit. Underline the conclusion and point back to the premises that justify it. If that route cannot be traced, the paragraph has asserted more than it proved.

6 Control the whole paper

🏗️ Shape outline, introduction and conclusion

Because each thesis-question heading may contain several developed paragraphs, the course allows the outline to look shorter than a descriptive or argumentative outline Part 1 · 00:27:16 Part 1 · 00:29:05. Shorter means fewer substantial dimensions, not less intellectual coverage.

The introduction funnel

The lecturer proposes flexible openings: a story, anecdote, rhetorical question, current event, philosophical view, or accurate fact or statistic Part 1 · 00:30:31 Part 1 · 01:35:49. Whatever the device, the introduction must build towards the final thesis rather than wander through attractive but unrelated material Part 1 · 00:31:01.

The thesis triad

Topic

Reflect the prompt clearly, often through its own key wording.

Stance

State the interpretation or answer the essay will defend.

Reasons

Signal the arguments that make the stance defensible Part 1 · 01:14:24 Part 1 · 01:16:51.

Course-safe technique. The class keeps the explicit argumentative thesis in the final sentence and avoids previewing every body point throughout the introduction Part 1 · 00:30:02 Part 1 · 01:36:30. This is a disciplined exam method, not a universal rule for every form of academic introduction.

7 Worked prompt one

👥 “If youth knew, if age could”

Part 2 · 03:35-57:37

The second lecture demonstrates the complete movement from raw associations to a coherent plan. Its value lies in the method, not in treating every brainstormed generalisation about age or youth as established fact.

Step 1: inventory each clause

If youth knew

Ask what youth already possesses, what it may lack, and what positive or negative consequences would follow if those gaps disappeared Part 2 · 00:07:16 Part 2 · 00:14:12.

If age could

Ask what age is assumed to know, what capacities it may lack, and how renewed ability might affect individuals and society Part 2 · 00:14:40 Part 2 · 00:16:21.

Step 2: resist the two-list outline

A plan that lists advantages and disadvantages of each clause can be extensive yet still lack a meaningful whole. The teacher asks the class to combine the two sides and locate the principle that makes them mutually relevant Part 2 · 00:22:43 Part 2 · 00:25:02.

Step 3: choose one governing route

The lecture offers generation gap and intergenerational complementarity as one possible decoding. It asks whether youth can deliver without older experience, whether age can sustain society without younger enthusiasm and creativity, and how the two can be brought into productive relation Part 2 · 00:54:25 Part 2 · 00:55:53.

Illustrative thesis-question sequence

  1. What does the conditional contrast reveal about the generation gap?
  2. Can youthful capacity fulfil its promise without inherited experience?
  3. Can accumulated experience remain socially productive without youthful energy?
  4. When do the strengths of one generation reproduce the limits of the other?
  5. How can intergenerational cooperation convert difference into continuity and change?

Editorial reconstruction. The sequence above organises questions raised across the workshop; it is not a verbatim outline supplied by the lecturer. The lecture explicitly allows several valid abstractions and warns that ten writers need not choose the same one Part 2 · 00:56:17.

Transferable lesson. If the draft can be cut into two independent mini-essays, the collective reading has not yet done its work.

8 Worked prompt two

🚶 “Life is a long journey between human being and being human”

Part 2 · 57:37-01:36:51

This exercise begins by distinguishing biological existence from humane or moral becoming, then asks what makes the movement between them a long journey Part 2 · 00:57:37 Part 2 · 00:59:11.

Brainstorming map

Prompt elementQuestions generated in classPossible structural use
Human beingWhat belongs to biological existence?Clarify the starting point.
Being humanWhich values or moral capacities define humane conduct?Clarify the destination.
Long journeyWhy is the transition difficult, incomplete or conscious?Explain obstacles and transformation.
Missing valuesHow do failures of humane conduct damage society?Build the problem dimension Part 2 · 01:00:38 Part 2 · 01:01:36.

Questions that create a journey

  1. Why might being human be treated as a purpose rather than an automatic condition?
  2. Why does biological membership fail to guarantee humane conduct?
  3. Which obstacles widen the distance between existence and moral becoming?
  4. How can a person or society close that distance?
  5. How does life change when humane values guide conduct?

These questions reflect the sample progression discussed near the end of the class: purpose, failure to arrive, process of becoming and the consequences of transformation Part 2 · 01:28:30 Part 2 · 01:30:31. They are linked by movement from a starting condition towards a proposed end.

Lessons from rejected headings

  • A broad question about society can still be irrelevant if its connection to the journey is hidden Part 2 · 00:43:49.
  • A heading that merely describes two stages may fail to raise an inquiry Part 2 · 01:23:05.
  • A phrase such as “our biological nature” breaks the course's preferred third-person stance Part 2 · 01:20:33.
  • A heading should be concise enough to guide the argument rather than contain the argument itself Part 2 · 01:27:45.

Keep claims provisional. The prompt invites moral argument. Examples such as jingoism, sexism, communalism or mob violence appear during brainstorming Part 2 · 01:01:09, but any causal or empirical claim built around them needs evidence in the final essay.

Reusable introduction audit

  1. Does the opening focus on the prompt's actual concept?
  2. Is every anecdote, date, person or statistic accurate and attributable?
  3. Does the middle narrow the discussion rather than open unrelated themes?
  4. Does the final sentence contain the topic, the interpretation or stance, and the reasons?
  5. Does the diction remain appropriately formal and controlled?

Correction: first person is not universally forbidden. The lecturer categorically excludes first person from academic writing Part 1 · 01:16:51. Academic conventions vary by discipline and task. For this course's CSS essay method, third person is the safe default; treat that as register guidance rather than a universal law.

🃏 Flashcards

What is the chief challenge in the course's philosophical essay?

Interpreting the prompt's layers, hidden relationships and governing abstraction.

What is the first interpretation task?

Define every keyword in the context of the complete prompt.

What follows component reading?

A collective reading that asks what the components mean together.

Why ask questions of each component?

To uncover meanings, relationships, implications and possible counterviews.

Does every philosophical prompt use one fixed formula?

No. The method must be adapted to the merits and wording of each prompt.

What is unstructured thinking?

Capturing relevant ideas, examples, quotations, current events and opposing views before organising them.

What is structured thinking?

Sorting the brainstorm through the prompt's parts, categories and combined meaning.

What does a governing abstraction do?

It turns separate ideas into one connected inquiry.

Where can the abstraction appear in the essay?

In exposition, where it establishes context for the later headings.

What is a thesis question in this course?

A main-heading question the essay raises and answers through several arguments or paragraphs.

What makes a thesis question strong?

Visible relevance to the prompt, enough depth for development, and a clear role in the whole discourse.

Must one heading be labelled “the thesis question”?

No. In the course model, all substantive body headings function as thesis questions.

Is a fixed number of headings an FPSC rule?

No. Heading counts in the lecture are course recommendations.

How are counterviews handled?

They can be integrated within the inquiry without a formal argumentative-essay refutation block.

What begins a body paragraph?

A topic-sentence argument.

What must come before the evidence?

The reasons or premises that explain why the argument should be accepted.

When is reasoning deductively valid?

When the conclusion necessarily follows if the premises are true.

Should a paragraph conclusion repeat its topic sentence?

No. It should draw out the paragraph's result and link it to the larger inquiry.

When may a conclusion become a maxim?

When the paragraph's reasoning genuinely supports a wider principle.

What are the three thesis ingredients taught in class?

The topic, the stance or interpretation, and the supporting reasons.

What opening devices does the course allow?

A relevant story, anecdote, rhetorical question, current event, philosophical view, or verified fact or statistic.

What invalidates an attractive opening?

Conceptual drift or an inaccurate example.

What is the key lesson from “If youth knew, if age could”?

Do not stop at two lists; find the principle that explains the relationship between the clauses.

What is the key lesson from “Life is a long journey...”?

Build connected questions around starting condition, destination, obstacles, transformation and consequences.

Is first person universally banned from academic writing?

No. Third person is the safe default for this course's CSS essay method.

What does FPSC officially assess in the essay paper?

Comprehensive and research-based knowledge, articulation, expression and technical treatment of English essay writing.
