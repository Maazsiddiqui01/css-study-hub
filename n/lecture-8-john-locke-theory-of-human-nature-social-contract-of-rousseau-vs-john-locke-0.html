The Social Contract: Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau & Montesquieu

Four philosophers, one question: where does the state's authority actually come from, and what may it do to you once it has it?

⭐ The five-minute version

  • The problem all four are solving. Before these thinkers, a king ruled by divine right: God made him king, so he answered to God and not to you. There was no agreement between ruler and ruled, therefore no duties running downward and no grounds for complaint.
  • The move they all make. Replace God with an agreement. Imagine humans before the state (the state of nature), work out why they would ever consent to be ruled, and the terms of that consent then define what the state may and may not do. In a modern state the written form of that agreement is the constitution.
  • Hobbes. Man is a monster; the state of nature is a war of all against all. So surrender everything to one sovereign, permanently. The contract is unilateral (the sovereign is not a party to it) and irrevocable. The sovereign owes you only security of life and property, and may use any means. Destination: absolutism.
  • Locke. Man is good, sympathetic and cooperative; the state of nature is peaceful equality under natural law. Natural law only lacks three things: a body to define it, one to enforce it, and one to judge under it. So the contract is narrow and bilateral: the government is itself a party to it and is your representative, it must protect life, property and above all liberty, and it may be removed if it fails. Destination: representative democracy.
  • Rousseau. Rejects both. Do not surrender to a sovereign (Hobbes) or to representatives (Locke): surrender to the community itself. The community becomes a public person or body politic, which is the state when passive and the sovereign when active, and its members are citizens when they make law and subjects when they obey it. Law follows the general will, and since the general will is your own will, obeying it is obeying yourself, which is freedom.
  • Montesquieu. Changes the question from who rules to how power is arranged. Liberty is not doing as you please, which is a licence to crime; real liberty is exercised under law. Only a moderate government makes good law, and a government is moderate when power checks power. Hence separation of powers across legislature, executive and judiciary.
  • How to answer it (suggested approach). Almost every question here is a comparison. Run Hobbes and Locke down the same four rails, which is exactly how the lecturer summarises them: human nature, state of nature, why a contract was needed, and the nature of the contract. Add Rousseau on the same rails when a question calls for him. Then finish with the modern regime each one lands you in.
1 The idea itself

📜 What a social contract is

00:02 – 00:06

Start from your own position. You expect the state to do things for you, above all to keep you secure. The state expects things from you: taxes and obedience. That mutual expectation is a contract, even though you never signed it and neither did your forefathers 00:02:33.

That same agreement goes by two names. In a country it is called the constitution. Here, in this subject, it is called the social contract 00:03:03.

What came before. Before Thomas Hobbes there was no contract between the individual and the state. There was a king, and the king was all in all. His claim was the divine right of kings: "ruling is my divine right, God has made me king, not you." The consequence was the important part: "I should not be responsible to you, because you didn't make me. God did, and I will answer Him in the hereafter" 00:03:47. No accountability ran downward to the people.

This is what Hobbes attacks. His message to the king is blunt: Thomas Hobbes will challenge this divine right, and if you want to rule over us, then forget about divine right 00:03:47. Authority has to come from the people's agreement instead.

Why they all invent a "state of nature"

Each of these writers tells you a story about how the state came into being, and each admits the story is an assumption that might be true or false 00:04:35. They reach into an imagined past: first it was like this, then like that 00:05:51. Do not read it as history, because it is not offered as history.

Why the assumption is doing real work. The state of nature is an argument device, not a claim about the past. Your picture of people without a state determines how much power they must hand over to get one. Assume the worst about human nature and you will justify a sovereign with unlimited power. Assume the best and you will only justify a narrow, revocable grant. That single move is why Hobbes ends in absolutism and Locke ends in democracy, and saying so explicitly is what separates a strong answer from a list of names.

2 Man the monster

👹 Thomas Hobbes

00:06 – 00:28

Hobbes was born in 1588 in England, the year of the Spanish Armada, the great war between England and Spain. Spain had been the superpower of the world; England defeated it and rose in its place 00:06:32.

His life then ran straight through England's most turbulent period 00:08:37. In 1642 civil war broke out, a struggle for power between parliament and the king, between the people and the king. A king was beheaded in it. Hobbes lived through the reigns of James I, Charles I and Charles II 00:07:56.

Correction (lecturer's slip). The king beheaded in the English Civil War was Charles I, executed on 30 January 1649. The lecture says Charles II. Charles II was the executed king's son, and he was restored to the throne in 1660, dying in his bed in 1685. Write Charles I in the exam.

Why the biography is worth the marks (editorial reading). The lecturer gives the life and then moves to the theory without joining them, so join them yourself: a man who watched his country tear itself apart in civil war, and watched a king lose his head to it, ranks order above everything. Read that way, every hard edge in Hobbes (total surrender, no right of revolt, any means permitted) is a man building a system in which 1642 cannot happen again. This is interpretation rather than something said in class, so present it as your own analysis.

Human nature

Hobbes opens with human nature, before he ever gets to the contract 00:09:48. His picture is dark. He is deeply pessimistic: for him, man is a monster 00:10:17.

State of nature

Given that nature, life before the state is a war of all against all. There is no concept of just and unjust, right and wrong, good and bad. Might is right 00:29:51. Man in this condition has no concept of society at all 00:31:06.

Beyond the lecture (editorial context). Hobbes' book is Leviathan (1651), and the sovereign is the Leviathan of its title, a biblical sea monster. The lecturer makes the same point through the Clash of the Titans line "release the Kraken" 00:15:57: to control a world of small monsters you need one very large monster. Hobbes' own summary of life in the state of nature, worth quoting, is that it is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short."

The contract, and what makes it Hobbesian

To escape that war, people say to the sovereign: we have surrendered our power, our freedom and our rights to you, and we will not revolt. In return you carry one responsibility, to secure our life and our property. Whatever means you choose is your right 00:11:19.

Two features do all the damage, and both are exam keywords:

  • Unilateral. The sovereign is not a party to the contract 00:36:50. The agreement is among the people, to obey him. He promises nothing, so he can breach nothing.
  • Irrevocable. You cannot revoke it. So long as you live under it, it holds 00:13:41.

The end is security of life and property, and for Hobbes that end justifies the means 00:12:10. On revolution his answer is refusal: do not run to revolution over a little, small failure, because revolution simply returns you to the state of nature, which is the one thing worse than a bad sovereign 00:14:34.

Precision on "irrevocable" (editorial, and it protects marks). Write irrevocable by the subjects' will: they cannot lawfully depose the sovereign or swap the form of government, which is the lecture's point. Do not write that it binds unconditionally. Hobbes himself limits it in Leviathan ch. 21: the obligation lasts "as long, and no longer, than the power lasteth by which he is able to protect them." The right of self-preservation is inalienable, so no one covenants away the right to resist force aimed at his own life. An examiner who knows Hobbes looks for exactly this qualification.

The tension worth noting in an answer. Hobbes attacks the divine right of kings, then hands the king everything the divine right ever gave him 00:15:17. He changes the source of authority from God to consent while leaving its extent unlimited. He is a revolutionary about the foundation and a reactionary about the result.

Where Hobbes lands. Absolute rule and dictatorship 00:45:33.

3 Man the cooperator

🕊️ John Locke

00:28 – 00:46

Locke takes Hobbes' structure and inverts the premise. By nature, man is good. He is simple, sympathetic and cooperative 00:28:51. Locke is optimistic about human nature and paints a bright picture of man 00:29:05.

State of nature

Where Hobbes had war, Locke has peace. There was no war of all against all. There was equality, and there was natural law, and under natural law everybody was equal. Nature provided resources and people distributed them equally among themselves 00:31:53.

Locke also denies Hobbes' claim that man had no concept of society. He argues from a biblical source: the first man is Adam, who knew from the first day what was day and night, good and bad. So man was social from the very first day 00:30:27.

Then why contract at all?

This is the obvious objection, and the lecturer puts it directly: if everything was fine, why did man need a contract? 00:32:36 Locke's answer is that natural law had three drawbacks 00:33:41. The contract exists only to remove those three, and nothing more 00:34:53.

Drawback in the state of natureWhat was missingInstitution created
Natural law was undefinedNo body to define what natural law isLegislature
Natural law was unenforcedNo body to implement or execute it in a true senseExecutive
Natural law was uninterpretedNo body to interpret itJudiciary

Beyond the lecture (editorial context). Locke's own wording in the Second Treatise (1689) is worth having, because examiners like it: the state of nature lacks (i) "an established, settled, known law", (ii) "a known and indifferent judge", and (iii) "power to back and support the sentence". The lecture's legislature / executive / judiciary mapping is the standard teaching gloss on exactly those three.

The contract is two-fold

Locke's contract has two aspects 00:35:26:

  1. First, a society. People gather and agree that they are one people. "We are all Pakistani, and everyone says we are all Pakistani." That agreement itself is the first contract 00:36:00.
  2. Second, a government. That society then creates an authority, and here is the decisive break from Hobbes.

The key difference. Hobbes' contract was unilateral and the sovereign stood outside it. In Locke, your government is a party to the contract. It sits among you. It is your representative 00:37:31. The contract is therefore bilateral: both sides have duties, so either side can breach.

What the government owes you

Authority has a duty to protect your life, your property, and most importantly your liberty 00:38:37. Liberty is the headline right in Locke, and the state's chief job is to secure it, including the liberty to express your thinking 00:39:37.

And if it fails

If the government fails in that security, you may overthrow it. But note the qualification carefully, because it is the whole modern point: peacefully 00:40:59.

A peaceful overthrow has a name. It is an election. If we do not like him, the term ends, we change him with our votes. That, too, is a revolution, because revolution means change, and you do not need anybody's head for it 00:41:28. Where Hobbes forbids revolt entirely, Locke domesticates it into a routine, scheduled event.

Where Locke lands. Representative democracy 00:43:08.

The Glorious Revolution

Locke's own politics track his theory. He contributed to the Glorious Revolution, in which the king of England voluntarily surrendered his power before parliament 00:22:49. It was bloodless 00:23:49, and once the king's power passed to parliament the system had converted from monarchy to democracy.

Dates, and the trap inside them (editorial). The Glorious Revolution is 1688 and Locke's Two Treatises of Government was published in 1689. Do not conclude from that order that Locke wrote the book to justify the revolution, which is the standard discriminator question. Peter Laslett's dating established that the Second Treatise was substantially written around 1679 to 1683, during the Exclusion Crisis. It was composed before the revolution and published after it.

On bloodless: fair for England, where the transfer cost very few lives, and that is the lecturer's point. Add the qualifier if you have room, because James II's overthrow set off real wars in Scotland (Killiecrankie, 1689) and Ireland (the Boyne, 1690). Safest phrasing is "largely bloodless in England."

5 Born free, in chains

🔗 Rousseau & the General Will

00:48 – 01:20

Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712 to 1778) was Swiss by birth, born in Geneva, but he lived his life in Paris, so he is counted a French philosopher. His famous theory is the general will 00:48:18.

Correction (a slip worth knowing). "Swiss by birth" is an anachronism, and a Rousseau examiner will notice. Geneva in 1712 was not in Switzerland: it was an independent Calvinist city state, the Republic of Geneva, and it only joined the Swiss Confederation as a canton in 1815, thirty-seven years after Rousseau died. He signed himself Citoyen de Genève, citizen of Geneva, on his title pages, including The Social Contract. Safest phrasing: Genevan-born, French-language philosopher of the French Enlightenment. The lecturer's substantive point stands: born outside France, made his life and reputation in Paris.

The story: freedom lost to property

Rousseau's man in the caves was better off than today's materialistic man, for one reason: he had absolute freedom. Then he came out of the caves and understood a brand new concept, property. Once property arrived, so did "thine and mine" 00:51:01.

Disputes over property meant people needed an authority to control them. And when they created that authority, it took their freedom 00:52:18. Hence the line the whole theory hangs on:

"Man is born free, but everywhere he is in chains." 00:53:25 The chains are not natural. They are the authority we ourselves built to police the property we ourselves invented.

Editorial: this is the opening line of The Social Contract (1762), so it is safe to quote and attribute.

Two ways back to absolute freedom

Rousseau sees only two routes 00:53:58. The first is to go back to the caves, which he immediately concedes is impossible now. So the only route left is to contract again, and this time design the contract so that it returns absolute freedom instead of taking it.

What he rejects

  • Against Hobbes. In Hobbes' contract the community surrendered all its liberty and freedom to a sovereign. Rousseau categorically rejects this 00:54:46.
  • Against Locke. Locke had us surrender partial rights and partial liberty to our representatives, who then make law for us. Rousseau disagrees here too 00:55:27, because for him the power to make law cannot be handed to anyone else and still be yours 01:17:28.

The move: surrender to the community itself

Not to a sovereign. Not to an assembly of persons. The community surrenders its power to the community itself 00:57:49. The giver and the receiver are the same body, so nothing is actually lost.

That community, having surrendered power to itself, becomes what Rousseau calls a public person, and by another name a body politic 00:58:48.

The double identity (learn this precisely, it is a favourite).

  • The body politic in its passive form is the state. In its active form it is the sovereign. The same community is both at once 00:59:53.
  • Its members likewise carry dual character. They are citizens when they make the law, and subjects when they obey the same law they imposed on themselves 01:02:02.

Particular will against general will

The lecture draws this as a two-way contrast, and treats "will of all" as another name for the particular will 01:02:39.

WillWhat it is
Particular will
(the lecture also calls this the will of all)		What one individual wants. In a class of a hundred, one person says one thing and another says another 01:02:39.
General willThat upon which the whole community agrees 01:04:02. It is my will, and your will, and everyone's will at once 01:05:14.

Where the textbook splits what the lecture joins (editorial, and worth marks). Rousseau's own scheme has three wills, not two, and the middle one is the point of the theory. Particular will is what one person wants. Will of all is those private wants merely added up, a majority of selfish preferences. General will is what aims at the common good. A vote can deliver the will of all and still miss the general will, which is precisely how Rousseau explains a majority getting it wrong. This lecture uses "will of all" as a synonym for the particular will, so learn the lecture's two-way contrast for the class and the three-way distinction for the paper.

Why this equals freedom

The argument is short and worth reproducing exactly, because it is the point of the whole theory 01:06:12:

  1. The general will is my will.
  2. If I obey the general will, I am obeying my own will.
  3. If I obey myself, I am free.

So law made on the basis of the general will, and not on a particular will nor on the will of God, means you are making the law for yourself, and therefore you remain absolutely free even while obeying 01:06:56.

The practical objection, and his answers

The lecturer raises the objection Rousseau's contemporaries raised: this is very idealistic and very abstract, so tell us in practical terms how the body politic actually operates 01:07:45. Rousseau gives two answers, and both create problems.

Answer 1: majority vote. The body politic runs on a majority vote 01:09:04. The trouble arrives immediately: the moment you say majority, you have created a minority 01:10:30. And what of those whose view differs from the general will? Rousseau's answer is the notorious one: they must be forced to be free 01:12:13. Freedom for him is the will of the majority, so compelling you to follow it is, on his logic, compelling you to be free. Editorial note worth carrying into an answer: this is the line later critics seize on to connect Rousseau to totalitarianism, though the lecturer himself does not raise that criticism and rates Rousseau highly 01:18:12.

Answer 2: the legislature must be composed of the whole community. 01:13:44 Not representatives, the whole community, because "the power to make law is the power to make law", and if you give it away to someone else it is no longer yours 01:17:28. The objection is obvious and the lecturer presses it: this is not a Greek city state, this is Pakistan with millions of people, so where is the hall? 01:14:41 Rousseau's model only works in a small state, a Greek city state 01:16:18.

The fair verdict. The lecturer's own reading is that Rousseau is impractical yet right in principle: people will contradict him the moment you question them on operation, but understand how golden the underlying idea is 01:16:48. Use that balance. Rousseau gives the strongest account of self-rule and freedom that exists, and the weakest account of how to run a country of millions. Direct democracy is his triumph and his limit.

6 Power checks power

⚙️ Montesquieu: liberty under law

01:25 – 01:45

Montesquieu, another French philosopher, appeared as a question in 2020 01:25:20. His purpose is single and clear: to secure the liberty of the individual 01:26:41.

The permanent problem of political science. Political science, in simple terms, is the relationship of the individual with the state 01:27:06. The never-ending question is which of the two to prioritise. Some philosophers say the state is important and the individual must be sacrificed for it. Others say the individual is the purpose, and the state exists to serve him, not the other way around. The two are inversely proportional: increase the authority of the state and the liberty of the individual automatically decreases, and vice versa 01:28:43. Montesquieu is trying to draw the balance line between them.

Liberty is not licence

Take the word liberty literally, as unrestrained and unchecked, and giving a man that does him no good at all. It is simply a licence to crime 01:29:45.

Real liberty, for Montesquieu, is the liberty we exercise under the law. Without law, liberty is a licence to crime. There must be laws, and there must not be anarchy, which means lawlessness 01:31:00.

Only a moderate government makes good laws

Laws must be of good form, and only a moderate government can make good laws. A government is moderate when you check power with power: separate the powers, and set each to check the other 01:31:50.

The state has three institutions: legislature, executive and judiciary 01:32:41. If the legislature can both make a law and implement it, where is your liberty? Give all that power to a single institution and your liberty is at risk 01:33:28. So: separation of powers, and checks and balances 01:42:16.

How his legislature is built

Montesquieu agrees with Rousseau that the legislature ought to be the whole community, then answers himself: in this era, direct parliament is not possible. So you must use the means of representation 01:34:17.

The line to remember. "Everybody doesn't know how to rule, but he knows who to rule." 01:34:17 That is the case for elections in one sentence: competence to govern is rare, competence to choose a governor is common.

Society contains two types of people, rich and poor, and both must be brought into the legislature. Montesquieu's device is a separate electorate: the rich vote for the rich, and the common people vote for their own 01:35:10. Some are royal or of quality by birth and belong in an upper class; the lower class elects the lower class. With both inside, parliament balances itself, and a balanced parliament makes good law 01:36:05.

Executive and judiciary

The executive of his day was the king, who with his ministers implements the laws passed by the balanced legislature 01:37:47. Crucially, the king's ministers can be called into parliament for questions and are bound to answer 01:38:47. That is ministerial accountability in embryo.

The judiciary was the weak one. Before the American Supreme Court, judiciary was not an important power, because it was always controlled by the executive, the king 01:38:47. That changed with John Marshall, Chief Justice of the USA, who reviewed a law in Marbury v. Madison in 1803 01:39:33. After that, judiciary won respect all over the world 01:40:33.

The irony that makes the best paragraph in the answer. Montesquieu visited England and wrote that England's institutions functioned by separation of powers 01:42:16. Ask the question the lecturer asks: is there separation of powers in England? No. There is supremacy of parliament. No authority or institution can stop the UK parliament from passing any law 01:42:53. So Montesquieu described a Britain that did not exist, and the political system his mistake actually built was the United States, which works quite the opposite way to the UK 01:43:41. A misreading of one country became the constitution of another.

7 Classification

👑 Montesquieu's three forms of government

01:49 – 02:03

Montesquieu classifies government into three kinds, and for each one he names the governing principle, the thing that actually makes it run 01:49:25. Get the pairing right and this section answers itself.

FormDefinitionWho really holds powerGoverning principle
Republic A government where rulers are elected 01:49:42. Few ruling makes it an aristocratic republic; many ruling makes it a democratic republic 01:50:33. The elected, drawn from the people Equality and rule of law: everybody is equal before the law 01:51:14
Monarchy A government of one person, through established laws 01:51:58. The nobles, who establish the laws and check the king 01:52:44 Honour: the ruler needs respect, the ruled need good government, so each serves the other 01:55:04
Despotism A government of one person, not through established laws 01:55:50. The ruler's word is law. He is not under the law, he is above it; in fact he is the law. The despot alone Fear: people follow because they are afraid 01:56:34

Precision note (editorial). Do not write "equality" or "rule of law" as the principle. The lecture gives the republic's governing principle as equality and rule of law. In The Spirit of the Laws (1748) Montesquieu's own term is virtue, and "rule of law" is not part of his scheme at all. Equality is not a synonym for the principle; it is one step down the chain from it. Montesquieu's own sequence runs: the principle is virtue, virtue in a republic is love of the republic, and love of the republic in a democracy is love of equality and of frugality. So equality is what that virtue consists in.

Two refinements that earn marks. First, "virtue" here means political virtue, love of country, equality and frugality, and not moral or Christian virtue; Montesquieu says so himself. Second, strictly the republic subdivides, so democracy runs on virtue and aristocracy runs on moderation, and "republic = virtue" collapses that distinction. Safest answer: republic (democratic) = virtue, aristocratic republic = moderation, monarchy = honour, despotism = fear.

Monarchy is a balance, and it can collapse

Think of it as a balance, which is the lecturer's own word for it 01:58:52. A monarchy has three elements: king, nobles and common people. Power resides with the nobles 01:58:01, and that is what holds the balance. Tip it and the form changes:

  • Nobles hold the balance → monarchy, ruling through established laws.
  • The king rises above them → the monarchy converts into despotism.
  • The people rise → democracy 01:58:52.

The worked contemporary example. Saudi Arabia was a monarchy, but as the king rises above the nobles it is converting now into despotism: "I am the law, I am the state" 01:59:41. The lecturer pairs this with Louis XIV, who declared "I am the state" 01:56:34.

Editorial caution, and it earns marks rather than losing them: "L'état, c'est moi" is attributed to Louis XIV rather than documented. It appears in no record of the sitting it is credited to, and surfaces in print only in 1818, more than 160 years later. His attested deathbed line runs almost the opposite way: "I depart, but the State shall always remain." Use it as the standard epigram of absolutism and label it apocryphal.

The trap in this section. Do not equate monarchy with despotism. Montesquieu's distinction is precise and it is exactly what a question tests: both are the rule of one person, and the entire difference is whether he rules through established laws. Rule of one under law is monarchy; rule of one above law is despotism. Equally, do not confuse republic with democracy: for Montesquieu, republic means rulers are elected, and democracy is only the variety of republic in which many rule.

Reference

📖 Glossary

Social contract
The agreement between the individual and the state that creates political authority; in a modern country its written form is the constitution.
Divine right of kings
The pre-contract claim that God makes the king, so the king answers to God alone and owes the people nothing.
State of nature
The imagined condition of humans before the state; an assumption used to work out how much power the state may have.
Natural law
In Locke, the law governing the state of nature, under which all were equal; it lacked only a body to define, execute and interpret it.
Unilateral contract
Hobbes' contract: an agreement among the people only. The sovereign is not a party, promises nothing and cannot breach.
Irrevocable
Hobbes' term of the contract: it cannot be revoked or taken back for as long as you live under it.
Bilateral contract
Locke's contract: the government is itself a party to it, so it carries duties and can be removed for breach.
Leviathan (editorial)
Hobbes' book (1651) and his name for the sovereign, a great monster built to hold all the smaller monsters in check. Outside context: in class the same idea arrives through the "release the Kraken" analogy.
Glorious Revolution
The transfer in which England's king voluntarily surrendered his power to parliament, converting monarchy toward democracy; bloodless, and Locke contributed to it. Editorial: the year is 1688.
Public person / body politic
Rousseau's name for the community once it has surrendered its power to itself.
State vs sovereign (Rousseau)
The same body politic: the state when passive, the sovereign when active.
Citizen vs subject (Rousseau)
The same person: a citizen when making the law, a subject when obeying it.
Particular will
What a single individual wants. This lecture uses "will of all" as another name for it.
Will of all (editorial)
In Rousseau's own scheme, a third term distinct from the other two: the particular wills merely added up, a majority of private preferences, which can differ from the common good. Not the sense used in this lecture.
General will
That on which the whole community agrees. Because it is your own will, obeying it is obeying yourself, which Rousseau calls freedom.
Forced to be free
Rousseau's answer to the dissenting minority: freedom is the will of the majority, so compelling them to follow it is compelling them to be free.
Liberty (Montesquieu)
Not unrestrained and unchecked freedom, which is a licence to crime, but freedom exercised under law.
Moderate government
Montesquieu's condition for good laws: a government in which power checks power.
Separation of powers
Dividing legislature, executive and judiciary so no single institution both makes and enforces the law.
Anarchy
Lawlessness; the condition Montesquieu says liberty collapses into without law.
Separate electorate
Montesquieu's device for a balanced legislature: rich elect the rich and commoners elect their own, so both classes sit in parliament.
Republic (Montesquieu)
A government where rulers are elected: aristocratic if few rule, democratic if many.
Despotism
Rule of one person without established laws, where the ruler is above the law and is the law; its principle is fear.
Marbury v. Madison (1803)
The case in which Chief Justice John Marshall reviewed a law, establishing judicial review and winning the judiciary its standing.
Self-test

🎴 Flashcards

What did the social contract replace, and why did that matter?

The divine right of kings. Under divine right the king was made by God, so he owed the people nothing and no accountability ran downward.

Why does every contract theorist invent a state of nature?

It is an argument device. Your picture of people without a state fixes how much power they must surrender to get one, so the assumption decides the conclusion.

Hobbes on human nature and the state of nature?

Pessimistic: man is a monster. The state of nature is a war of all against all, with no just or unjust, and might is right.

Name the two features of Hobbes' contract.

Unilateral (the sovereign is not a party to it) and irrevocable (you cannot take it back).

Why does Hobbes forbid revolution?

Revolution returns you to the state of nature, which is worse than any bad sovereign. Even real failures do not justify revolt.

What is the tension in Hobbes worth naming in an answer?

He destroys the divine right of kings, then grants the sovereign everything divine right ever gave him. He changes the source of authority but not its extent.

Locke on human nature?

Optimistic: man is good, simple, sympathetic and cooperative, and social from the very first day.

If Locke's state of nature was peaceful, why contract at all?

Natural law had three drawbacks: nothing to define it (legislature), nothing to implement it (executive), nothing to interpret it (judiciary). The contract does only that.

What makes Locke's contract bilateral?

The government is itself a party to it and is your representative, so it carries duties and can breach them, unlike Hobbes' sovereign.

What does Locke's state exist to protect, and what happens if it fails?

Life, property and above all liberty. If it fails you may overthrow it, but peacefully, and a peaceful overthrow is an election.

Where do Hobbes and Locke each land?

Hobbes on dictatorship and absolute rule; Locke on representative democracy.

Which revolution is Locke tied to, and what changed?

The Glorious Revolution (1688). It was bloodless, the king voluntarily surrendered power to parliament, converting monarchy toward democracy.

Quote Rousseau's opening line and explain the chains.

"Man is born free, but everywhere he is in chains." The chains are the authority we built ourselves to police the property we invented.

How does Rousseau's contract differ from Hobbes' and Locke's?

Power is surrendered neither to a sovereign nor to representatives, but to the community itself, so giver and receiver are the same body and nothing is lost.

Explain the two dual identities in Rousseau.

The body politic is the state when passive and the sovereign when active. Its members are citizens when making law and subjects when obeying it.

Distinguish particular will from general will, as this lecture draws it.

Particular will is one person's want, and the lecture calls that the will of all too. General will is what the whole community agrees on.

How does Rousseau's own scheme split those three? (editorial)

Particular will is one person's want; will of all is those wants merely added up; general will aims at the common good. A vote can produce the will of all and still miss the general will.

Why does obeying the general will count as freedom?

The general will is my will, so obeying it is obeying myself, and obeying myself is being free.

Give the two practical answers Rousseau offers, and the problem with each.

Majority vote, which creates a minority who must be "forced to be free"; and a legislature of the whole community, which only works in a small Greek city state.

What is liberty for Montesquieu?

Not unrestrained and unchecked freedom, which is a licence to crime, but liberty exercised under law.

What is a moderate government and why does it matter?

One where power checks power through separated institutions. Only a moderate government can make good laws.

State the irony about Montesquieu and England.

He described England as having separation of powers, but the UK has supremacy of parliament instead. His misreading shaped the United States, which works oppositely to the UK.

Distinguish monarchy from despotism in one line.

Both are rule of one person: monarchy rules through established laws, despotism rules without them, so the despot is above the law and is the law.

Pair each of Montesquieu's forms with its governing principle.

Republic: virtue, meaning political virtue, which is love of the republic and so love of equality and frugality (the lecture says equality and rule of law; write virtue). Monarchy: honour. Despotism: fear. Strictly, virtue belongs to the democratic republic and moderation to the aristocratic one.

How can a monarchy collapse?

It is a balance: power rests with the nobles. If the king rises above them it becomes despotism; if the people rise it becomes democracy. Saudi Arabia is the worked example of the first.

What is Marbury v. Madison (1803) doing in a Montesquieu lecture?

Judiciary was weak because the king controlled it. John Marshall's review of a law established judicial review and gave the third branch real standing.

🎯 Exam focus

  • Answer on rails, not in blocks. The comparison in section 4 is the highest-value thing in this lecture. Take human nature, then state of nature, then need of contract, then nature of contract, and give each philosopher on each rail. The lecturer models this at 00:44:45 and it reads as analysis rather than recall.
  • Always close the loop to a modern regime. The lecturer names two of these outright: Hobbes to dictatorship and absolute rule, Locke to representative democracy. The other two follow from what he teaches rather than from a label he gives: Rousseau to direct democracy (a legislature of the whole community, workable only in a small state), and Montesquieu to constitutional separation of powers. Theory questions reward the "so what".
  • Trace everything back to the assumption about human nature. The strongest single sentence you can write here is that Hobbes and Locke run the same argument and diverge only because one thinks man is a monster and the other thinks man is cooperative.
  • Know the precise keywords cold. Unilateral and irrevocable (Hobbes); bilateral and revocable (Locke); public person, body politic, general will, forced to be free (Rousseau); moderate government, separation of powers, checks and balances (Montesquieu). These are the words that earn marks.
  • Use the dates and cases as anchors. 1588 Armada and Hobbes' birth, 1642 civil war, 1649 Charles I executed, 1688 Glorious Revolution, 1803 Marbury v. Madison.
  • Keep the two traps straight. Monarchy is not despotism (established laws is the whole difference), and republic is not democracy (democracy is the variety of republic where many rule).
  • Criticism earns marks, so carry one per thinker. Hobbes rebuilds absolutism on new foundations; Locke's peaceful state of nature makes the contract look unnecessary; Rousseau's general will licenses coercion and only scales to a city state; Montesquieu misread the very country he cited.
