Criminal Investigation: Intelligence, Evidence, Interviews and Interrogation

How investigators turn raw information into action, use databases and scientific evidence, interview people without accusation, interrogate primary suspects, and shape the material into past-paper answers.

A sound investigation protects the innocent as carefully as it identifies the offender. Follow the information, preserve the evidence, and make every step explainable. 🔍

⭐ The five-minute version

  • Investigation must be intelligence-led and evidence-based. Information becomes useful after it is collected, analysed, evaluated and sent to the authority able to act on it. The aim may be prevention, suspect identification or the direction of the next investigative step.
  • Intelligence comes from different channels. The lecture uses open sources, bank records, cyberspace, informants, undercover work, phone records and surveillance. A planned urban bombing exercise shows how separate clues become one assessed picture.
  • Database investigation identifies or verifies people and things. Fingerprints, identity records, vehicle registration, telecom data, passport and immigration records, offender records and DNA databases can connect a trace to an existing record.
  • Electronic investigation follows digital evidence. Phones, computers, email, social media, GPS, cloud storage, call records, messages and deleted files may reveal contact, location and activity. The lecture places cybercrime inside the broader field of electronic investigation.
  • Forensics asks what collected evidence scientifically demonstrates. The examples include ballistics, toxicology, DNA analysis, pathology and forensic entomology. The point is to interpret evidence, support a lawful case and reduce the risk of a wrongful conviction.
  • An investigative interview is non-accusatory. It seeks truthful information from a victim, witness or suspect, with dignity and procedural safeguards. The lecture's interview methods are PEACE, free recall and the enhanced cognitive interview.
  • An interrogation is accusatory and suspect-focused. Its possible purpose is a confession. The class covers direct questioning, the Reid technique and good cop, bad cop, while rejecting physical violence.
  • Past-paper answers must connect process to justice. Start at the crime scene, protect the evidence, explain search patterns and chain of custody, then show how sound investigation establishes facts, identifies the offender, protects innocent people and gives courts admissible evidence.

The intelligence cycle in the urban-bombing exercise

The teacher asks the class to imagine a potential bombing being planned in an urban centre 00:03:57. The exercise is less about guessing one clue and more about combining different sources:

  1. Collect: informant reports, undercover observations, CCTV, phone records, digital activity and purchasing records.
  2. Analyse: look for recurring numbers, shared locations, common companies or unusual purchases such as repeated fertilizer orders 00:06:44.
  3. Classify: identify people or transactions that deserve closer examination.
  4. Evaluate: test innocent explanations. A farmer or a legitimate business may have a normal reason for buying fertilizer, so the information must be assessed before the suspect list is narrowed 00:08:24.
  5. Disseminate: send the assessed intelligence to the body able to act, such as the Counter Terrorism Department, local police or a bomb disposal unit 00:09:01.

The examiner's takeaway. Intelligence is analysed and evaluated information, not a loose collection of tips. In a prevention question, show the movement from several sources to assessment, prioritisation and timely dissemination. The lecture names CTD, FIA and the Intelligence Bureau as Pakistan-facing examples 00:10:13.

2 Records as evidence

🗃️ Database investigation

10:47-15:13

A database is an organised collection of data that can be retrieved through software. Database investigation, also called database-forensics investigation in the lecture, uses computerised records to identify a suspect or verify an identity 00:10:47.

The practical move. A trace from the case is run against an existing record. A crime-scene fingerprint can be compared with a fingerprint database; a licence plate can be checked against vehicle registration; an identity can be tested against national identity records. The database does not create the trace. It supplies the stored comparison that may turn the trace into a lead 00:11:54.

Databases named in class

Database or recordInvestigative use described
Offender and fingerprint recordsCompare a print or search people with a previous record relevant to the offence
NADRA identity recordsVerify national identity details
Vehicle registration / ETO recordsRun a number plate and determine the registered status of a vehicle

DNA databasesCompare a biological sample with a stored profile when an appropriate database exists

The lecture also asks students to read about China's extensive surveillance databases as a comparative example 00:12:48. Its purpose in this class is to show the reach of electronic record systems, not to supply a detailed China case study.

Distinguish method from result. Database investigation can identify, verify, include or exclude a possible suspect. An answer should still explain how the matched record relates to the case and how other evidence supports it.

4 Scientific interpretation

🧪 Forensic investigation

19:38-24:34

Forensic investigation uses scientific methods to examine collected evidence and determine what that evidence can support. Its result may guide the investigation or contribute to later court proceedings 00:19:38.

Forensic area namedQuestion it may help answer
Ballistics / firearm examinationWhat type of weapon may have fired a bullet, and what the impact may suggest about firing position or distance
ToxicologyWhat a substance in a sample may indicate
DNA analysisWhether a biological sample can be associated with a person or compared with another profile
PathologyWhat examination of the body or tissue may reveal
Forensic entomologyWhat insects associated with a body, vehicle or location may contribute to an inference

The vehicle-and-insects example

In the teacher's example, a murder suspect tries to flee in a car. Investigators examine insects caught in the grille or engine area. Because insect populations may be associated with particular environments, they may contribute to an assessment of where the vehicle travelled 00:21:41. The example illustrates the lecture's wider point: a small trace becomes meaningful through a specialised scientific field.

Four forms, four questions. Intelligence asks what collected information means and where it should go. A database asks whether a trace matches a stored record. Electronic investigation asks what digital devices and accounts reveal. Forensics asks what physical or biological evidence scientifically supports 00:23:07.

Why accuracy matters. The teacher links careful evidence gathering with a fair criminal justice process. A wrongful conviction punishes an innocent person, wastes the time of courts and prosecutors, and leaves the actual offence unresolved 00:24:02.

5 Worked application

🧩 Integrating the four forms

24:34-31:22

The class works through a hypothetical bank robbery in Islamabad involving four armed men who escape in a white Corolla 00:24:34. The transcript gives two different amounts for the stolen money, so the amount is omitted here. It does not affect the investigative method.

FormApplication to the scenario
Intelligence operationBefore the offence, gather tips, monitor digital footprints and assess information that could support interception. After the offence, analyse information and direct it to the relevant units.
Database investigationSearch for people previously connected with similar robberies, compare fingerprints, run the Corolla's registration through vehicle records and check official databases.
Electronic investigationCollect CCTV footage, examine mobile data and review relevant electronic devices and surveillance systems.
Forensic investigationSecure the scene, collect fingerprints and other physical traces, examine ballistic evidence and assess any footprints or biological material.

How the methods connect

  1. Secure the crime scene so evidence is preserved.
  2. Collect traces such as prints, possible weapon-related evidence, CCTV and vehicle information.
  3. Interpret traces through the suitable forensic discipline.
  4. Run identifiers through databases to generate or test leads.
  5. Examine electronic records for contact, movement and activity.
  6. Analyse the combined picture before narrowing the suspect list or deciding the next action.

The teacher also suggests that footprint analysis may contribute to tentative estimates about a person's physical characteristics 00:29:37. Treat such an inference as supporting information, not as a standalone identification.

Use the scenario as a paragraph plan. An applied question becomes easier when each investigative form has its own evidence, purpose and next step. Do not list techniques without showing how they operate on the facts.

6 Non-accusatory fact finding

💬 Investigative interviews

34:28-53:08

An investigative interview is a non-accusatory conversation used to obtain truthful information from a victim, witness or suspect. Its purpose is fact finding that helps solve the case. The interviewer is not seeking a confession and should not use force 00:37:30.

The presumption and the tone. The interview begins from a presumption of innocence. The teacher connects that presumption with an open, respectful process that avoids bias and treats the person as a source of information rather than as someone already proved guilty 00:41:36.

Safeguards emphasised in class

  • Respect the person's dignity.
  • Do not degrade the person or use torture and ill treatment.
  • Do not turn an information-gathering interview into arrest or detention.
  • Explain the person's rights and the process.
  • Do not compel speech merely because the investigator wants more information 00:40:30.

The lecturer illustrates the voluntary character by saying a person can ask whether they are being accused and, if the answer is no, ask to leave 00:42:16. This is presented as the classroom expression of a non-accusatory interview, not as a complete statement of arrest and detention law.

The PEACE model

The lecture places the PEACE model in the United Kingdom in the 1990s and links its development to concern about forceful confessions and the need for human-rights safeguards 00:44:09. It teaches the following five-stage route:

PhaseWhat the interviewer does
P: Preparation and planningClarify the purpose, information needs and plan before the conversation.
E: Engage and ExplainBuild rapport, help the person feel comfortable, and develop trust in the interviewer and process 00:45:29.
A: Account, Clarification and ChallengeObtain the person's detailed account. Ask questions that clarify the investigation and ensure important details are accounted for 00:45:50.
C: ClosureSummarise the interview, confirm the shared understanding and explain the next steps 00:47:04.
E: EvaluationAssess the information, the quality of the answers and whether the interview produced useful information 00:47:44.

Editorial: formal wording verified. The lecture uses the short labels "Engage" and "Account". The recognised formal wording is Engage and Explain and Account, Clarification and Challenge.

Two further interview techniques

Free recall. Allow the interviewee to describe everything remembered without interruption. Questions follow after the uninterrupted account, so the person's own sequence and details emerge first 00:49:21.

Enhanced cognitive interview (ECI). Use context and memory prompts, including closing the eyes and imagining the scene 00:50:29. The teacher says the method requires expertise in cognitive psychology 00:51:16.

A possible-offender sketch may be prepared before, during or after the interview 00:52:05.

The line to hold. An interview seeks information in a non-accusatory setting. A confession is not its organising purpose.

7 Accusatory questioning

🔍 Interrogation

54:13-1:23:08

Interrogation is the stronger, accusatory form of questioning used with an accused person or primary suspect. It is focused, may use direct questions, and may aim to obtain a confession when the investigator's information points strongly to the suspect 00:55:59.

DimensionInvestigative interviewInterrogation
Basic characterNon-accusatoryAccusatory
People involvedVictim, witness or suspectUsually a primary suspect or accused person
Main purposeGather truthful, additional informationTest the suspect's account and possibly obtain a confession
Presumption describedApproach the person as innocentProceed because the existing information has made the person a strong suspect
ToneOpen, soft and fact-findingFocused, harder and direct

Direct questioning

Direct questioning confronts the suspect with focused questions. The teacher notes that it may be ineffective with a hardened offender who recognises the attempt to obtain a statement and simply refuses to provide one 00:59:03. This leads into structured psychological techniques.

The Reid technique as taught

The lecture attributes the method to John E. Reid in the United States and describes it as confession-oriented: a structured attempt to obtain a voluntary confession without physical force 00:59:32. It presents two broad stages:

  1. Non-accusatory interview: observe verbal and non-verbal responses and decide whether the account appears truthful or deceptive 01:01:36.
  2. Nine-step interrogation: if the investigator judges the person deceptive, move into the accusatory sequence 01:02:37.

Watch the evidential leap. The lecture describes the first stage as observing whether a suspect appears truthful or deceptive. An investigator's reading of behaviour is still an assessment, not proof that the person is lying. The case must remain anchored in corroborated evidence.

The steps the class explains in detail

Step taughtLecture explanation
1. Direct or positive confrontationTake control and state the accusation firmly 01:05:31.
2. Theme developmentOffer a narrative that makes the conduct psychologically easier to discuss, such as an accident, anger or a crime of passion 01:07:35.
3. Handling denialsExpect denials, interrupt them and keep the theme moving. The teacher also notes that a denial may reveal another name or detail 01:09:21.
4. Overcoming objectionsListen to and use the suspect's explanations, show some empathy and avoid shutting the suspect down completely 01:11:03.
Later movementThe recording summarises the remaining steps rather than naming each one: use alternatives and the information already disclosed to work toward an oral confession, then record it 01:12:34.

The lecturer says the Reid technique has been widely used, has attracted criticism, and is accepted by some police departments and rejected by others 01:13:05. The class does not develop the criticism, so this note does not invent a list of objections.

Good cop, bad cop

Two interrogators coordinate contrasting roles. The bad cop adopts an aggressive, accusatory and uncooperative posture. The good cop then removes or distances that pressure, shows empathy and offers a softer conversational corner. The intended psychological movement is from discomfort toward cooperation with the apparently sympathetic interrogator 01:15:20.

Legal and ethical boundary. The teacher repeatedly rejects physical torture and violence, calling them contrary to law and human rights 01:18:14. A confession-oriented technique does not authorise physical abuse.

The lecture closes the distinction by restating that interviews seek additional information without accusation, while interrogations use an accusatory tone with primary suspects and may seek an oral confession 01:22:24.

2023: crime scene and search patterns

Question as read in class: Why is a crime scene important for investigators, and what are crime-scene search patterns? 01:23:47

The teacher proposes the following answer structure:

  1. Open with the crime scene as the starting point of every criminal investigation and the investigator's first major point of interaction.
  2. Identify the evidence located there: physical, biological and digital material, including fingerprints, DNA, bloodstains, weapons and footprints 01:24:46.
  3. Explain preservation: secure the scene and keep evidence untampered so it remains useful in court.
  4. Show the outcomes: reconstruct the crime, identify the offender, link a suspect with evidence and choose the next course of action.
  5. Close with justice: proper scene work supports the right conviction and reduces the risk of a wrongful one 01:25:51.

Search patterns taught

PatternHow the lecture describes it

Correction: use the technical pattern definitions. The lecture informally describes a strip search as looking first at large areas and a grid search as checking furniture and drawers. More precise usage is spatial: a line or strip search moves in parallel lines, a grid repeats the line search at a right angle, and a zone or quadrant search divides the scene into manageable sectors. Keep room furniture and drawers as examples of places searched, not as the definition of a grid pattern.

F Recall

🃏 Flashcards

When does information become intelligence?

When it has been analysed and evaluated so it can support a decision, prevention, suspect identification or another action.

Name the intelligence sources captured in this recording.

Open sources, financial records, cyberspace, human sources or undercover work, signals or phone records, and surveillance such as CCTV.

What are the key moves in the urban-bombing exercise?

Collect from several sources, analyse links, classify leads, evaluate innocent explanations, narrow the suspect list and disseminate to the unit able to act.

What does database investigation do?

It runs a trace or identifier against organised stored records to identify, verify, include or exclude a lead.

Which databases does the lecture name?

Offender and fingerprint records, NADRA identity data, vehicle registration, telecom records, passport and immigration records, and DNA databases.

What evidence may electronic investigation examine?

Phones, computers, laptops, email, social media, GPS, cloud storage, call records, messages and deleted files.

How is electronic investigation related to cybercrime?

Electronic investigation is the broader method; cybercrime is one category of offending that can require it.

What question does forensic investigation answer?

What does the collected evidence scientifically support?

Name five forensic areas from the lecture.

Ballistics, toxicology, DNA analysis, pathology and forensic entomology.

What is an investigative interview?

A non-accusatory, information-gathering conversation with a victim, witness or suspect.

What does PEACE mean in the class sequence?

Preparation and Planning; Engage and Explain; Account, Clarification and Challenge; Closure; Evaluation. The lecture uses shorter labels for the middle phases.

How does free recall work?

The interviewee gives an uninterrupted account of everything remembered, followed by clarifying questions.

What is the enhanced cognitive interview?

A method using context reconstruction and memory prompts to help an interviewee retrieve details.

How does interrogation differ from interview?

It is accusatory, aimed at a primary suspect and may seek a confession; an interview is non-accusatory fact finding.

What two broad stages of the Reid technique are taught?

A non-accusatory interview to assess the account, followed, if deception is suspected, by a nine-step interrogation.

Which Reid steps does the lecture explain?

Direct or positive confrontation, theme development, handling denials and overcoming objections; later steps are summarised as movement toward an oral confession.

What is the psychology of good cop, bad cop?

One interrogator creates pressure through aggression; the other offers empathy and a softer route toward cooperation.

Why is the crime scene important?

It is the investigation's starting point and holds evidence needed to reconstruct the offence, identify the offender and determine the next action.

Name the three search patterns taught.

Line or strip, grid, and quadrant or zone.

Why does a sound investigation matter to justice?

It establishes facts, identifies the correct offender, protects innocent people, finds admissible evidence and strengthens public confidence.

Suggested answer structure

  1. Define the process and tie it to fact finding, fairness and the criminal justice system.
  2. Separate the forms by the type of material each uses: assessed information, stored records, digital evidence and scientific evidence.
  3. Add the human techniques by drawing the interview-interrogation distinction before naming PEACE or Reid.
  4. Use one worked example to show how methods combine. The bank-robbery exercise is ready-made.
  5. End with safeguards and outcomes: legality, objectivity, preservation, chain of custody, admissibility, correct identification and protection of the innocent.

Common mistakes

  • Treating raw information as intelligence without analysis and evaluation.
  • Using database, electronic and forensic investigation as interchangeable labels.
  • Calling an investigative interview confession-oriented.
  • Listing all nine Reid steps when this lecture explains only the first four in detail.
  • Repeating the teacher's informal grid-search definition instead of the technical spatial definition.
  • Writing "FMU is under SBP" or using an incomplete name for the Qanun-e-Shahadat law.
  • Quoting a past-paper year or wording that the audio does not clearly support.

Editorial: the one-line thesis. A credible investigation converts information into tested evidence through lawful methods, then presents a traceable case that can identify the offender without sacrificing the protection owed to innocent people.
