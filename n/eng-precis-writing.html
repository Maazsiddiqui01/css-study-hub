Précis Writing: The Five-Reading Method

The single most decisive question on the paper, taught as a repeatable process. Five readings, a core idea that guides everything, and a draft cut to one-third in your own words.

Do not write more. Write the gist. The whole skill is finding the one central idea and letting it decide what stays and what goes. ✍️

⭐ The five-minute version

  • What it is. A précis is a shortened version of a passage, about one-third of its length, written in your own words, that keeps every important idea and drops everything else. No examples, no quotations, no comments of your own.
  • Why it matters. Sir Danish calls the précis the deciding factor in the Précis & Composition paper. It rewards a real skill, so a strong précis lifts the whole paper and a lost core idea can sink it.
  • The method is five readings, in order, and you skip none. (1) Read for the general sense. (2) Read to underline keywords and find the core idea. (3) Read to note down every essential point. (4) Combine the points into a first draft. (5) Revise the draft.
  • The core idea is the whole game. It is the "north star" that tells you which points are important and which are not. Find it, and the précis writes itself around it. Miss it, and you can walk in the wrong direction and score zero.
  • Then cut. Replace wordy phrases with single words: "due to the fact that" becomes because, "in today's modern world" becomes nowadays. Simple words with the right structure beat long ones.
  • Give it a title. Build it from the chain of key ideas. For a passage on how climate shapes a nation's working habits, the title "Climate and National Character" falls out of that chain.
  • In the exam, budget about 50 minutes: roughly 10 to 15 to read and find the core idea, 15 to extract and order points, 10 to 12 to draft, 10 to finalise. Keep a few minutes at the end to review.
  • How to practise now. Quality first, speed later. Do every step slowly, and start timing yourself only from around December. Send your essential points and your final draft to your teacher so your teacher can correct the whole process, the reading and the drafting, where the marks are really won or lost.
1 The stakes

🎯 What a précis is, and why it decides the paper

Précis Writing

A précis is a compressed version of a passage that keeps the substance and throws away the padding. The word is the point: you are precising, boiling a passage down to its essence, in far fewer words but with nothing important lost.

Sir Danish is blunt about where this sits in the paper. "Usually people say that précis is the most deciding factor in Précis and Composition" Practice · 01:00. It carries real weight and, unlike the shorter mechanical questions, it tests a skill you cannot fake: can you read a dense passage, find what it is actually saying, and say it again, shorter and cleaner, in your own English?

Why it rewards you. The précis is a genuine skill, so improvement is visible and bankable. Once you own the process, "you will feel more liberated," as he puts it, because you know that whatever passage appears, you can walk it through the same five steps and come out with something worth marks Practice · 01:31.

And why it can hurt you. The same weight cuts both ways. If you miss the central idea and start summarising the wrong thing, "it's very likely that you are going in a very different direction, which can cause zero marks" Practice · 03:53. A précis is not marked kindly for effort if it has understood the passage backwards. That single risk is why the method below front-loads so much care into finding the core idea before you write a word.

2 The rules

📏 The rules a précis must obey

Précis Writing 2

Before the method, the requirements. These are the fixed conditions your finished précis has to satisfy. Sir Danish has students quickly revise them before writing Précis 2 · 21:04; treat them as a checklist your finished précis must pass.

RequirementWhat it means in practice
About one-third the lengthThe default target is roughly a third of the original passage Précis 2 · 21:14. If the question specifies an exact word count (say 160 or 170 words), write to that number instead Précis 2 · 22:39.
Continuous narrativeIt must read as one flowing piece. Each sentence should lead into the next with no jolt or jerk of thought between them Précis 2 · 23:24. Not a list, not bullet points.
All important ideas keptEvery relevant idea in the passage should be present. You decide what is important and what is filler, then keep the important and cut the rest.
Your own wordsDo not lift the passage's phrases. Recast the meaning in your own English. This is what the exercise is testing.
A titleThe précis carries a title that captures the central idea (see section 4 for how to build one).

Editorial: the standard précis conventions worth adding. Beyond what the class states, examiners expect a few more fixed habits, and they are free marks once they are automatic. Write in the third person and in reported (indirect) speech, so any dialogue in the passage is converted out of quotation marks. Keep it in the past tense as the default narrative tense. Include no direct quotations, no illustrative examples, and no opinion of your own: a précis reports the passage, it does not argue with it. And it is one single, unbroken paragraph.

3 The process

🪜 The five-reading method

Précis Writing

The heart of the class is a five-step method, and each step is a fresh reading of the passage with a different job. The rule is simple: do all five, in order, and skip none Précis · 13:17.

ReadingIts jobWhat you produce
1. First readingUnderstand the general sense of the passage. Do not write anything. Just grasp what it is broadly about.A feel for the topic
2. Second readingUnderline keywords and find the core idea. Mark the words that carry the meaning.Marked keywords + the central idea
3. Third readingNote down all the essential points, in your own short form, beside the passage.A list of essential points
4. Fourth readingCombine the essential points into a first draft, in continuous sentences.A rough first draft
5. Fifth readingRevise the draft: tighten the language, check the length, fix the flow.The final précis

He repeats the sequence deliberately so it lodges in memory: "First reading, to understand a general sense of the passage. Second reading, underline keywords and find core idea. Third reading, note down all essential points. Fourth reading, combine all essential points and make first draft. Fifth reading, revise your draft" Précis · 12:46.

Why skipping a step backfires. Each reading does work the next one depends on. Jump straight to drafting without having isolated the core idea and your points have no spine to hang on; skip the revision and the wordiness and errors stay in. As he says, "if you miss any step, or if you want to skip it, it will not be good" Précis · 13:17. Right now, treat each step as a separate exercise: a whole class could be spent on any one of them.

These first drafts are rough work, and that is allowed. In the exam you do this on the last pages of the answer sheet, which are meant for rough work. You write your essential points there, then a first draft, then a revised draft, and only when you are satisfied do you copy the final version onto the main answer sheet Précis · 16:21.

4 The centre of gravity

⭐ The core idea: your north star

Précis + Practice

If you take one thing from this note, take this. The core idea is the central point the whole passage exists to make, and it is the single most important thing you extract.

Sir Danish gives it the best possible image: the core idea is the north star that guides you, the fixed point that tells you which passing point is important and which is not, and how the points should be organised Time Mgmt · 01:24. Everything downstream, which points you keep, what order they go in, what the title is, is decided by the core idea. So you find it early, at the second reading, and you hold onto it.

This is where marks are won or lost outright. "If you miss the core idea and do not get the main gist of the passage, it's very likely that you are going in a very different direction, which can cause zero marks" Practice · 03:53. On the other hand, even if you get some smaller points slightly wrong, a correctly identified core idea keeps you pointed the right way: "it helps you to give, go in a direction; the only thing is that you can craft it better" Practice · 03:22. So the core idea is both the biggest risk and the biggest safety net on the paper.

How to build the title from the core idea

The title is just the core idea named in a few words, and Sir Danish shows how to derive it by chaining the key concepts. Working the climate passage, he lays out the links in his head: climate, then labour, then habits, then national character. From that chain he lands on "Climate and National Character" as the title, because it captures the two ends of the chain and everything in between Précis · 12:15. Build your title the same way: list the two or three ideas the passage connects, then name the connection.

The title is worth real marks, so never leave it blank. In the FPSC scheme the précis question carries 20 marks, and 5 of those are for the title alone (the other 15 for the précis itself). That is a quarter of the question for a few words. Keep it short, four to eight words, a phrase and not a full sentence, and make sure it names the core idea rather than just a topic the passage mentions in passing.

5 Worked example

🌡️ The method in action: climate and national character

Précis Writing

The whole class turns on one passage, and watching it go through the five readings is the fastest way to see how the method feels. The passage argues that climate shapes the working habits of a people, and through those habits, a nation's character.

Reading 1: the general sense

The passage says climate influences labour in two ways: by energising or draining it, and, more tellingly, through the effect it has on the regularity of a worker's habits Précis · 00:12. After one reading you should be able to say, roughly: this is about how climate affects how steadily people work.

Reading 2: keywords and core idea

Now the underlining. The words that carry the meaning: climate, labour, habits, northern regions, temperate regions, steady, industry, national character. And the core idea surfaces: how climate influences labour habits, and through them the national character of a country Précis · 06:51. He is strict that this is discovered, not invented: "I am not creating my story, but I feel that these are important words that are underlined" Précis · 03:07.

Reading 3: the essential points

Noted down in short form, the points come out as:

  • Climate influences labour and affects its habits.
  • People of temperate regions work steadily and regularly.
  • People of colder northern regions do not: severe weather and short, dark days make regular outdoor exertion hard, and the labouring class loses its motivation.
  • So such countries develop a changing, unstable national character, while regions of steady activity develop a steady one.
  • Agriculture is inconsistent where the weather is harsh Précis · 05:15.

Readings 4 and 5: draft, then revise

The points are combined into a first draft, and then that draft is tightened. His revised version reads close to: "Climate shapes national character by dictating the activities of the labour class and affecting their everyday habits", opening on the core idea, then a sentence contrasting the enterprising temperate worker with the north whose zeal is interrupted by extreme weather, so an erratic national character emerges Précis · 09:38. Notice the finished piece is continuous, in his own words, and built outward from the central idea.

The title, derived. Chaining climate → labour → habits → character, he settles on "Climate and National Character" Précis · 12:15. It names the two ends of the passage's argument in four words.

What to copy from this. Do not memorise this passage; memorise the shape of the walk. General sense, then keywords and core idea, then a bare list of points, then a flowing draft that starts from the core idea, then a tightening pass. The content changes every year; the walk does not.

6 Compression

✂️ Cutting the words: say it shorter

Précis Writing 2

A précis lives or dies on economy. A large part of the second class is a drill in eliminating unnecessary words: spotting a long phrase and replacing it with the single word that means the same thing Précis 2 · 02:13.

Wordy originalTightened
due to the fact thatbecause
in today's modern worldnowadays
make a decisiondecide
postponed the event due to bad weatherpostponed it because of bad (or inclement) weather
the authorities (a whole institution, one word will do)keep only the word that carries the meaning

The worked lines from the class: "the authorities postponed the event due to bad weather" tightens to "due to unfavourable weather," and if your vocabulary is good, "bad" can become inclement Précis 2 · 02:58. "In today's modern world" is simply nowadays Précis 2 · 04:16. Phrases like "due to the fact that" and "for the purpose of making a decision" are pure padding and go straight out Précis 2 · 03:36.

The principle underneath the drill. Sir Danish returns to this again and again: you do not need big words, you need the right words. Simple words in a clean structure carry more force than long or archaic ones, and in a précis every wasted word is a word you did not have room for. Cutting wordiness is not decoration; it is how you fit all the important ideas into one-third of the space.

7 A subtle mark-winner

🖋️ "The author says": when to attribute

Précis Writing 2

A finer point that separates a good précis from an average one: knowing when to write "the author says" and when to state something as a plain fact. A student asks exactly this, and the answer is a useful rule Précis 2 · 14:41.

The rule. Look at the information being conveyed. If it is a specific, personalised claim, an opinion or a particular recommendation the writer is making, attribute it: "the author suggests", "the writer argues". If it is a general truth that holds for everyone, state it plainly, with no attribution needed Précis 2 · 15:18.

His worked contrast: if the passage says you should read literature, history and current affairs beyond your syllabus, that is a specific recommendation from the writer, so you write "the author advises...". But if the underlying point is the general one, that wide reading improves writing, you can state it directly: "extensive reading is required for improving writing skills", a generic truth that needs no attribution Précis 2 · 16:47.

Why this is two skills, not one. He frames it as two abilities you need at the same time: the ability to capture the central idea and state it generically, and the ability to associate that idea with the specific subject or opinion the writer attached to it. For a précis you often want both: the general truth, then, where the writer was being specific, the attribution that shows you noticed Précis 2 · 17:32.

8 Second worked example

😴 Finding the gist: a passage in praise of idleness

Précis Practice

A second passage, worked live in the practice class, shows how much rests on the core idea, because this one is designed to mislead you. It looks like an attack on laziness and turns out to be the opposite.

The passage opens: "All the evils in this world are brought about by the persons who are always up and doing, but do not know when they ought to be up, nor what they ought to be doing" Practice · 59:57. It pictures the Devil as the busiest creature in the universe, denouncing laziness and angry at the smallest waste of time. Then it turns: the world is in a muddle, but the writer does not think laziness caused it. "It is not the active virtues that it lacks, but the passive ones. It is capable of anything but kindness and a little steady thought" Practice · 01:02:21. Its example is the statesmen who rushed to the Versailles peace conference "with a bundle of ill-digested notions and a great deal of energy," when the world would have gained had they lounged on a hillside doing nothing first.

The trap, and how the core idea springs it. Read carelessly and you summarise "laziness is bad, be active." That is the surface, and it is wrong. Students in the class first offer exactly that, then correct toward the real point Practice · 01:10:09. The core idea is that the world's problems come from misdirected, restless activity, not from laziness; what the world lacks is kindness and calm, steady reflection, not more energy Practice · 01:18:36. Everything you keep in the précis has to serve that idea. The busy-Devil image and the Versailles example are illustrations of it, so in the précis they compress to a single clause or drop out entirely.

The lesson stacked on the lesson. This is why the second reading matters so much. A passage can spend its first half sounding like it argues one thing and its second half reversing it. Only a reader who is hunting for the whole gist, not the opening sentiment, catches the turn. "Précis is about reading well," he says. "Everything is about" the reading Practice · 01:08:41.

9 Under the clock

⏱️ Timing the précis in the exam

Time Management

In the exam the précis gets roughly 50 minutes, and Sir Danish maps the five readings onto that clock so you never run out of time mid-draft Time Mgmt · 01:24.

MinutesWhat you are doing
First 10 to 15Read the passage repeatedly for the general sense and the core idea. This is readings 1 and 2. Do not rush it: the core idea is worth the time Time Mgmt · 01:24.
Next ~15Extract the essential points, arrange them in order, and plan how you will write them (reading 3).
At ~30 minPut the passage aside. Working only from your points and your mental outline, write the rough draft in the next 10 to 12 minutes (reading 4) Time Mgmt · 01:54.
Final ~10Give the draft its final form (reading 5), then copy the finished précis onto the answer sheet.

Protect the review window. His firm instruction: by about 10:45 in a paper that ends at 11, you should be writing out the final draft, not still drafting, so that you keep the last 5 to 10 minutes to review the whole paper Time Mgmt · 02:23. A précis finished at the buzzer with no review usually reads worse than one finished five minutes early and checked.

The mindset line. He splits the paper's outcome as roughly 60% preparation and mindset, 40% what you do once the clock starts Time Mgmt · 02:51. And a warning that speaks to the whole paper: writing more does not earn more. "Just quickly writing a lot" does not bring good marks; a controlled, well-managed answer does Time Mgmt · 02:51.

How to practise between now and the exam

Two habits close the note. First, quality before speed: right now, do every step slowly and properly, without worrying about the clock, and only start timing yourself from around December once the process is solid Practice · 05:08. Second, practise the whole process, rough work included: send your teacher your essential points (step 3) and your revised final draft (step 5), because seeing your rough work is how a teacher catches where your reading went wrong, which the final draft alone would hide Précis · 18:50.

X Strategy

🎯 Exam focus

The one habit that matters most

Do not start writing until you can say the core idea out loud in a sentence. Everything else in this note, the points, the order, the title, the cutting, is downstream of that one sentence. A précis with the right core idea and clumsy English still scores; a polished précis of the wrong idea does not.

The checklist to run before you copy out

  • Length: about one-third, or the exact count if given. Count it.
  • Own words: no phrases lifted from the passage.
  • All important ideas in, illustrations out: examples and quotations compressed or dropped.
  • One flowing paragraph: continuous narrative, no bullet points, no jerks between sentences.
  • Third person, indirect speech, past tense.
  • No opinion of your own. You are reporting the passage, not debating it.
  • A title that names the core idea.

Common ways to lose marks

MistakeFix
Summarising the opening sentiment, not the whole gistRead to the end before deciding the core idea; watch for a mid-passage turn (the laziness trap)
Lifting the passage's phrasesRecast in your own words; if you have written a whole phrase from the passage, rewrite it
Keeping examples and quotationsCompress an illustration to the point it illustrates, or cut it
Writing too much because it "feels safer"More is not better. Hit one-third and stop. Controlled beats long
Running out of time with no reviewBe writing the final draft by ~10:45; protect the last 5 to 10 minutes
Forgetting the titleBuild it last from the core idea, but never leave it blank

The through-line of the whole method. Précis is a reading skill wearing a writing skill's clothes. "Précis is about reading well," Sir Danish says, and the five steps are really four readings that make sure you have understood before one act of writing that says it back, shorter. Get the reading right and the writing is easy. Rush the reading and no amount of good English saves the answer.
