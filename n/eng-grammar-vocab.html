⭐ The five-minute version

  • The paper. English (Précis and Composition) is 100 marks in 3 hours, all written (no MCQs). Seven sections: Précis (20), Comprehension (20), Grammar & Vocabulary (20), Sentence Correction (10), Grouping of Words (10), Pairs of Words (10), Translation (10).
  • The two big skills are covered in their own notes. Précis, Comprehension and Translation each have a dedicated note. This note is the rest: the grammar and vocabulary questions, and the strategy that ties the whole paper together.
  • Spend the three hours deliberately. Sir Danish's plan: about 35 minutes on comprehension, roughly an hour on the short questions (translation, pairs, prepositions, sentence correction), and about 50 minutes to an hour on the précis, with a few minutes at the end to review.
  • Prepositions are small words that carry meaning. Learn the pairs that trip people up: at vs in for time, by vs with for agent and instrument, among vs between, beside vs besides, and the phrasal verbs (call on/off/for, put up with, and the rest).
  • Vocabulary is a daily habit, not a cram. Three confusable words a day, from a fixed source, builds 180 to 200 in two months. Cramming eight hours the night before does not stick.
  • The governing idea. "Simple words with the right prepositions can have very strong meaning." You do not need big words. You need the right small ones, used correctly.
  • Do not chase marks by writing more. Across the whole paper, a controlled, well-timed answer beats a long, rushed one.
1 The map

🗺️ The paper at a glance

Paper overview

Before the individual questions, the shape of the whole paper. It is a 100-mark, three-hour paper, entirely descriptive, with seven sections. Knowing the marks tells you where to spend your time.

SectionWhat it asksMarks
Précis WritingPrécis a passage to one-third, and give it a title (15 for the précis, 5 for the title)20
Reading ComprehensionA passage with five questions, four marks each, answered in your own words20
Grammar & VocabularyTenses, articles, prepositions, conjunctions, punctuation, phrasal verbs, synonyms and antonyms20
Sentence CorrectionRewrite flawed sentences, correcting the error with the smallest change10
Grouping of WordsPair 20 words by similar or opposite meaning10
Pairs of WordsDifferentiate five of ten confusable pairs, explain and use each in a sentence10
TranslationTen short Urdu sentences (or a short paragraph) into English10
Total100

This is not the Essay paper. CSS has a separate compulsory English Essay paper (100 marks, one long essay). Précis and Composition is the language-skills paper above. Do not confuse the two, and do not bring essay-style writing into the short answers here: this paper rewards precision, not expansion.

Where the reliable marks are. Précis and comprehension reward skill and carry risk. The grammar, vocabulary, grouping, pairs and translation questions, 60 marks together, reward preparation and carry almost no risk if you have done the daily work. A candidate who quietly banks those 60 has room to breathe on the two harder sections. That is why the "small" questions deserve serious, if short, daily attention.

2 The clock

⏱️ How to spend the three hours

Time Management

Sir Danish gives a concrete time plan for the whole paper. The point is to reach the précis with enough time left to do it justice, and to keep a review window at the end.

TimeWhat you are doing
First ~35 minComprehension. Five short answers on the passage Time Mgmt Precis · 00:21.
Next ~1 hourThe short questions: translation, pairs of words, prepositions, sentence correction. These are the four quick 10-mark items Time Mgmt Precis · 00:21.
Within that, ~25 minSynonyms, antonyms and grouping. Answer clearly (write the option or the word), and keep an eye on time and efficiency Time Mgmt Precis · 01:28.
Last ~50 to 60 minPrécis. First 10 minutes for the general sense, then points, draft and revision (see the Précis note for the minute-by-minute plan) Time Mgmt Precis · 02:55.
Final 5 to 10 minReview the whole paper. Protect this window; do not still be drafting at the buzzer.

Why the précis goes last for many candidates. There is a case, which Sir Danish discusses, for doing the précis at the end: it is the most demanding question, and finishing the mechanical sections first "liberates" you, so you meet the précis with a settled mind and a clear block of time rather than under early-paper pressure Practice · 01:31. The sequence on the answer sheet can differ from the order questions are printed; that is allowed, as long as you number your answers correctly.

The mindset split. He puts the outcome at roughly 60% preparation and mindset, 40% execution once the clock starts. And the warning that governs the whole paper: writing more does not earn more. A calm, well-managed script beats a frantic, over-long one. Answer, then stop.

3 Prepositions

🔗 The small words that carry weight

Practice

Prepositions are a favourite of this paper because they are small and precise: one wrong preposition changes the meaning. Sir Danish's framing is that they are powerful tools, and using the right one enhances your writing more than any long word could Practice · 47:30.

The pairs that trip people up

PairRuleExample
at / in (time)at for a point of time; in for a period or portion of time"at four o'clock"; "in a few weeks"
by / withby for the agent or doer; with for the instrument"I write by hand with a pencil"
by / with (again)by also = nearness; with = accompaniment"he sat by the river"; "he lives with me"
among / betweenamong for three or more; between for two"divided among three brothers"; "between two brothers"
beside / besidesbeside = next to; besides = in addition to"sat beside me"; "besides Emma, others were present"

Worked from the class: "I write by my hand with a pencil", where by marks the doer and with the instrument Practice · 44:59; "the property was divided among three brothers" but "between two brothers" Practice · 46:26; and "besides Emma, others were present at the dinner" for besides meaning in addition to Practice · 46:58.

Phrasal verbs: one verb, many meanings

The other half of the prepositions question is phrasal verbs, where a single verb plus different prepositions gives completely different meanings. Sir Danish's point: if you know the exact meaning of each, they are more powerful than a single big word Practice · 48:29. His run of examples:

VerbSome of its phrasal forms
callcall on, call off, call out, call for, call upon, call by, call into
putput in, put off, put down, put on, put out, put through, put up, put up with
letlet in, let out, let down, let off
makemake out, make off, make up
give / gogive in, give up; go in, go out, go through

How to prepare prepositions. He points to chapter 3 of "Exploring the World of English" and advises dedicating a few focused hours (two to five days' worth) to it Practice · 49:58. Learn each phrasal verb with its meaning and one example sentence. And his closing instruction: do not learn prepositions just to pass the ten marks. Use them as tools to strengthen every sentence you write across the whole paper, including the précis and translation.

4 Vocabulary

🔤 Pairs of words and grouping

Practice

Two of the vocabulary questions reward the same daily habit: knowing your confusable words cold.

Pairs of Words (10 marks)

You are given ten pairs of confusable words and asked to differentiate five of them, explaining the difference and using each in a sentence. The words are the ones people mix up: the classic "words liable to be confused or misused". Sir Danish points to chapter 2 of "Exploring the World of English", whose title is exactly that, as the source to work through Practice · 51:13.

Grouping of Words (10 marks)

A related task: you are given a set of about 20 words and asked to group them into pairs of similar or opposite meaning. This is the synonyms-and-antonyms work, and in the exam it gets roughly 25 minutes. Answer with clarity, either writing the matched word or, if it is set as a choice, the correct option, and keep moving so time does not run away Time Mgmt Precis · 01:28.

The method that actually works: small and daily. His strongest study advice on the whole paper is here. Do three confusable words (or pairs) a day, which takes only five to seven minutes. Over two months that is 180 to 200 pairs, effectively the whole chapter, learned painlessly Practice · 52:25. The alternative, sitting down to cram six or eight hours of vocabulary at once, does not stick and burns a day you did not have.

Layer a past paper onto it. Alongside the daily three words, solve one past paper's worth of pairs and prepositions each day in the run-up. Over 26 or so days that is five full papers' worth of the exact question types, which is how you turn learned words into fast, confident answers under time Practice · 53:22.

5 Sentence correction

🩹 Sentence correction: the smallest fix

Practice

The sentence-correction question (10 marks) gives you flawed sentences to rewrite correctly. The skill is spotting the grammatical error and fixing it with the minimum change, not rewriting the whole sentence in your own style.

How to prepare it. Sir Danish's advice is volume of practice from a dedicated grammar book with a large sentence-correction section: work steadily through its exercises rather than reading rules passively Practice · 54:24. Correction is a pattern-recognition skill, and the patterns (subject-verb agreement, tense consistency, article use, preposition choice, misplaced modifiers, parallelism) repeat, so the more sentences you correct, the faster you see the fault.

Editorial: on the recommended book. The recording names a grammar book for this practice, but the title is garbled in the audio and I have not verified it, so I am not repeating a possibly-wrong name here. Any standard, well-regarded English grammar workbook with an extensive sentence-correction exercise set serves the purpose; ask Sir Danish directly for the exact title he recommends. The method, steady daily correction practice, matters more than the specific book.

The rule of minimal correction. Change only what is wrong. If a sentence has one agreement error, fix that one word and leave the rest untouched. A full rewrite risks introducing a new error and can lose the mark even if your version is elegant. Identify the fault, make the smallest change that removes it, and stop.

6 The habit

📅 The daily routine that wins these marks

Practice

If there is one idea Sir Danish repeats across the practice class, it is that these sections are won by a little, every day, not by heroic cramming. Here is the routine, pulled together.

Daily (5 to 20 min each)Why
3 pairs of words from chapter 2 of "Exploring the World of English"Builds 180 to 200 in two months; 5 to 7 minutes a day Practice · 52:25
1 past paper's pairs and prepositionsTurns learned words into fast exam answers; 5 papers over ~26 days Practice · 53:22
A page or two of sentence correctionCorrection is pattern practice; volume is what improves it
In blocks (a few hours, once)Why
Chapter 3 (prepositions and phrasal verbs) of "Exploring the World of English"Learn each with its meaning and an example; 2 to 5 days' worth Practice · 49:58
Timed practice from DecemberQuality first now; add the clock once the process is solid Practice · 05:08

The line to hold onto. The whole philosophy in one sentence: make it a daily dose of three pairs, and in two months you will have finished the whole chapter Practice · 52:25. Trying to eat a month's vocabulary in one sitting is like trying to eat a week's food in one meal. Break it into daily portions and it becomes effortless.

The order of preparation across the year. Précis is the deciding factor, so give it the most sustained skill practice. But the grammar and vocabulary sections are the safest 40 to 60 marks on the paper, and they are built by the daily habit above. Do the habit from now, so that by the exam these questions are automatic and your real thinking time goes to the précis and comprehension.

🎴 Flashcards

Give the seven sections of the paper and their marks.

Précis 20, Comprehension 20, Grammar & Vocabulary 20, Sentence Correction 10, Grouping of Words 10, Pairs of Words 10, Translation 10. Total 100, in 3 hours.

How does Sir Danish split the three hours?

About 35 min comprehension, ~1 hour on the short questions (translation, pairs, prepositions, sentence correction), ~50 to 60 min précis, and 5 to 10 min to review.

Why do many candidates do the précis last?

It is the hardest question; finishing the mechanical sections first frees the mind and leaves a clear block of time for it. The answer-sheet order may differ from the printed order.

"at" vs "in" for time?

"at" for a point of time (at four o'clock); "in" for a period (in a few weeks).

"by" vs "with"?

"by" for the agent or doer (and for nearness); "with" for the instrument (and for accompaniment). "I write by hand with a pencil."

"among" vs "between", "beside" vs "besides"?

"among" for three or more, "between" for two. "beside" = next to, "besides" = in addition to.

Why does Sir Danish call prepositions powerful?

Simple words with the right prepositions carry strong meaning; a well-chosen phrasal verb beats a big word. Do not learn them just for the 10 marks, use them everywhere.

What is the vocabulary study routine?

Three confusable words a day (5 to 7 min) from chapter 2 of "Exploring the World of English", building 180 to 200 in two months. Cramming does not stick.

What does the Grouping of Words question ask?

Pair about 20 given words by similar or opposite meaning. It gets roughly 25 minutes; answer clearly and keep to time.

The rule for sentence correction?

Change only what is wrong, with the smallest fix. A full rewrite risks a new error. Prepare by volume of practice.

The 60/40 rule?

Roughly 60% of the outcome is preparation and mindset, 40% is execution once the clock starts. Writing more does not earn more.

X Strategy

🎯 Exam focus

Bank the safe marks first

Sixty of the hundred marks (grammar and vocabulary 20, sentence correction 10, grouping 10, pairs 10, translation 10) are preparation-driven and low-risk. If you have done the daily habit, they are close to guaranteed. Secure them cleanly and quickly so that your scarce thinking time and nerve go to the two skill sections, précis and comprehension.

The whole-paper checklist

  • Know the marks map so you spend time in proportion (do not lavish twenty minutes on a 10-mark grouping question).
  • Comprehension in ~35 min, five tight answers.
  • The short questions in ~1 hour, banked steadily.
  • Précis in the last ~50 to 60 min, with its own internal plan.
  • Keep 5 to 10 minutes to review the whole paper. Never still be drafting at the buzzer.
  • Number your answers correctly if you attempt sections out of order.

The preparation checklist

  • Daily: three confusable words, one past paper's pairs and prepositions, a page of sentence correction.
  • In blocks: the prepositions and phrasal-verbs chapter, learned with meanings and examples.
  • From December: add the clock, full timed papers, without dropping quality.
  • Throughout: use good prepositions and simple, correct words across every section, the précis and translation as much as the grammar question.

The one line to remember. This paper is not won by big vocabulary or long answers. It is won by the right small words used correctly, and a little practice every day. The daily habit turns 60 marks into near-certainties, the time plan gets you to the précis with a clear head, and the whole thing comes down to preparation plus a calm three hours.
