⭐ The five-minute version

  • The class opens by finishing global-warming mitigation (points 6 to 10): promote sustainable industrialisation by installing pollution-control technology, implement international commitments (UNFCCC 1992, Kyoto 1997, Paris 2015), improve disaster management, build environmental governance, and strengthen international cooperation with developed countries leading.
  • Biodiversity is the variety of life on Earth, and it works at three levels: genetic (variation within a species' genetic make-up), species (the number and kinds of organisms in an area), and ecosystem (variation between the habitats organisms live in, since a desert supports life a forest or ocean cannot).
  • It matters because almost everything traces back to it: food, timber, medicine, energy, culture and clothing, environmental services such as pollution control and climate stability, and research.
  • Biodiversity loss is "the declining numbers and kinds of living organisms on earth", driven by 12 causes the lecturer lists, headed by population explosion, over-exploitation, all four kinds of pollution, climate change, invasive species and deforestation.
  • Conservation is a 9-point list, running from controlling over-exploitation and pollution to protected areas, in-situ and ex-situ conservation, awareness, and implementing the Convention on Biological Diversity.
  • Water pollution is any physical, chemical or biological change in water quality that makes it unsuitable for its desired use, and it comes in three types: physical (colour, taste, odour), chemical (nitrates, phosphorus, heavy metals) and biological (bacteria and algal growth).
  • Eutrophication is the headline case: nitrogen and phosphorus wash in, algae bloom across the surface, sunlight can no longer reach the plants below, photosynthesis stops, the bloom then collapses, and bacteria decomposing the dead mass strip the oxygen until the fish suffocate. Learn it as a chain, because that is how it is examined.
  • How to answer any of these. Definition, then causes, then effects, then controls. Every topic in this class is built that way, and the controls should mirror the causes one for one.

2 Definitions

🧬 What biodiversity is

00:14 – 00:32

Start from the word itself: bio means life and diversity means variety, so biodiversity is the variety of life on Earth. It covers plants, animals and microbes, and in each case the point is variety rather than a single species.

Two formal definitions to quote.

  • Global biodiversity strategy: "the totality of genes, numbers and kinds of living organisms on earth".
  • Convention on Biological Diversity: the variation between living organisms, among living organisms, and within the species of living organisms.

Notice that the CBD definition already contains the three levels below, which is why it is the more useful one to open an answer with.

Correction: both definitions as dictated drop the word "ecosystems", and that is a third of the answer. On a definition question this is expensive, so use the real wording:

  • CBD, Article 2 (the one to quote): biological diversity means "the variability among living organisms from all sources… this includes diversity within species, between species and of ecosystems." The class version says "between… among… within", which repeats one idea twice and loses the ecosystem level.
  • Global Biodiversity Strategy: the totality of genes, species and ecosystems, rather than "genes, numbers and kinds".

Both correct versions name all three levels, which sets up the next part of the answer for free.

The three levels of biodiversity

LevelWhat variesThe lecturer's handle on it
1. Genetic diversityThe genetic make-up within a speciesEvery living organism has its own genetic make-up, so each life form carries its own characters.
2. Species diversityThe total numbers and kinds of organisms in a placeIt refers to the total numbers and kinds of living organisms at a particular area or location, and their distribution is uneven.
3. Ecosystem diversityThe habitats themselvesVariation in the ecosystems where the living organisms reside. Each ecosystem has its own environment and can support its own type of life 00:30:41.

The example that fixes ecosystem diversity. What you find in a desert you cannot expect in the sea, and what lives in the sea you cannot expect in a forest 00:31:15. Every ecosystem has its own living conditions and its own suitable environment, so it hosts its own life forms. That is the level people most often forget, and the easiest to illustrate.

3 The value

💚 Why biodiversity matters

00:33 – 00:50

The framing to open with: major aspects of life, the economy, society, culture, education, energy, employment and survival itself, are all directly or indirectly linked with biodiversity. Then give the seven specifics.

Correction: the "70% of medicines from plants" figure. No authoritative source gives a 70/30 plant-to-animal split. Use one of these instead, each defensible:

  • About 25% of modern prescription drugs are derived from higher plants.
  • About half of new small-molecule drugs approved since 1981 are natural products or derived from them.
  • WHO: about 80% of people in developing countries rely on traditional, largely plant-based medicine for primary healthcare. This is the strongest line for a CSS answer, and is probably the figure that became "70%" in class.

The lecturer's underlying point stands completely: medicine depends on biodiversity. Only the number needs replacing.

Turn the values into the effects of loss. The lecturer's own instruction on this slide is to convert the importance into effects. That is a genuinely efficient exam trick: every value listed above becomes a loss when biodiversity declines. Lose biodiversity and you lose food security, medicine, timber, energy, cultural livelihoods and climate stability. Write the list once, then invert it.

Correction: the IUCN bird and mammal figures are reversed. This is the single most-examined biodiversity statistic, and as taught it is wrong in both directions. Birds are the less threatened of the two at roughly 1 in 8; mammals are the more threatened at roughly 1 in 4. The "1/3 of mammals" figure also overstates the real number. If you remember one correction from this page, make it this one.

In-situ against ex-situ, since the terms are always asked. In-situ conservation protects a species in its own natural habitat, which is what national parks and protected areas do. Ex-situ conservation protects it outside that habitat, in botanical gardens, zoos, seed banks and gene banks. The lecturer's list pairs them deliberately: the parks in point 6 are the in-situ arm, the gardens and zoos the ex-situ arm.

How he frames the whole answer 01:05:34: to counter biodiversity loss you apply several means at once, legal means, institutional involvement, better public awareness, and the technical work of developing protected areas and national parks. Presenting conservation as a package rather than a list is what makes it read as policy.

6 The second topic

💧 Water pollution: types & sources

01:35 – 01:50

The definition as dictated: water pollution is "any physical or chemical change in the quality of water which makes water unsuitable for desirable use". The phrase unsuitable for desirable use is doing the work, because water fit for irrigation may still be unfit to drink.

Correction: add "biological" to the definition. The standard wording is any physical, chemical or biological change in water quality that harms living organisms or makes the water unfit for its intended use. Dropping "biological" contradicts the lecture itself, since it immediately goes on to give a biological type of pollution and to list cholera, typhoid and hepatitis. Write all three.

TypeWhat changesSources
1. PhysicalAny physical change in water quality: colour, taste, odour 01:40:37Industrial effluent; municipal sources (domestic waste water from homes); commercial centres and markets 01:40:07
2. ChemicalThe addition of chemicals: nitrates, nitrogen, phosphorus, carbon, acids, and heavy metals (cadmium, arsenic, lead)Industries and chemicals; domestic detergents; oil spills
3. BiologicalThe addition or growth of biological organisms in the water: bacteria, algaeNutrient enrichment. The headline case is eutrophication, the next section

The logic behind the sources. Whether the waste water comes from a home, an industry or a commercial centre, the moment it is added to a nearby water body the natural water changes, and that change is what we classify as pollution 01:40:37. Source and type are therefore separate questions, and a good answer keeps them apart.

7 The chain

🦠 Eutrophication

01:50 – 02:15

Definition: eutrophication is "an excessive plant and algal growth in the water bodies due to the increasing levels of growth factors in the water bodies". It belongs to shallow and stagnant waters, and it is a form of water pollution.

Growth factors and causes. The growth factors are the nutrients: carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, magnesium, potassium, calcium and so on. They arrive through agrochemical and fertiliser remains carried by agricultural water run-off, and through organic waste: plant waste, animal waste and crop waste.

Two precision points that earn marks.

  • Name the two nutrients. "Increasing levels of growth factors" is vague. The drivers are nitrogen and phosphorus. Add the standard refinement: phosphorus is usually the limiting nutrient in freshwater, nitrogen in marine and estuarine water.
  • Classification. The class files eutrophication under biological water pollution. Examiners normally call it nutrient pollution, a chemical enrichment process with biological consequences; "biological pollution" usually means pathogens or invasive species. The safe phrasing keeps both: "nutrient pollution, whose visible effect is biological (an algal bloom)."

The nine-step process

The lecturer builds this as a causal chain on the board, each step causing the next. Reproduce it as a chain and the marks follow:

StepWhat happensWhich causes…
1A fertiliser flows into the water body↓
2Excessive plant and algal growth across the surface↓
3It prevents the penetration of solar heat and sunlight into the water body 02:05:10↓
4Photosynthesis is disturbed in the plants below, because photosynthesis needs sunlight 02:05:37↓
5Food preparation is disturbed in those plants 02:06:01↓
6The smaller green lives die↓
7The dead plants decompose↓
8Oxygen is consumed and CO₂ increases↓
9Suffocation, causing the death of fisheries and aquatic animalsthe end state

Three refinements to the chain, all worth marks. The class version is substantially right, including the two load-bearing steps (the bloom really does block the light, and decomposition really is what consumes the oxygen). Tighten it like this:

  • Name the decomposers. It is aerobic bacterial decomposition that strips the oxygen, not "decomposition" in the abstract.
  • Put the algal die-off first. The dominant oxygen sink is the decay of the algal bloom itself when it collapses, not mainly the shaded plants underneath.
  • Fish die of hypoxia, not CO₂. The killer is dissolved-oxygen depletion. Worth adding that algae also consume oxygen by respiring at night, before the bloom even dies, and that some cyanobacteria release toxins that kill fish directly.

The mark-perfect chain: fertiliser and sewage run-off → nitrogen and phosphorus enrichment → algal bloom → bloom blocks light → submerged plants die → bloom collapses → aerobic bacteria decompose the dead mass → dissolved oxygen crashes → hypoxia and "dead zones" → fish kills.

Why this chain is such a good exam answer. It connects an agricultural practice to a chemical input, a biological response, a physical effect on light, and finally an ecological collapse. If a question asks how farming kills fish, this is the answer, and each arrow is a mark.

8 Damage and response

🚰 Water pollution: effects & controls

02:15 – 02:43

Effects

#EffectDetail
1Harms the water itselfClarity, taste and odour.
2Harms human healthCholera, typhoid, hepatitis from pathogens; renal damage and kidney dysfunction and pain in joints from heavy metals. (The class also lists leukaemia here; see the correction below.)
3Biodiversity lossThe killing of fisheries. This is where the two halves of the class join up.
4Harms the aesthetic valueOf the water bodies themselves.

Correction: leukaemia is not a heavy-metal disease. Leukaemia is classically linked to benzene (an organic solvent) and ionising radiation, not to the metals in polluted water. Arsenic causes skin, bladder and lung cancer rather than leukaemia. Note also that the two symptoms listed separately in class, kidney damage and joint pain, are the same agent: cadmium.

The mapping to memorise instead:

  • Cadmium → renal tubular damage and itai-itai disease (severe bone and joint pain, from cadmium-contaminated irrigation water in Japan's Jinzu River basin).
  • Arsenic → skin lesions and pigmentation changes, skin, bladder and lung cancer.
  • Lead → neurological damage, loss of IQ in children, anaemia.
  • Mercury → Minamata disease (central nervous system damage).
  • Nitrate → methaemoglobinaemia, the "blue-baby syndrome".

Pakistan-specific marks: arsenic in Indus-plain groundwater, and fluoride causing fluorosis. Both are examiner-friendly and locally grounded.

The eight controlling measures

#Measure
1Reducing the use of agrochemicals, and promoting bio-fertilisers such as compost and sludge
2Ensuring solid waste management
3Environmental awareness
4Developing a separate mitigation system for agricultural water run-off
5Installation of waste water treatment plants
6Using micro-organisms to treat waste water, for example sulfur-oxidising bacteria (SOBs) and Effective Micro-organisms (EM)
7Screening of solid waste
8Implementing water-related commitments, legislation and the SDGs

On measure 6, one half is solid and one is contested. Sulfur-oxidising bacteria are well established, used for biological nitrogen removal and for stripping hydrogen sulfide from sewers and biogas. Effective Micro-organisms are real but scientifically contested: the mixture is proprietary and results are inconsistent, so treat EM as a low-cost adjunct rather than a standard process. Anchor any treatment answer on conventional primary, secondary and tertiary treatment, then add these as supplements.

Match the control to the cause. Measures 1 and 4 answer agricultural run-off, which is the trigger of eutrophication. Measures 2 and 7 answer solid waste. Measures 5 and 6 answer the effluent itself. Measures 3 and 8 are the behavioural and legal layer. An answer that pairs them this way reads as a plan rather than a list, and it lets you carry the eutrophication chain straight into the solution.

Reference

📖 Glossary

Biodiversity
The variety of life on Earth. Formally (CBD Article 2), variability among living organisms including diversity within species, between species and of ecosystems.
Genetic diversity
Level 1: variation in the genetic make-up within a species, giving each life form its own characters.
Species diversity
Level 2: the total numbers and kinds of living organisms in a particular area, unevenly distributed.
Ecosystem diversity
Level 3: variation between the ecosystems organisms live in, each supporting its own type of life (desert, sea, forest).
Biodiversity loss
The declining numbers and kinds of living organisms on earth.
In-situ conservation
Protecting a species inside its own natural habitat: national parks and protected areas.
Ex-situ conservation
Protecting a species outside its habitat: botanical gardens, zoos, seed and gene banks.
CBD
The Convention on Biological Diversity, the international agreement whose implementation is the ninth conservation measure.
IUCN
International Union for Conservation of Nature, the source of the threatened-species figures.
Water pollution
Any physical or chemical change in the quality of water that makes it unsuitable for its desirable use.
Physical water pollution
Change in colour, taste or odour, from industrial effluent, municipal and commercial sources.
Chemical water pollution
Addition of nitrates, nitrogen, phosphorus, carbon, acids or heavy metals (cadmium, arsenic, lead), from industry, detergents and oil spills.
Biological water pollution
Addition or growth of biological organisms such as bacteria and algae; eutrophication is the main case.
Eutrophication
Nutrient enrichment (mainly nitrogen and phosphorus) driving excessive algal and plant growth and then oxygen depletion; typical of shallow, stagnant water. Standardly called nutrient pollution.
Growth factors
The nutrients that drive a bloom: carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, magnesium, potassium, calcium.
Agricultural run-off
Water carrying fertiliser and agrochemical remains off farmland into water bodies; the trigger of eutrophication.
SOBs / EM
Sulfur-oxidising bacteria and Effective Micro-organisms, used biologically to treat waste water.
Self-test

🎴 Flashcards

Give mitigation points 6 to 10 for global warming.

6 sustainable industrialisation with pollution-control technology; 7 implementing commitments (UNFCCC 1992, Kyoto 1997, Paris 2015); 8 improving disaster management; 9 environmental governance; 10 international cooperation led by developed countries.

Name the pollution-control technologies listed.

Absorption, adsorption, fabric filters, bio-filters, catalytic reactors, cyclone method and smog towers.

Define biodiversity in one line.

The variety of life on Earth: the totality of genes, numbers and kinds of living organisms.

How does the CBD define it?

Variation between living organisms, among living organisms, and within the species of living organisms.

Name the three levels of biodiversity.

Genetic (within a species' make-up), species (numbers and kinds in an area), and ecosystem (variation between the habitats themselves).

Illustrate ecosystem diversity.

What lives in a desert cannot be expected in the sea, and what lives in the sea cannot be expected in a forest. Each ecosystem has its own conditions and supports its own life.

Give the seven values of biodiversity.

Food, timber products, treatment (medicine), energy, cultural uses, environmental services, and research.

What share of medicines comes from plants?

Do not use the class's 70/30 split. Say about 25% of prescription drugs derive from plants, about half of new drugs since 1981 are natural-product derived, and WHO's 80% figure refers to reliance on traditional medicine in developing countries.

What environmental services does biodiversity provide?

Pollution control by plants and microbes, temperature and climate stability from forests, and sustenance of the food chain.

Define biodiversity loss.

The declining numbers and kinds of living organisms on earth.

List the leading causes of biodiversity loss.

Population explosion, over-exploitation, air/water/land/noise pollution, global warming and climate change, invasion of non-native species, water logging, deforestation, urbanisation, and environmental crime.

Quote the IUCN threat figures (corrected).

About 1 in 8 birds (~12%) and 1 in 4 mammals (~26%) are threatened; the class swapped these. Marine turtles were 6 of 7 threatened, now 5 of 7 after the green turtle's October 2025 downlisting. Crop genetic diversity: ~75% lost 1900-2000, and that is FAO, not IUCN.

What does the IPCC figure say?

40 to 70% of species assessed face significant extinction risk as warming exceeds about 3.5°C (AR4, 2007); 20 to 30% above 1.5 to 2.5°C. Say "species assessed", not "all living organisms".

What fraction of global fisheries is over-exploited?

37.7% are fished at biologically unsustainable levels (FAO, SOFIA 2024). The ~70 to 90% figure applies only to "fully exploited or overexploited" combined.

Distinguish in-situ from ex-situ conservation.

In-situ protects a species in its natural habitat (national parks, protected areas); ex-situ protects it outside (botanical gardens, zoos, seed and gene banks).

Give the conservation measures for biodiversity.

Control over-exploitation and env crime and population growth; promote sustainable use; legal means and institutions; control pollution; control climate change; develop parks and gardens; in-situ and ex-situ conservation; awareness; implement the CBD.

Define water pollution.

Any physical, chemical or biological change in water quality that harms living organisms or makes the water unsuitable for its desirable use. Include all three.

Name the three types of water pollution with an example each.

Physical (colour, taste, odour), chemical (nitrates, phosphorus, heavy metals like cadmium, arsenic, lead), biological (bacteria and algal growth, i.e. eutrophication).

Define eutrophication.

Excessive plant and algal growth in water bodies due to increasing levels of growth factors, typical of shallow, stagnant water.

What are the growth factors and where do they come from?

Carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, magnesium, potassium, calcium; from agrochemical and fertiliser remains in agricultural run-off, and from organic, plant, animal and crop waste.

Run the eutrophication chain.

Fertiliser run-off → nitrogen and phosphorus enrichment → algal bloom → blocks sunlight → submerged plants die → bloom collapses → aerobic bacteria decompose the dead mass → dissolved oxygen crashes → hypoxia and dead zones → fish kills. The killer is oxygen depletion, not CO₂.

Give the effects of water pollution.

Harms water clarity, taste and odour; harms human health (cholera, typhoid, hepatitis from pathogens; cadmium causing kidney damage and itai-itai joint pain); causes biodiversity loss by killing fisheries; harms the aesthetic value of water bodies.

Give the main controlling measures for water pollution.

Cut agrochemicals and promote bio-fertilisers; solid waste management; awareness; a separate system for agricultural run-off; waste water treatment plants; treating water with micro-organisms (SOBs, EM); screening solid waste; and implementing water commitments, legislation and the SDGs.

Before the paper

🎯 Exam focus

  • Use the same four-part skeleton for every topic here: definition, causes, effects, controls. Biodiversity and water pollution are both taught in exactly that shape, so one structure answers either question.
  • Open with a formal definition, not your own words. For biodiversity use the CBD wording, because it already names the three levels and lets you move straight into them.
  • Always give all three levels of biodiversity, and illustrate ecosystem diversity with the desert, sea and forest contrast. Most candidates give genetic and species and stop.
  • Convert the values into the effects of loss. The lecturer says this outright: the seven values become seven consequences when biodiversity declines. It is the cheapest way to double the length of an answer without padding.
  • Quantify with the named sources: the Millennium Ecosystem Assessment, the IPCC 3.5°C figure, the IUCN threat fractions and crop-genetic-diversity loss, and the fisheries figure. Attributing a number to a body is worth more than the number alone.
  • Pair conservation measures to causes. Nine measures against twelve causes maps almost one for one, and showing the mapping is the difference between listing and analysing.
  • Know in-situ against ex-situ cold. It is a standard short-question, and the parks-versus-gardens split makes it easy to remember.
  • The eutrophication chain is the single highest-value thing in this class. Learn all nine steps in order with the causal arrows. It answers questions on water pollution, on agriculture and the environment, and on aquatic biodiversity loss.
  • Keep type and source apart for water pollution. Three types (physical, chemical, biological) and separately the sources (industrial, municipal, commercial, agricultural). Candidates routinely blur them.
