  • Modern law enforcement is preventive and evidence-led. The lecture asks police to supplement conventional investigation with intelligence, technology, partnerships and professional decision-making 00:04:08.
  • Intelligence-led policing is a cycle. Collect information from relevant sources, process and collate it, identify patterns, produce useful intelligence, and disseminate it to the agency able to act 00:07:35. GIS, crime mapping, data analytics, databases and surveillance systems support the cycle.
  • Prediction is an aid, never proof. The lecture describes person-level behavioural prediction and even suggests interrogation or harassment 00:14:56. Correctly framed, analytical output is a lead or risk indicator. Any coercive step still needs lawful grounds, due process, human oversight and protection against bias.
  • Community policing rests on trust. Police and citizens work together through local observation and reporting, consultation, problem-solving and crime-prevention education 00:24:15.   • Public-private cooperation fills capacity gaps. Businesses, technology firms, NGOs and academia can contribute finance, management, technology, victim support and rehabilitation capacity 01:11:04. 1 Model one

🧭 Intelligence-led policing

00:04:08–00:23:33

Intelligence-led policing uses analysed information to guide decisions, investigations, prevention and the allocation of police attention. It supplements conventional police work with a pre-emptive, evidence-led approach 00:05:30.

The lecture begins with a broad proposition: the conventional system is insufficient on its own. Modern crime control calls for professional investigation, technology, partnerships and evidence-based strategies 00:04:08. Intelligence is therefore a decision resource. It can help identify patterns, choose priorities, direct an investigation and intervene before a planned offence occurs.

The intelligence cycle taught in class

StageWhat happensLecture illustration
1. CollectGather relevant information from human, signals, electronic and social sources.A case may draw on people, communications, digital traces or online behaviour 00:07:35.
2. Process and collateOrganise, combine and filter the material so that irrelevant volume does not obscure the problem.A robbery inquiry may need a particular slice of digital surveillance rather than every available record 00:09:13.
3. AnalyseLook for meaningful relationships, movement, plans and recurring patterns.The lecturer proposes examining the movements and plans of potential robbery suspects 00:10:10.
4. DisseminateSend the resulting intelligence to the unit with the jurisdiction and capacity to act.Counter-terrorism or another specialised force receives the relevant product 00:10:59.
5. Act and reviewUse the product for a lawful operational decision, then feed the result back into later collection and analysis.The lecture presents the model as a repeating cycle rather than a one-time search 00:12:40.

The practical distinction. Raw information is material collected from sources. Intelligence is the assessed, filtered and analysed product that can support a decision. The cycle adds value between collection and action.

Tools and data sources

The lecture names GIS, crime mapping, data analytics, artificial intelligence, criminal and identity databases, CCTV, transaction patterns and social-media traces 00:13:42. Used carefully, these tools can reveal geographical concentrations, recurring methods, temporal patterns and associations that a purely reactive inquiry might miss.

Its worked example follows a person previously connected with a terrorist network after release from prison. The proposed system combines travel, shopping, payment and social-media patterns to construct a behavioural model 00:14:56. The example demonstrates the reach of modern data analysis, but it also exposes the model's central legal and ethical danger.

Correction: prediction does not establish guilt or authorise harassment. The lecture says such systems can predict which person will offend, that police may take the person for interrogation or harassment, and that the tools are “very precise” 00:17:22. A model can produce a lead or risk score, not proof that a named person will commit a crime. Historical data may reproduce earlier policing bias and create feedback loops. Any surveillance, stop, questioning, arrest or search still requires a lawful basis, proportionality, human review and due-process safeguards. Harassment is never a legitimate police technique. This correction follows the UN Committee on the Elimination of Racial Discrimination's guidance on algorithmic profiling and the constitutional protections of life and liberty, fair trial, dignity and privacy.

Pakistan illustrations, gains and limits

The lecture connects the model to Counter Terrorism Departments, inter-agency coordination and Safe City projects in Lahore and Islamabad 00:19:21. Its red-alert illustration shows the preventive logic: intelligence about a possible threat may justify proportionate security measures while agencies locate and assess the suspected vehicles or persons 00:20:25.

Potential gainLimit or safeguard
Earlier recognition of patterns and threatsIntelligence can be incomplete, stale or wrong.
Better coordination across agenciesSharing requires clear jurisdiction, security and accountability.
Focused deployment of scarce resourcesWeak digital infrastructure, expertise and budgets constrain the model 00:22:30.
Technology-supported preventionPrivacy, bias, proportionality and human oversight must shape every use.

Exam frame. Define the model, draw the cycle, name the tools, give CTD and Safe City as Pakistan illustrations, then evaluate infrastructure and rights constraints. A diagram earns clarity, but the safeguards earn analysis.

2 Model two

🤝 Community policing

00:24:15–00:31:26

Community policing is a collaborative approach in which police and community members identify local safety problems, exchange information and work on prevention. The lecture calls it “inviting help of the society” 00:24:15.

The model does not turn citizens into police officers. Their role is civic and bounded: observe local conditions, report suspicious activity, join consultations and help address the social conditions linked to crime. Formal police powers remain with authorised officials 00:26:38.

The governing principle is trust. Cooperation depends on citizens believing that information will be handled fairly and that the police will respond lawfully. Without trust, reporting, consultation and joint problem-solving all weaken 00:26:21. The United States Department of Justice likewise describes community policing through community partnerships, problem-solving and organisational transformation.

Three components from the lecture

ComponentCitizen rolePolice or specialist role
Local observation and patrollingWatch the neighbourhood, notice irregular activity and report it.Assess the report and take any authorised operational action 00:27:21.
Advisory committees and consultationRaise recurring problems, give feedback and discuss local priorities.Meet communities at regular intervals and convert concerns into plans 00:27:41.
Crime-prevention educationLearn how risks arise and how collective behaviour can reduce them.Police, criminologists, sociologists and behavioural specialists design credible education 00:28:46.

The lecture links prevention education to theories already studied, including strain, differential association, behavioural and labelling perspectives 00:29:16. That link matters. Community policing is strongest when it responds to the social processes behind local offending rather than treating every problem as a matter for patrol and arrest.

Why the model can help

  • Local knowledge: residents can reveal patterns that central databases miss.
  • Legitimacy: respectful participation can improve confidence in police action 00:37:08.
  • Prevention: communities can identify risks and address lower-level problems before they escalate.
  • Problem-solving: recurring safety concerns can be tackled with agencies, schools, service providers and local groups.

Editorial: do not romanticise the model. Participation can become informal surveillance, vigilantism or selective reporting if roles and safeguards are unclear. A sound answer should specify lawful reporting channels, confidentiality, inclusion, police accountability and protection against discriminatory targeting.

3 Pakistan application

🏘️ Community policing in Pakistan

00:31:26–00:45:22

Pakistan has official and local community-policing initiatives, but the lecture's central question is implementation: why do formal units and committees produce uneven results?

The lecture names the National Police Bureau, community-policing units, community liaison arrangements and what the transcript hears as “Salty Committees” 00:32:01. It describes a local forum that can relay suspicious activity, discuss trends and, for suitable lower-level disputes, support diversion or alternative resolution 00:33:55.

Correction: use the verified institutional wording. The National Police Bureau has introduced a Community Policing Unit (CPU) for policy, training, compliance, data analysis and community engagement. The official page does not establish that NPB created a CPU inside every local police station. Punjab Police pages separately document Masalhati Committees and Citizen Police Liaison Committees in local practice. Write these as related examples, not one uniform nationwide structure.

The lecture's street-level example is useful. Residents notice a group gathering at particular times and associate that pattern with rising petty crime. A formal liaison forum gives them a channel to report the pattern without claiming police powers or confronting the group themselves 00:35:30. Police must still verify the information and avoid treating suspicion as guilt.

Why implementation remains difficult

ChallengeHow it weakens the model
Limited administrative and budget supportCommittees and outreach cannot become routine without staff, time and resources 00:38:47.
Public distrustPerceptions of corruption, abuse of power and weak accountability discourage cooperation 00:39:23.
Political interferenceCitizens doubt that information and cases will be handled impartially 00:41:01.
Inadequate specialist trainingCrime-prevention education needs criminological, sociological and behavioural expertise 00:41:38.
Internal resistanceA reactive organisational culture may resist new roles and shared problem-solving 00:42:50.
Evolving and complex crimeTerrorism, organised crime and technically advanced offences can exceed a neighbourhood forum's capacity 00:44:11.

Correction: avoid the Canada and Switzerland generalisation. The lecture says community policing is smoother there because crimes are less serious 00:44:49. Crime seriousness varies within every country, and no evidence is supplied for this comparison. Use it only as the lecturer's contrast between enabling and difficult environments. A stronger answer analyses trust, institutional capacity, accountability, local crime patterns and programme design.

A balanced Pakistan answer. Move from concept and components to verified initiatives, then test implementation against trust, capacity, political neutrality, training and accountability. Finish with practical reforms: local needs assessments, trained liaison officers, protected reporting, published performance measures and independent complaint mechanisms.

4 Model three

🏗️ Public-private cooperation in law enforcement

01:11:04–01:21:56

The lecture defines a public-private partnership in this context as institutionalised cooperation between law-enforcement bodies and private actors. Unlike community policing, the partner is an organisation rather than an individual resident 01:11:04.

Possible partners include businesses, technology firms, NGOs and academia. They may supply financial resources, managerial expertise, specialist training, technology or service-delivery capacity that a public agency lacks 01:13:40.

Areas of collaboration

AreaPossible contributionControl question
Smart surveillanceSensors, CCTV integration, control-room systems and analytics 01:14:46Who may collect, access, retain and audit the data?
AI-assisted forecastingPattern detection and analytical tools 01:16:23How are bias, explainability and human review handled?
CybersecurityNetworks, specialist tools and technical expertiseWhat security, secrecy and procurement standards apply?
Victim supportNGO-run counselling, shelter, legal referral and case support 01:16:56Are confidentiality, safeguarding and service quality enforceable?
RehabilitationFacilities, education, treatment and reintegration programmesWho is accountable for rights and outcomes?

The lecture presents the Punjab Safe Cities Authority and Lahore's command-and-control, CCTV analytics and communication infrastructure as its main Pakistan example 01:18:25. It also points students to the Public Private Partnership Authority, or P3A, to understand how formal PPP models are structured 01:20:18.

Correction: separate a public authority, procurement and a formal PPP. PSCA is a statutory public body under the Punjab Safe Cities Authority Act, 2016. A private firm supplying technology through procurement or a joint technical arrangement does not, by that fact alone, prove that the project is a legally structured PPP. The federal Public Private Partnership Authority Act, 2017 establishes P3A to facilitate federal PPP transactions. Cite PSCA as a technology-enabled public-safety example unless the particular project's formal PPP documents are available.

Evaluation checklist

  • Public purpose: the partnership must answer a defined safety or justice need.
  • Legal authority: powers that belong to the state cannot be transferred casually.
  • Value and capacity: test whether the partner adds finance, expertise or delivery capacity that the agency needs.
  • Accountability: specify procurement, audit, complaint, data-protection and performance rules.
  • Rights: privacy, equality, due process and access to remedy remain public responsibilities.
5 Rights framework

🛡️ Human rights and vulnerable groups

01:23:47–01:48:48

A vulnerable group is a set of people whose circumstances expose them to a heightened risk of discrimination, exploitation or victimisation. The lecture names age, gender, disability, socioeconomic position, minority status, ethnicity and displacement as relevant factors 01:32:23.

Modern criminology studies these risks because the same justice process can affect groups differently. The lecture uses displaced communities to show how ethnicity, religion and outsider status may increase exposure to hate crime or exploitation 01:34:38. Protection therefore requires both equal legal status and procedures responsive to particular needs.

Constitutional base

The lecture identifies Articles 14 and 25 as constitutional safeguards 01:37:14. Article 14 protects dignity and, subject to law, privacy of the home. Article 25 establishes equality before law and equal protection of law and prohibits discrimination on the basis of sex, while allowing special provision for women and children.

Editorial: use precise provisions for minorities. The lecture correctly links minority protection to religious freedom and non-discrimination 01:44:22. For an exact constitutional answer, add Article 20 on professing, practising and propagating religion and managing religious institutions; Article 22 on safeguards in educational institutions concerning religion; and Article 36, a Principle of Policy on protection of minorities. Do not describe every one of these provisions as an individually enforceable fundamental right.

Children: Juvenile Justice System Act, 2018

The lecture revises the juvenile framework rather than reteaching it in full 01:38:09. The Act defines a child as a person who has not attained eighteen years, provides for legal assistance at state expense, creates juvenile courts, and establishes diversion as an alternative to formal judicial proceedings in eligible cases. Its stated orientation includes social reintegration.

FeatureExam-ready meaning
ChildA person below eighteen for purposes of the Act.
Juvenile courtA specialised forum for juvenile cases rather than ordinary treatment without adjustment.
DiversionAn alternative process that determines responsibility and treatment without formal judicial proceedings, subject to the Act.
Legal assistanceThe juvenile has a statutory entitlement to legal assistance at state expense.
ReintegrationThe response should consider the child's development and return to society, not merely custody.

Women: exact legal and institutional references

ServicesThe lecture mentions helplines, shelters and the authority; official PWPA services include the Violence Against Women Center in Multan and helpline support 01:42:38.

Correction: repair abbreviated or garbled law names. “Harassment of Women at Workplace Act 2010” is shorthand; use the full title above. The lecture's “pretext of Honor Act” is an amendment Act with the full title above. The student interjection transcribed as “Asset Control Act” is too unclear to identify reliably, so it is not asserted here.

Law on paper and protection in practice

The lecturer's analytical assignment is essential: after identifying legislation and institutions, assess their effectiveness using current evidence on gender-based violence and children's experience of the justice system 01:46:33. Passing a law and creating an authority do not establish implementation. A critical answer tests awareness, accessibility, police response, investigation, prosecution, shelter capacity, geographic coverage, budgets, monitoring and outcomes.

Question plan. Define vulnerability, identify the constitutional base, organise the answer by children, women and minorities, name exact laws, then evaluate the implementation gap with current evidence. Keep the legal description separate from the empirical verdict.

6 Specialized agency one

🔎 Federal Investigation Agency

01:48:50–02:06:53

The Federal Investigation Agency (FIA) is a federal investigative body under the Ministry of Interior. The lecture treats it as the priority specialised-agency case because FIA questions recur and its legal structure supplies a model for studying other bodies 01:48:50.

Legal basis and command

The legal foundation is the Federal Investigation Agency Act, 1974. The official text identifies it as Act VIII of 1975, published in the Gazette on 17 January 1975. Section 3 authorises the Federal Government to constitute the Agency for inquiry into and investigation of offences specified in the Schedule. A Director-General appointed by the Federal Government administers it under the Federal Government's superintendence 01:50:35.

The lecture also names the Federal Investigation Agency (Inquiries and Investigations) Rules, 2002, which address procedures for inquiries and investigations 01:53:23.

Correction: the Act does define FIA's core work and powers. The lecture says the Act creates the body and grants powers but “has not defined its work” 01:53:23. Section 3 expressly ties FIA to inquiry and investigation of scheduled offences; section 5 grants officers specified police powers for those functions; and the Schedule identifies covered laws and offences. The 2002 Rules add procedural detail rather than supplying an otherwise absent mandate.

Mandate and functional areas

The lecture organises FIA by wings so that students can convert an organisational chart into functions 01:58:05. The current official FIA site lists Anti-Corruption, Anti-Money Laundering, Technical, Anti-Human Trafficking and Smuggling, Integrated Border Management System, Intellectual Property Rights, Immigration, Interpol and FIA Academy. It separately lists counter-terrorism among matters investigated.

AreaCore workBoundary to remember
Anti-CorruptionFederal corruption matters, especially involving federal public servants and federal funds.Do not describe all private-sector corruption as FIA Anti-Corruption Wing jurisdiction merely because a private actor is involved 01:58:22.
Anti-Money LaunderingInvestigation within the federal statutory mandate relating to money laundering and linked financial offences.State the enabling law or scheduled offence when analysing jurisdiction.
Technical and digital forensicsTechnical support and forensic examination for investigations 02:01:40.Current PECA inquiry, investigation and prosecution authority belongs to NCCIA.
Anti-Human Trafficking and SmugglingHuman trafficking, migrant smuggling and unlawful migration networks.“Smuggling” here does not turn the wing into the federal drug-enforcement agency.
ImmigrationBorder and immigration controls, including action on false travel documents and unlawful migration 02:03:03.Apply written law and procedure; suspicion alone is insufficient.
INTERPOL / NCBPakistan's national contact and coordination function with INTERPOL 02:04:16.International cooperation does not replace domestic legal process.
Counter-terrorismInvestigation of relevant federal and scheduled offences and terrorism-financing matters.Coordinate with other competent federal and provincial bodies.

Correction: three current jurisdiction points. First, FIA's official Anti-Corruption Wing focuses on corruption involving federal public servants and federal funds, so the lecture's broad “public and private sectors” phrasing needs a statutory link. Second, the lecture includes drug smuggling under the Anti-Human Trafficking and Smuggling Wing 02:02:17; that wing addresses trafficking in persons and smuggling of migrants, while narcotics enforcement is associated with the Anti Narcotics Force. Third, under section 29 of the Prevention of Electronic Crimes Act, 2016 as amended in 2025, the National Cyber Crime Investigation Agency (NCCIA) is the authorised agency for PECA inquiries, investigations and prosecutions. FIA can retain technical-forensic capabilities without being described as the current general PECA investigation agency.

Why FIA is specialised

Ordinary territorial policing covers a broad range of local crime and order functions. FIA is specialised because federal legislation assigns it particular scheduled offences, cross-border and federal domains, and technical investigative roles 02:05:27. The best answer therefore uses legal mandate and jurisdiction, not the mere existence of wings, to explain specialisation.

FIA answer sequence. Federal Investigation Agency Act, 1974; Ministry of Interior and DG; scheduled-offence mandate; 2002 Inquiry and Investigation Rules; functional areas; present jurisdictional updates such as NCCIA; then problems, coordination and reforms. A current answer must distinguish FIA's historical cybercrime role from the 2025 PECA position.

7 Specialized agency two

🏛️ National Accountability Bureau

02:07:13–02:18:10

The National Accountability Bureau (NAB) was established in 1999 under the National Accountability Ordinance, 1999. Its core functions are investigation, prosecution and prevention of corruption and corrupt practices 02:07:47.

Offence areas highlighted in the lecture

  • Corruption and corrupt practices.
  • Misuse or abuse of authority.
  • Embezzlement and misappropriation.
  • Kickbacks and illegal gratification.
  • Assets beyond known means.
  • Cheating the public at large, money laundering and major financial misconduct where the current law gives NAB jurisdiction.

The lecture groups these as large or “mega-corruption” matters affecting the public exchequer 02:11:21. The current official NAB description also identifies government institutions, public-office holders, private schemes, housing schemes and public-at-large fraud within its statutory work.

Correction: do not use a timeless FIA-versus-NAB monetary threshold. The lecture says FIA has no limit while NAB has one 02:00:08. The National Accountability Ordinance has been amended repeatedly, including major changes in 2022 and 2023, and jurisdiction depends on the current statutory text, the alleged conduct, the persons and property involved, and exclusions in section 4. State the current legal basis for the case instead of memorising a threshold from an undated lecture.

The operational sequence

The lecture gives a simple cycle: inquiry, investigation, then a reference filed in an Accountability Court where the allegation must be proved 02:13:10. This is useful as a study scaffold, but it needs one front-end qualification.

Editorial: treat the cycle as simplified. NAB's current public material describes complaint receipt, screening and complaint verification before a matter advances. Its 2023–24 report records a revised complaint-handling and verification framework, while the Ordinance governs inquiries, investigations, prosecution and Accountability Courts. A safe sequence for an exam is: complaint and screening → verification where applicable → inquiry → investigation → reference and prosecution before the Accountability Court. Each transition requires the competent authorisation under the current law and rules.

Criticisms raised in class

Lecture criticismWhat a critical answer must ask

These are the lecturer's analytical claims, not self-proving conclusions. A good answer must support them with current judicial decisions, official statistics, audit material or well-sourced empirical research. “Controversial” is not an argument unless the mechanism and evidence are explained.

ANF boundary. The lecturer assigns the Anti Narcotics Force for independent study and provides no substantive ANF content 02:18:39. This note does not invent it. For a separate ANF question, research its legal cover, mandate, organisation, investigation and prosecution roles, coordination problems and current evidence afresh.

8 Course close

✍️ How to study and answer these topics

02:19:13–02:35:42

The final discussion turns course content into answer technique. The key instruction is to read the command word, identify the dimensions demanded, and organise the response so an examiner can see the argument immediately.

Build knowledge in layers

The lecturer identifies three course sources: the spoken lectures, the supplied notes, and case studies or reading material 02:21:00. Larry Siegel is suggested as optional foundation-building for earlier sections, rather than a substitute for the course package 02:22:00.

Criminology answers need a theoretical perspective where relevant because the paper asks about crime, criminal behaviour and scientific explanation, not a lawyer's bare recital of statutes 02:24:19. Pakistan references become essential when the question calls for a local application.

Decode the command word

CommandWhat to doCommon failure
Describe / explainSet out the meaning, structure, mechanism and significance clearly.Listing labels without explaining how they connect.
AnalyseBreak the topic into parts and examine the relationship among them 02:27:24.Repeating a narrative without identifying components or causes.
EvaluateJudge performance or worth against stated criteria, with merits and limitations 02:26:51.Giving balanced-looking lists but no reasoned judgment.
Critically analyseAnalyse through evidence, test competing explanations and reach a qualified conclusion 02:27:53.Using “criticism” as opinion without evidence.

Structure before detail

Headings are essential because they let the examiner scan the answer's logic. For FIA, for example, use legal landscape, mandate, major wings or functions, operational issues and reforms 02:29:33. Paragraphs should carry the explanation. Bullets are suitable for compact groups of examples, but an entire 20-mark answer should not become bullet fragments 02:33:39.

Correction: avoid a universal claim about first-time offenders. The lecture says empirical evidence shows first-time offenders are rehabilitated 02:25:50. Outcomes vary by person, offence, programme, supervision and social conditions. Use labelling and behavioural theories to justify considering diversion and rehabilitation, then cite specific evidence for the population and intervention being discussed.

Reusable specialised-agency outline. (1) definition and legal cover, (2) formation and administrative control, (3) mandate and jurisdiction, (4) functions or wings, (5) process, (6) contributions, (7) problems supported by evidence, (8) reforms tied to those problems, and (9) a judgment answering the exact command.

Course-map correction. The opening slide labels the material “Section 3,” but the lecturer immediately corrects it to Section 4, contemporary or modern criminology 00:03:08. Use Section 4 in revision notes.

F Recall

🎴 Flashcards

What is intelligence-led policing?

A decision model in which collected and analysed intelligence guides investigation, prevention, priorities and resource allocation.

Name the stages of the intelligence cycle.

Collect; process, collate and filter; analyse patterns; disseminate; act lawfully; review and feed results back.

Name five intelligence-led policing tools or sources.

GIS, crime mapping, data analytics, CCTV, databases, financial transactions, social-media traces and human intelligence.

Why is a predictive score not proof?

It estimates risk from data that can be incomplete or biased. Coercive action still needs lawful grounds, evidence, proportionality and human review.

What are the three core components of community policing in this lecture?

Local observation and reporting, advisory consultation, and crime-prevention education.

What principle makes community policing possible?

Trust between police and the community, reinforced by respectful, lawful and accountable conduct.

What is the verified NPB institution?

The National Police Bureau's Community Policing Unit, which works on policy, training, compliance, data analysis and engagement. It is not proof of a CPU at every station.

What are Masalhati Committees?

Local conciliation or liaison committees documented on Punjab Police district sites and linked to community policing and suitable non-cognisable disputes.

List six obstacles to community policing in Pakistan.

Resource gaps, distrust, political interference, inadequate specialist training, internal resistance and complex or evolving crime.

Who can participate in public-private cooperation?

Public law-enforcement bodies with businesses, technology firms, NGOs, academia and other capable private organisations.

Why is PSCA not automatically proof of a formal PPP?

PSCA is a statutory public authority. Buying technology or using contractors does not by itself establish a legally structured PPP for a particular project.

What factors can create vulnerability?

Age, gender, disability, socioeconomic status, minority status, ethnicity and displacement.

Which constitutional articles anchor this lecture's rights discussion?

Articles 14 and 25 for dignity, privacy and equality; Articles 20 and 22 for religious and educational safeguards; Article 36 as a Principle of Policy on minorities.

How does JJSA 2018 define a child?

A person who has not attained eighteen years.

What is diversion under JJSA 2018?

An alternative process for responsibility and treatment without resort to formal judicial proceedings, subject to the Act.

Give the exact federal workplace-harassment law title.

Protection against Harassment of Women at the Workplace Act, 2010, amended in 2022.

Give the exact 2016 honour-offences law title.

Criminal Law (Amendment) (Offences in the Name or on pretext of Honour) Act, 2016, Act XLIII of 2016.

What is FIA's legal basis?

Federal Investigation Agency Act, 1974, Act VIII of 1975, read with its Schedule and the 2002 Inquiry and Investigation Rules.

What does section 3 of the FIA Act do?

It authorises the Federal Government to constitute FIA for inquiry into and investigation of offences specified in the Schedule.

Who appoints FIA's Director-General?

The Federal Government.

What current cybercrime update must an FIA answer include?

Under PECA as amended in 2025, NCCIA is the authorised agency for PECA inquiries, investigations and prosecutions.

Why is “drug smuggling” misplaced under FIA's AHTS Wing?

That wing addresses human trafficking and smuggling of migrants. Federal narcotics enforcement belongs principally to ANF under its own legal framework.

What is NAB's legal basis?

National Accountability Ordinance, 1999, as amended.

Give the simplified NAB case sequence.

Complaint and screening, verification where applicable, inquiry, investigation, then an authorised reference and prosecution in the Accountability Court.

Name three critiques of NAB raised in class.

Selective accountability, long delays and disputed effectiveness. Each requires current evidence in an exam answer.

What does “critically analyse” require?

Break the topic into parts, test them through evidence, consider competing interpretations and reach a qualified judgment.

Common mistakes

  • Treating an algorithmic prediction as evidence that a person will offend.
  • Claiming NPB established a community-policing unit in every police station.
  • Calling any technology procurement a formal PPP.
  • Using inaccurate law titles or listing old FIA wings without NCCIA's current PECA mandate.
  • Putting drug smuggling under FIA's human-trafficking and migrant-smuggling wing.
  • Reciting a fixed NAB threshold without checking the current amended Ordinance.
  • Repeating critiques such as “selective accountability” without evidence or criteria.

The synthesis line. Modern law enforcement gains reach from intelligence, communities and specialised partners, but legal authority, evidence, accountability and human dignity determine whether that reach becomes legitimate prevention or unchecked power.
