Global Environmental Agreements: Stockholm to Paris

The international regime in one place: the vocabulary of treaties and protocols, Stockholm 1972, the Rio Earth Summit, the ozone regime from Montreal to Kigali, the Kyoto Protocol and why it fell short, and the change of approach at Paris.

One storyline runs through all of it: the world moves from declarations, to binding targets that nobody meets, to voluntary pledges that everybody signs. 🌍

⭐ The five-minute version

  • Get the vocabulary right first. Treaty is the umbrella term; a convention is a formal multilateral treaty setting the framework, and a protocol is a further treaty adding substantive obligations under it. The classic pair: UNFCCC is the framework convention, the Kyoto Protocol is its protocol.
  • Stockholm 1972 is where international environmental politics begins: the first global conference to treat the environment as a principal issue, and the moment the world first agreed the environment was a shared problem.
  • Rio 1992, the Earth Summit, is the hinge. Its organising concept was sustainable development, and it opened the UNFCCC for signature, the parent of everything that follows on climate.
  • The ozone regime is the success story. The Montreal Protocol (1987) phased out CFCs, and the Kigali Amendment (2016) extended it to phase down HFCs, which do not harm ozone but many of which have a global warming potential in the thousands. That is how an ozone treaty became a climate treaty.
  • Kyoto 1997 tried binding targets and fell short. It gave developed countries real reduction commitments plus three market mechanisms (emissions trading, CDM, joint implementation), but excluded major developing emitters, lost key participants, and global emissions still rose.
  • Paris 2015 changed the approach. Adopted at COP21, it abandoned imposed targets in favour of pledges every country sets itself, with no sanctions for missing them, aiming to hold warming well below 2°C and ideally to 1.5°C.
  • The essay in one line: the world traded enforceability for participation. Kyoto was binding but narrow; Paris is near-universal but voluntary. Whether that trade works is the question examiners want you to argue.
1 The vocabulary

📜 Treaty, protocol, convention & MEAs

lecture 1

Before any of the agreements, get the words right, because a question can turn on the difference between them.

TreatyProtocolConvention
DefinitionAn international agreement between two parties, members or even nationsA document that allows alterations and amendments to the main treatySomething that happens prior to a treaty being formed; it can also mean a treaty among a number of countries
Legally bindingYesYesYes
ExampleGlobal Plan of Action to Combat Trafficking in PersonsKyoto ProtocolGeneva Convention

Correction: the table above is the lecture's simplification, and two parts of it are wrong. Use this instead, because definition questions are common and the class version reads as untrained.

  • "Treaty" is the umbrella term, not one category of three. Under the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (1969), a treaty is an international agreement in written form governed by international law, "whatever its particular designation." Conventions and protocols are treaties.
  • A convention is not "something that happens prior to a treaty." That confuses the diplomatic conference with the instrument. A convention is a formal multilateral treaty, usually negotiated under the auspices of an international organisation.
  • A protocol does more than amend. In environmental law the important kind is the framework-treaty protocol, which adds new substantive obligations. Kyoto did not amend the UNFCCC; it added obligations under it.
  • The "Legally binding: Yes" row is the real error. Binding force never comes from the label. It comes from an intention to create legal obligations, a state's consent to be bound (ratification or accession), and entry into force. The UN's own guidance is that the title of an instrument has normally no overriding legal effect.

Correction: the treaty example is not a treaty. The Global Plan of Action to Combat Trafficking in Persons is a UN General Assembly resolution (A/RES/64/293, 2010) and is non-binding. The binding instrument on trafficking is the Palermo Protocol (2000), supplementing the UN Convention against Transnational Organized Crime. The Kyoto and Geneva examples are apt.

How a state actually becomes bound. The sequence taught in class: signing → ratification → publication and parliamentary approval → notification. Signing alone does not bind a state; ratification is the step that does. This is exactly why the United States could sign an agreement and still never be bound by it, which is precisely what happened with Kyoto.

Multilateral Environmental Agreements (MEAs)

An MEA is a legally binding instrument between two or more nations about some aspect of the environment. The class divides them four ways:

TypeWhat it isExample
Global MEASigned by many countriesThe Convention on Biological Diversity; the UNFCCC
Bilateral agreementAn agreement between two statesArgentina and Chile; and for Pakistan, the Indus Waters Treaty
Regional MEABased on a specific geographical regionRegional seas and river-basin agreements
Framework MEASets broad objectives and then adds protocols to set standards and firm requirementsUNFCCC signed 1992, its Kyoto Protocol opened 1997

Correction: "multilateral" means three or more. An MEA is an agreement among three or more states, so a bilateral agreement is not a type of MEA at all. Keep the Indus Waters Treaty as an example of a bilateral environmental agreement, but do not file it under MEAs. UNEP's own split is simply global and regional. "Framework" is a description of an agreement's architecture, not its geographic scope, so it sits on a different axis from the other three.

Currency note: India declared the Indus Waters Treaty "in abeyance" in April 2025 and it remains suspended, though the Court of Arbitration has held the treaty contains no provision allowing unilateral abeyance.

The one relationship to be able to draw. UNFCCC is the framework convention: it states the objective but sets no hard targets. Kyoto is the protocol that puts numbers on it. Paris is the later agreement that replaces Kyoto's method. If you can lay that chain out in a sentence, most questions on the climate regime become straightforward.

2 The beginning

🇸🇪 Stockholm 1972

lecture 3.4

The United Nations Conference on the Human Environment, held in Stockholm, Sweden, in 1972. In the course's four-phase history of the environmental movement, it is the culmination of the second phase, roughly 1960 to 1972.

Why it matters, in the lecturer's own terms.

  • It was the first conference at global level at which the world reached a consensus that the environment was a serious problem needing to be addressed.
  • It was the first conference to discuss environmental issues principally, rather than as a side matter.
  • It laid the foundation for the later environmental movement and for international environmental action.
  • The line to quote: it "marked a turning point in the development of international environmental politics".

The detail the class leaves out, and it is all free marks.

  • Exact dates: 5 to 16 June 1972, with 113 states attending.
  • Theme: "Only One Earth".
  • Outputs: the Stockholm Declaration (26 principles) and an Action Plan (109 recommendations).
  • UNEP was not created at the conference itself. It was established later that year by UN General Assembly resolution 2997, in December 1972. Saying "Stockholm created UNEP" is a common slip.
  • 5 June is marked as World Environment Day because of this conference.

How to use it. Stockholm is your opening paragraph in any question on international environmental cooperation. It establishes the starting point, so that Rio, Kyoto and Paris can be presented as a developing sequence rather than a list of conferences. The single most useful fact is that before 1972 there was no global forum treating the environment as a subject in its own right.

3 The hinge

🌎 Rio 1992: the Earth Summit

lecture 5

The United Nations Conference on Environment and Development (UNCED), better known as the Earth Summit or the Rio Conference, held in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil.

DetailValue
Dates3 to 14 June 1992
Host governmentBrazil
Governments participating172, of which 108 were represented at head of state or government level
Secretary-General of the conferenceMaurice F. Strong (Canada), who had also led Stockholm 1972 and was UNEP's first Executive Director
Organising conceptSustainable development, integrating environmental protection with economic and social development
NGO presenceApproximately 2,400 NGO representatives, with about 17,000 at the parallel '92 Global Forum

The output that matters most. The United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) was opened for signature at Rio. It is the parent instrument of the entire climate regime: Kyoto is its protocol and Paris is negotiated under it. If you remember one thing about Rio, remember that it produced the UNFCCC.

Precision: the UNFCCC was adopted on 9 May 1992 in New York and then opened for signature at Rio on 4 June 1992; it entered into force on 21 March 1994. Say "opened for signature at Rio" rather than "adopted at Rio".

Rio's full output, which is worth listing. Alongside the UNFCCC: the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD), also opened for signature there; the Rio Declaration (27 principles); Agenda 21, the non-binding action programme; and the Forest Principles.

A trap worth knowing: the UNCCD (desertification) is often listed as a third "Rio Convention" signed at Rio. It was not. Rio only mandated it through Agenda 21; the UNCCD was adopted in Paris in 1994.

Why Rio is the hinge of the whole answer. Stockholm established that the environment mattered; Rio fused it with development. The phrase "sustainable development" moves from theory into the centre of international policy here, which is why Rio, not Stockholm, is the conference that generates binding machinery.

4 The success story

🕳️ The ozone regime: Montreal & Kigali

lecture 4

The ozone treaties are the one part of the international regime that plainly worked, and the lecturer treats the Kigali Amendment as the distinctive point most textbooks miss.

The evolution in one sentence. The Montreal Protocol (1987) no longer deals with ozone depletion alone: through Kigali it now also addresses climate change. An ozone treaty grew into a climate instrument.

The dates to have ready: Vienna Convention adopted 1985, in force 1988; Montreal Protocol adopted 16 September 1987, in force 1 January 1989; Kigali Amendment adopted 15 October 2016, in force 1 January 2019. Pakistan ratified Kigali on 22 October 2025, so any older source listing Pakistan as outstanding is out of date.

Precision on a popular claim: it was the Vienna Convention and the Montreal Protocol together that became the first set of treaties in UN history to achieve universal ratification, in 2009. Saying "the Montreal Protocol was the first universally ratified treaty" on its own is loose.

Why the amendment was needed: the CFC to HFC trap

The logic is a chain, and it is the best part of this topic:

  1. CFCs were the original ozone-depleting substances, used across major industries.
  2. Montreal phased them out, and industry replaced them with HFCs (hydrofluorocarbons).
  3. HFCs solved the ozone problem: they do not deplete ozone.
  4. But many HFCs have a global warming potential in the thousands, so the ozone fix created a climate problem.
  5. Hence Kigali (2016): phase down HFCs by cutting their production and consumption.

Global warming potential (GWP), the concept that makes the argument work. GWP measures a gas's warming effect against a reference, and the reference is carbon dioxide, whose GWP is 1. Methane is 28 on the current reporting standard (IPCC AR5); the class's 25 is the older AR4 figure. HFCs run into the thousands, which is precisely why a gas that is harmless to ozone still had to be restricted.

The Kigali target as taught: a reduction of over 80% in HFC consumption by 2047, by cutting production and consumption on an agreed schedule.

Correction, and this one hands Pakistani candidates a free mark. There is no single global deadline. Kigali sets four different tracks, each cutting to 80 to 85% below its own baseline, and 2047 is the endpoint for only one of them.

GroupFreezeFinal cut
Developed countriesFirst step 201985% by 2036
Belarus, Russia, Kazakhstan and othersFirst step 202085% by 2036
Developing (Article 5) Group 1202480% by 2045
Developing (Article 5) Group 2, including Pakistan, India, Iran, Iraq and the Gulf states202885% by 2047

Write it as: Kigali phases HFCs down by 80 to 85% of baseline between 2036 and 2047 depending on the country group, and Pakistan sits in Article 5 Group 2, so its own schedule is a freeze in 2028 and an 85% cut by 2047. Naming Pakistan's own track is exactly the kind of specific that lifts an answer.

Two further points: it is a phase-down to a plateau, not a phase-out to zero (unlike CFCs), and Kigali is projected to avoid up to about 0.5°C of warming by 2100.

Why this is a high-value example. It gives you a rare success to set against Kyoto's failure, and it demonstrates a sophisticated point: environmental solutions can create new environmental problems, and good regimes are the ones that can adapt. Use Montreal-to-Kigali whenever a question asks whether international environmental cooperation can work.

5 Binding targets

🏭 The Kyoto Protocol

lectures 8.2 & 8.3

The UNFCCC set an objective but gave little direction, so the Kyoto Protocol was created to supply it: real, numerical emission-reduction commitments for developed countries. It was adopted on 11 December 1997 but only entered into force on 16 February 2005, because it needed ratification by 55 parties accounting for 55% of developed-country 1990 emissions, a threshold crossed only when Russia ratified in 2004.

The targets, precisely. First commitment period 2008 to 2012: an average cut of 5% against 1990 levels (the Annex B table works out to 5.2%, which is why both numbers circulate). Second period 2013 to 2020 under the Doha Amendment: 18% below 1990, but for a much smaller group of parties, and that amendment only entered into force on 31 December 2020, essentially the day the period it governed expired.

The three flexibility mechanisms

MechanismIdea
Emissions tradingCountries under their limit can sell surplus allowance to those over it, so reductions happen where they are cheapest.
Clean Development Mechanism (CDM)A developed country earns credit by funding an emission-reducing project in a developing country.
Joint implementationCredit earned through emission-reduction projects carried out jointly between developed countries.

The lecturer's figure on how uneven the CDM became: roughly 80% of CDM projects concentrated in China and India. The verified share of registered projects is closer to 70% (China about half, India about a fifth), and the sharper equity point is that Africa received only about 2 to 3%.

The shortcomings

  • Exclusion of major developing emitters. The definition of "developing" rested on per-capita income, so China, the world's biggest carbon emitter, and India, among the ten most polluting countries, carried no reduction obligation. This is the central criticism.
  • CBDR used as a shield. Common but differentiated responsibilities and respective capabilities (CBDR-RC) is a fair principle, but the lecturer argues states used it as a "scapegoat" to avoid action, citing Brazil and the Amazon fires of 2019.
  • Loss of key participants. The class states that the United States and Canada stepped away around the second commitment period (2012 to 2020). See the correction below for the precise position.
  • Developed nations were not serious about the commitments they had accepted.

Correction: the United States and Canada left in different ways. The class treats them together. In fact the United States signed but never ratified Kyoto, so it was never legally bound at all, while Canada ratified in 2002 and then formally withdrew, announcing it in December 2011 with effect from December 2012, which is the end of the first commitment period, not during the second. Canada is the only state ever to withdraw from Kyoto. Japan, Russia and New Zealand simply declined second-period targets without withdrawing, a further distinction examiners reward. Say: the US never ratified; Canada ratified and withdrew; others opted out of the second period.

Was Kyoto a success or a failure?

The lecturer poses this directly as an exam question and answers it with evidence on both sides:

For successFor failure
Developed-country (Annex I) emissions by 2012 came in far below the 5% target, on the order of 24% below 1990 levels.But most of that fall came from the post-Soviet economic collapse, not climate policy, the so-called "hot air" problem. Meanwhile global emissions rose substantially over the same period, so the treaty did not bend the world curve.
It created the first binding architecture and the market mechanisms that later regimes reuse.The biggest emitters were either excluded or absent, and developed states did not take it seriously.

How to answer the "success or failure" question. Concede the achievement (a first binding framework, real if modest reductions, durable mechanisms), then argue the verdict on the outcome: emissions rose, the largest emitters were outside the obligations, and participation eroded. The judgement to land on is that Kyoto failed in outcome but succeeded as a precedent, and that its failure is precisely what forced the redesign at Paris.

6 The redesign

🇫🇷 The Paris Agreement

lecture 9

By 2011 there was a consensus that a new treaty was needed by 2020, the year Kyoto's second commitment period ended. That negotiation produced the Paris Climate Accord, adopted in December 2015 at COP21, the 21st Conference of the Parties to the UNFCCC.

The temperature goal. Hold the rise in average global temperature by the end of the century to no more than 2°C, and ideally to 1.5°C. Those two numbers are the most quotable facts in the entire climate regime.

The change of approach

This is the analytical heart of the topic. Kyoto imposed targets from above; Paris asks each country to set its own and reports on progress, and it imposes no sanctions on countries that fall short.

Kyoto Protocol (1997)Paris Agreement (2015)
Who has targetsDeveloped countries onlyEffectively all parties
How targets are setNegotiated and imposed from aboveNationally determined by each country
EnforcementBinding commitmentsBinding to have and report a target, not to meet it; compliance body is non-punitive
ParticipationNarrow, and it erodedNear universal, adopted by 196 parties
Central goalPercentage cuts against 1990 levelsHold warming below 2°C, ideally 1.5°C
DatesAdopted 1997, in force 2005, periods to 2020Adopted Dec 2015, in force 4 November 2016, implementation from 2020

Bring it up to date, because the lectures stop in 2015 and a 2026 paper will not.

  • The United States is now outside the Paris Agreement, and is leaving the UNFCCC too. It withdrew from Paris once (effective 2020), rejoined in 2021, and withdrew again with effect from 27 January 2026. It has since gone further and notified withdrawal from the UNFCCC itself, effective 27 February 2027, which would make it the first state ever to leave the Convention. So the accurate line today is: out of Paris, still a Convention party until February 2027. Paris now has 194 parties, against 198 for the UNFCCC.
  • COP28 (Dubai, 2023) concluded the first Global Stocktake, with the landmark call for "transitioning away from fossil fuels" (note the wording: never "phase out"), plus tripling renewables by 2030. The Loss and Damage Fund was operationalised on the opening day.
  • COP29 (Baku, 2024) set a new climate finance goal of at least $300 billion a year by 2035, within a wider $1.3 trillion call, and finalised the Article 6 carbon-market rules, completing the Paris rulebook.
  • COP30 (Belém, 2025) is the one most guides will miss. Despite a push by more than 80 countries, the fossil-fuel roadmap was excluded from the final text entirely; the agreed outcomes were a Global Implementation Accelerator, a "Belém Mission to 1.5", and a call to at least triple adaptation finance by 2035.
  • COP31 will be in Antalya, Türkiye, in November 2026, under an unprecedented split arrangement in which Türkiye holds the presidency and Australia leads the negotiations. Guides still calling it "a Pacific COP hosted by Australia" are out of date.
  • The ICJ's advisory opinion of 23 July 2025 held unanimously that states' climate obligations arise under customary international law, not only the treaties, and that a breach is an internationally wrongful act giving rise to reparations. Two consequences worth stating: leaving Paris does not release a state from those obligations, and the Court treated the Glasgow and Dubai decisions as making 1.5°C the operative goal. This is the single most citable recent development.

The argument to make, and it is the best essay line in this chapter. Paris solved Kyoto's participation problem and created an enforcement problem. Kyoto was binding on a few; Paris is voluntary for nearly all. Whether swapping compulsion for near-universal buy-in was the right trade is exactly the debate an examiner wants argued rather than described, and you can answer it either way if you use the Kyoto evidence (emissions rose 17% under binding targets) to support your case.

Reference

📖 Glossary

Treaty
A binding international agreement between parties or nations.
Protocol
An instrument attached to an existing treaty that adds detail, standards or obligations. The Kyoto Protocol sits under the UNFCCC.
Convention
A broad framework agreement, often preceding or containing a treaty among many countries.
Ratification
The step by which a state actually becomes bound, after signing and domestic approval. Signing alone does not bind.
MEA
Multilateral Environmental Agreement: a legally binding instrument between two or more nations on an aspect of the environment.
Framework MEA
An agreement that sets objectives and then adds protocols to fix standards. UNFCCC (1992) with the Kyoto Protocol (1997) is the model.
Stockholm Conference 1972
UN Conference on the Human Environment; the first global conference to treat the environment as a principal issue.
Earth Summit 1992
UN Conference on Environment and Development, Rio de Janeiro, 3 to 14 June 1992; theme environment and sustainable development; opened the UNFCCC for signature.
UNFCCC
United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change, the parent instrument of the climate regime.
Montreal Protocol (1987)
The ozone treaty that phased out CFCs, later extended to climate through Kigali.
Kigali Amendment (2016)
Amends Montreal to phase HFCs down by 80 to 85% of baseline between 2036 and 2047, on four group schedules. Pakistan (Article 5 Group 2) freezes in 2028 and cuts 85% by 2047.
CFCs / HFCs
Chlorofluorocarbons deplete ozone. Hydrofluorocarbons replaced them, do not deplete ozone, but have very high global warming potential.
Global warming potential (GWP)
A gas's warming effect relative to carbon dioxide, which is 1. Methane is 28 (IPCC AR5 reporting standard; 25 under the older AR4). Many HFCs run into the thousands, though some are in the hundreds.
Kyoto Protocol (1997)
The protocol that put binding reduction targets on developed countries under the UNFCCC.
Emissions trading
Kyoto mechanism allowing countries under their limit to sell surplus allowance.
CDM
Clean Development Mechanism: a developed country earns credit by funding emission reductions in a developing country.
Joint implementation
Kyoto mechanism for credit from joint projects between developed countries.
CBDR-RC
Common but differentiated responsibilities and respective capabilities. From Rio Declaration Principle 7 (1992) and UNFCCC Articles 3.1 and 4.1; Paris Article 2.2 adds "in the light of different national circumstances", the softening that let Paris be universal. Use the bare "CBDR" for Rio Principle 7 and Kyoto Article 10, but "CBDR-RC" whenever you cite UNFCCC Article 3.1 or the Paris Agreement.
Paris Agreement (2015)
Adopted at COP21 by 196 parties, in force 4 November 2016; nationally determined contributions (NDCs), no penalty for missing them, holding warming well below 2°C and pursuing 1.5°C.
COP
Conference of the Parties, the annual decision-making meeting of the UNFCCC. Paris was COP21.
Self-test

🎴 Flashcards

Distinguish treaty, protocol and convention.

A convention is the broad framework agreement; a treaty is the binding agreement between parties; a protocol attaches to an existing treaty to add detail and obligations. UNFCCC is the convention, Kyoto its protocol.

How does a state actually become bound?

Signing, then ratification, then domestic publication and parliamentary approval, then notification. Signing alone does not bind.

Define an MEA and name its four types.

A legally binding instrument between two or more nations on an aspect of the environment. Global, bilateral, regional and framework MEAs.

Give the framework-MEA example.

UNFCCC, signed 1992, which then adopted the Kyoto Protocol in 1997 to set firm requirements.

What was the Stockholm Conference and why does it matter?

The 1972 UN Conference on the Human Environment in Sweden: the first global conference to treat the environment as a principal issue, marking a turning point in international environmental politics.

Give the Rio Earth Summit's key details.

UN Conference on Environment and Development, Rio de Janeiro, 3 to 14 June 1992, hosted by Brazil, 172 governments, Secretary-General Maurice F. Strong, theme environment and sustainable development.

What was the NGO presence at Rio?

About 2,400 NGO representatives, with 17,000 attending the parallel NGO forum.

What is Rio's most important output?

The UNFCCC was opened for signature there, making Rio the parent event of the entire climate regime.

How did an ozone treaty become a climate treaty?

Montreal (1987) phased out CFCs; industry switched to HFCs, which spare ozone but have GWP in the thousands; the Kigali Amendment (2016) therefore phases HFCs down.

What is the Kigali target?

A phase-down of 80 to 85% below baseline, between 2036 and 2047 depending on country group. Pakistan is in Article 5 Group 2: freeze in 2028, 85% cut by 2047.

Explain global warming potential with numbers.

GWP measures warming against carbon dioxide = 1. Methane is 28 on the current AR5 reporting standard (25 under the older AR4). Many HFCs run into the thousands, though some are only in the hundreds.

Name Kyoto's three flexibility mechanisms.

Emissions trading, the Clean Development Mechanism (CDM), and joint implementation.

What was uneven about the CDM?

About 70% of registered projects went to China and India (the class says 80%), while Africa received only around 2 to 3%.

Give Kyoto's central criticism.

It excluded major developing emitters: China, the largest carbon emitter, and India, among the most polluting, carried no reduction obligation.

How did the US and Canada differ on Kyoto?

The United States signed but never ratified, so was never bound. Canada ratified and then formally withdrew, announced in 2011. Do not lump them together.

Was Kyoto a success or a failure?

Annex I emissions fell far below the 5% target (around 24% below 1990), but largely through post-Soviet economic collapse rather than policy, the "hot air" critique, while global emissions rose. Verdict: it failed in outcome while succeeding as a precedent, and its failure forced the Paris redesign.

What is CBDR-RC and what is the criticism of it?

Common but differentiated responsibilities and respective capabilities: shared but unequal obligation, from Rio Principle 7 and UNFCCC Arts 3.1/4.1. The criticism is that states use it as a shield to avoid acting, as with Brazil and the 2019 Amazon fires.

When and where was Paris adopted?

December 2015, at COP21, the 21st Conference of the Parties to the UNFCCC, in Paris, adopted by 196 parties.

State the Paris temperature goal.

Hold the rise in average global temperature to no more than 2°C, and ideally to 1.5°C.

What is the key difference in approach between Kyoto and Paris?

Kyoto imposed binding targets on developed countries; Paris lets each country set its own NDC. Precisely: Paris binds parties to prepare, communicate and report a target, but not to achieve it, and its compliance body is facilitative and non-punitive.

Before the paper

🎯 Exam focus

  • Tell it as one story, not a list of conferences. Stockholm establishes the problem, Rio fuses environment with development and produces the UNFCCC, Kyoto tries binding targets and falls short, Paris trades enforceability for participation. That arc is the answer.
  • Get the instrument vocabulary right in the first paragraph. Convention, treaty, protocol, and the UNFCCC-Kyoto relationship. It signals command of the subject immediately.
  • Use Montreal and Kigali as your counter-example. Almost every candidate says international cooperation fails. The ozone regime proves it can work, and Kigali proves a regime can adapt when its own solution creates a new problem.
  • Quantify wherever possible: Rio's 172 governments and 3 to 14 June 1992, Kigali's 80% by 2047, GWP of 1 for CO₂ against thousands for HFCs, Kyoto's 1 billion tonnes against a 17% rise, Paris's 196 parties and 2°C / 1.5°C.
  • Answer "was Kyoto a success" with a judgement, not a shrug. Concede the precedent and the mechanisms, then rule on the outcome. Examiners reward a defended verdict.
  • Do not conflate the US and Canada on Kyoto. The US never ratified; Canada ratified and withdrew. The distinction is a discriminator.
  • Close on the real question: was it right to swap binding targets for universal participation? Argue it either way, but argue it. That is where the highest marks sit.
