⭐ The five-minute version

  • EIA informs a project decision before commitment. Screening, scoping, baseline, prediction and alternatives lead to mitigation, an EMP, reporting and follow-up.
  • Mitigation has an order and the authority decides. Avoid first, then minimize or restore, and address residual effects. The assessment supplies evidence; the authority grants, refuses or conditions approval.
  • SEA works at a strategic tier. It examines policies, plans or programmes and broader alternatives, while EIA usually examines a defined project.
1 Purpose and timing

🔎 Define EIA and place it early

8.4 · 00:00:27-08:29

The lecturer describes environmental impact assessment as an advance analysis of a proposed project or development 8.4 · 00:01:26. The assessment asks what the proposal could do to the environment and to connected socio-economic, cultural and human-health conditions.

Object of assessment

A proposed project or development, including realistic design, location and no-project alternatives.

Direction of effect

Both beneficial and adverse effects belong in the analysis 8.4 · 00:02:51.

Decision point

Assessment begins while avoidance, mitigation and alternative choices remain available 8.4 · 00:06:04.

Decision use

The proponent and approving agencies should consider environmental consequences before permission 8.4 · 00:07:30.

Correction: EIA neither began at a single 1960 moment nor belongs after commitment. The lecture gives both claims 8.4 · 00:04:42 8.4 · 00:01:26. A major US formal milestone was NEPA, enacted in 1969 and signed on 1 January 1970. Assessment should occur before approval and major design choices are locked.

2 What the process must achieve

3 Choose the assessment level

🚪 Screen the proposal

8.5 · 00:04:15-07:20

Screening asks whether assessment is required and what level of study fits the proposal. The answer depends on the applicable law or safeguard system, the project type, scale, location and likely significance of effects.

Editorial screening framework. The class names screening and the EIA-IEE choice. The checklist below expands that step using verified assessment practice so the classroom label can be applied in an answer.

Proposal characteristics

Type, size, technology, resource use, wastes and accident risk.

Location sensitivity

Protected areas, settlements, water bodies, cultural sites and other vulnerable receptors.

Likely effects

Magnitude, extent, duration, reversibility and cumulative setting.

Screening outcome

No further study, an initial examination, a full EIA, or another assessment route defined by the governing system.

Correction: an IEE is not chosen because damage is already visible. The lecture describes an initial environmental examination as the route where repercussions are visually present and details already exist 8.5 · 00:05:43. An IEE is a preliminary assessment used in some systems for a lower-risk or category-based proposal. Exact IEE and EIA thresholds are jurisdiction-specific.

Correction: six to eighteen months is not a universal EIA duration. The lecturer offers that range as a general rule 8.5 · 00:04:15. Duration depends on legal timelines, project complexity, seasonal baseline needs, data gaps, consultation, redesign and review. Use a stated range only when a named system or project source supports it.

Baseline means the before-project condition

The lecturer moves from TORs and literature review to background information and data collection 8.5 · 00:07:42. A useful baseline describes the environmental and social conditions against which predicted change will be measured. It should match the scoped issues, not collect every available fact.

Editorial baseline checklist: organise relevant physical conditions; habitats, species and seasons; health, livelihoods, land use and heritage; then label every dataset by source, date, method, coverage, season and limitation.

5 From evidence to choice

🧠 Predict impacts and test alternatives

8.5 · 00:08:11-11:59

Impact analysis connects a project action with a changed receptor, predicts the character of that change and evaluates its significance. The lecture then places alternatives beside the prediction so design can respond before the report is final.

Impact test from classQuestionDecision use
Beneficial or adverseImprove or degrade?Record both directions 8.5 · 00:08:11.
Reversible or irreversibleCan function recover?Irreversibility favours avoidance 8.5 · 00:09:08.
Temporary or permanentHow long?Duration shapes significance.
Magnitude and feasibilityHow large, and can controls work?Test technical and financial credibility 8.5 · 00:10:09.

Alternatives are the heart of prevention

The lecture moves to alternatives after impact prediction 8.5 · 00:09:45. Compare options with the same criteria so a preferred design is not protected by unequal evidence.

Editorial checklist: make the class's sound-and-feasible comparison concrete through these option types.

  • Location: can another site or route avoid a receptor?
  • Design and technology: can layout or controls reduce impact?
  • Scale and timing: would a smaller footprint or another season change risk?
  • No-project: what happens if the proposal does not proceed?

Correction: financial feasibility does not replace environmental acceptability. The lecture tests mitigation cost 8.5 · 00:10:09. Law, residual harm and enforceable controls remain separate decision tests.

7 The strategic tier

🌐 Move upstream with SEA

8.7 · 00:00:31-05:45

Strategic environmental assessment asks environmental questions while a policy, plan or programme is being prepared. Its broader tier can influence the framework within which later projects are selected and assessed.

1

Screen the strategic proposal

Identify whether the subject is sectoral, regional or national and whether SEA applies 8.7 · 00:00:31 8.7 · 00:01:00.

2

Scope the assessment

Set physical and strategic limits, key issues, reasonable alternatives and the decision context.

3

Build the strategic baseline

Describe current conditions and trends relevant to the plan or programme 8.7 · 00:01:59.

4

Compare options and measures

Test broad scenarios and define measures for sustainability and environmental compliance 8.7 · 00:02:28.

5

Consult stakeholders

Use agency and public input before the strategic decision is settled.

6

Decide, implement and follow up

Carry recommendations into the policy, plan or programme and its later course of action 8.7 · 00:02:57 8.7 · 00:03:24.

Correction: SEA is an upstream tier, not a guaranteed chronological predecessor. The lecturer says SEA precedes EIA 8.7 · 00:04:49. Ideally, SEA shapes policies, plans or programmes before project choices are fixed, and later EIAs assess specific projects. Whether both exist and how they connect depends on the legal and planning system.

🃏 Flashcards

What does EIA assess?

Likely beneficial and adverse effects of a proposed project or development before a competent decision.

Why must EIA start early?

So alternatives, avoidance, mitigation and the no-project option can still influence design and approval.

What is screening?

Determining whether assessment is required and which level or instrument applies.

Is an IEE triggered because damage is already visible?

No. It is a preliminary assessment route whose thresholds depend on the governing system.

What is scoping?

Defining significant issues, boundaries, alternatives, methods, stakeholders and TORs.

What is a baseline?

The relevant environmental and social condition before the proposal, used as the reference for prediction.

Which impact pairs does the lecture stress?

Beneficial or adverse, reversible or irreversible, and temporary or permanent.

What alternatives should EIA compare?

Location, design, technology, scale, timing and the no-project option.

What comes first in the mitigation hierarchy?

Avoidance.

When are compensation or offsets considered?

For significant residual effects after feasible avoidance, minimisation and restoration.

What makes an EMP enforceable?

Specific actions, locations, timing, responsibility, indicators, thresholds, budget, monitoring and corrective action.

Does an EIA approve a project?

No. It informs the competent authority, which approves, refuses or sets conditions.

Is six to eighteen months a universal EIA duration?

No. Timing depends on the project, rules, data needs, seasons, consultation and review.

Where can public participation occur?

At scoping, report review and other stages required by the applicable process; it is a cross-cutting function.

What does SEA assess?

Policies, plans or programmes at a strategic tier.

How do SEA alternatives differ?

They can compare broader scenarios and development directions before individual projects are fixed.

Does SEA always occur before EIA?

It is ideally upstream, but the legal system determines whether and how the tiers connect.

May SEA require a formal report?

Yes. Some legal SEA systems require an environmental report, consultation, decision information and monitoring.

Can project EIA assess cumulative effects?

Yes. SEA is well suited to the strategic scale, but many EIA systems also require cumulative analysis.

What is the safest EIA-SEA distinction?

EIA usually addresses a project; SEA addresses a policy, plan or programme.

Who can prepare or fund assessments?

Arrangements vary by jurisdiction; responsibility alone is not the durable distinction between EIA and SEA.

What is the assessment chain?

Screen, scope, baseline, predict, compare alternatives, mitigate, plan, report, review, decide and follow up.

Corrections to remember

  • Use NEPA 1969, signed 1 January 1970, as a major formal milestone; avoid a single 1960 launch claim.
  • IEE thresholds are system-specific, and an EIA report informs rather than grants approval.
  • Replace "balance" with the mitigation hierarchy and exclude the universal six-to-eighteen-month claim.
  • Treat participation as cross-cutting, with exact legal stages set by the governing framework.
  • SEA is upstream by decision tier; documentation, funding and independence vary by jurisdiction.
  • Both project EIA and SEA can examine cumulative effects at their appropriate scales.
