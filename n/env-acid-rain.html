⭐ The five-minute version

  • What it is. Acid rain is a mixture of deposited material, both wet and dry, coming from the atmosphere with more than the normal amount of nitric and sulfuric acid. The term was coined in 1852 by the Scottish chemist Robert Angus Smith, the "father of acid rain".
  • The two forms. Wet deposition falls as rain, snow, sleet, fog or mist. Dry deposition falls as dry particles, dust and smoke that stick to the ground and buildings, and it accounts for about half of the acidic pollutants that come down.
  • The chemistry. Two gases start it: sulfur oxides (SOₓ) and nitrogen oxides (NOₓ). In the air they oxidise and dissolve in water: SOₓ + H₂O → H₂SO₄ (sulfuric acid) and NOₓ + H₂O → HNO₃ (nitric acid).
  • Where the gases come from. A little is natural (volcanic eruptions and lightning), but about 95% is anthropogenic: burning fossil fuels in power stations, factories and vehicles.
  • The damage. It leaches nutrients out of soil, corrodes limestone buildings (the Taj Mahal, the Statue of Liberty), acidifies lakes until fish and frogs cannot breed, and harms human lungs and hearts.
  • How to stop it. Trap the gases before they escape (scrubbers remove about 95% of SO₂; catalytic converters break down NOₓ), switch to clean energy, and repair damaged lakes by liming.
  • How to answer it. Name the two acids and their two source gases, split causes into natural and man-made, then pair every kind of damage with a matching control. Quantify with pH: normal rain is 5.6, acid rain far lower.
1 Definition

🌧️ What acid rain is

lecture 6.1

The single sentence to memorise, straight from the slide: acid rain is a mixture of deposited material, both wet and dry, coming from the atmosphere containing more than normal amounts of nitric and sulfuric acid 6.1 · 04:18.

Acid rain sits at the boundary of air pollution and water pollution. It belongs to Module 6 (pollution), which splits into land, water and air pollution, and acid rain is really an amalgamation of the air and aquatic sides 6.1 · 00:58. The name has a history worth a mark: the term was coined in 1852 by the Scottish chemist Robert Angus Smith, whom the Royal Society of Chemistry calls the father of acid rain 6.1 · 01:58.

Editorial context worth adding. Smith coined the term around 1852 during his work in London, but his landmark study, the book Air and Rain: The Beginnings of a Chemical Climatology, appeared in 1872. If you meet the date 1872 elsewhere, that is the book, not the coining. Either way, Robert Angus Smith is the name and "father of acid rain" is the phrase examiners want.

How acidic is "acidic"? The pH scale

Acidity is measured on the pH scale, and the rule to hold onto is that a lower pH means more acidic. Distilled water is a neutral 7; concentrated lemon juice is a strongly acidic 2 Acid Deposition · 02:50.

SubstancepHMeaning
Lemon juice2Strongly acidic
Acid rain (US, 2000)as low as 4.2Well below normal
Normal rainwater5.6Slightly acidic by nature
Distilled water7Neutral

Why even normal rain is slightly acidic. Rainwater is naturally around pH 5.6, not 7, because carbon dioxide dissolves into it to form carbonic acid, the same weak acid that is in fizzy drinks Acid Deposition · 03:16. "Acid rain" means rain pushed well below that natural baseline. In the US in 2000, rainwater acidity dropped as low as 4.2.

2 The two forms

💧 Wet & dry deposition

lecture 6.1

Acid rain does not only mean rainfall. It comes down in two forms, and naming both is the difference between a weak answer and a full one 6.1 · 06:17.

FormHow it fallsKey detail
Wet depositionRain, snow, sleet, fog or mistThe wind carries the acids to wet areas, where they fall in precipitation and are removed from the atmosphere onto the earth's surface.
Dry depositionDry particles, dust and smokeWhere the weather is dry, the acidic pollutants slip into dust and smoke, fall as dry particles, and stick to the ground, cars, houses, trees and buildings. Almost 50% of the acidic pollutants come down this way.

Reading the diagram

The slide traces the full path. Two gases, sulfur dioxide (SO₂) and nitrogen oxides (NOₓ), rise from the ground. In the atmosphere they oxidise and combine with water vapour to become sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) and nitric acid (HNO₃). Those acids break into ions (2H⁺ + SO₄²⁻, H⁺ + NO₃⁻) 6.1 · 07:49 and return to earth as wet deposition in precipitation or dry deposition as particles 6.1 · 02:58.

The broader term: acid deposition. "Acid rain" is really only one part of acid deposition, which also covers acid snow, acid hail and acid fog Acid Deposition · 00:05. Using "acid deposition" in an answer shows you know rain is not the only carrier.

3 Where it comes from

🏭 What causes it

lecture 6.2

Every environmental problem has two kinds of cause, natural and anthropogenic (man-made), and acid rain is the textbook case 6.2 · 00:30. Both work by releasing the same two gases, which then form the same two acids.

The two reactions, from the slide. This is the chemistry to reproduce exactly:

  • SOₓ + H₂O → H₂SO₄ (sulfur oxides give sulfuric acid)
  • NOₓ + H₂O → HNO₃ (nitrogen oxides give nitric acid)
TypeSourcesWhat they emit
NaturalVolcanic eruptions; lightning strikesVolcanoes emit large amounts of SOₓ and NOₓ (with an emphasis on the sulfur oxides). Lightning produces nitric oxide, which reacts with water to form nitric acid.
Anthropogenic (man-made)Power stations; factories; automobiles and transportBurning fossil fuels / hydrocarbons (coal, oil, gas, petrol, diesel) releases sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxides. This is the dominant source.

Put a number on "dominant". The supplementary material is blunt about the split: anthropogenic sources cause about 95% of all acid deposition, and they produce nearly seven times the sulfur dioxide of natural sources. In the US, the EPA attributes roughly two-thirds of all SO₂ and one-quarter of all NOₓ to electricity generation that burns fossil fuels Acid Deposition · 01:12. Quoting these separates a strong answer from a vague one.

A neat divide worth stating. Coal burning largely accounts for the sulfur emissions, while the nitrogen oxides come mostly from fossil-fuel combustion in vehicles 6.5 · 00:57. That split is exactly why the two controls in section 5 are different devices: scrubbers for sulfur, catalytic converters for nitrogen.

4 The damage

🏚️ The damage it does

lectures 6.4 · Acid Deposition

Acid rain is acidic by nature, so whatever it touches takes on that acidity 6.4 · 00:31. The damage falls into four areas.

1. Soil: leaching of nutrients

Acid rain lowers the soil's pH and damages the microbes and biological activity that make soil fertile 6.4 · 00:31. The deeper harm is leaching. Soil holds vital nutrient ions, calcium (Ca²⁺), magnesium (Mg²⁺) and sodium (Na⁺). The acid's hydrogen ions (H⁺) are less reactive than those nutrients, so through ion exchange they displace them, and the freed nutrients wash away. The soil loses its nutrient value, and crops and vegetation suffer 6.4 · 03:23.

2. Stone and buildings: corrosion

Acid rain corrodes buildings made of limestone, which is calcium carbonate (CaCO₃). The reaction to memorise 6.4 · 06:54:

CaCO₃ + H₂SO₄ → CaSO₄ + H₂O + CO₂

The solid limestone (CaCO₃) turns into calcium sulfate (CaSO₄), a crumbling residue, plus water and carbon dioxide. The stone corrodes and its surface is eaten away. The slide lists the victims as modern buildings, cars, airplanes, steel bridges and pipes, plus three landmarks: the Statue of Liberty, St Paul's Cathedral in London and the Taj Mahal. The lecturer dwells on two of them, the Taj Mahal, whose white marble is yellowing and whose surface is degrading 6.4 · 07:23, and the Statue of Liberty 6.4 · 07:53.

3. Water: acidified lakes and dying life

When acid falls into a lake, the lake's pH drops, and every plant and animal can tolerate only so much acidity Acid Deposition · 03:16. The lecture's vivid cases:

  • The spring peeper, a small tree frog, develops normally at pH 7, but at pH 4 its embryo coils abnormally and fails to hatch.
  • The lake trout becomes thin and weak at pH 5.1 and ceases to produce young Acid Deposition · 03:47.

The timing makes it worse: acid snow builds up over winter, and when it melts in spring it releases all its trapped acid into lakes and streams at once Acid Deposition · 02:13.

4. Human health

Acid deposition degrades visibility, which can cause accidents, and the dry-deposited gases, nitrogen oxides and sulfur dioxide, cause serious health problems when inhaled, including lung and heart problems such as bronchitis and asthma 6.4 · public health slide.

5 The controls

🛠️ How we fix it

lecture 6.5

The solutions follow the causes. Because the gases come from burning fuel, most controls either trap the gases before they escape or stop making them in the first place 6.5 · 00:28.

1. Clean up exhaust pipes and smokestacks

Two different devices, because sulfur and nitrogen need different treatment:

DeviceWhereWhat it does
Scrubbers (flue gas desulfurization, FGD)Industrial smokestacksChemically strip SO₂ from the gases leaving the stack, so cleaner air is released. Efficiency is about 95% of SO₂ removed 6.5 · 02:26.
Catalytic convertersAutomobilesNeutralise NOₓ, breaking it down into harmless nitrogen and oxygen, and convert carbon monoxide to CO₂. No acid-forming gas is released 6.5 · 02:56.

2. Switch to clean energy

If the fuel does not burn, the gases are never made. The lecture's alternative energy sources are wind, geothermal, solar, hydropower and nuclear, and fuel cells, natural gas and batteries can substitute for fossil fuels as cleaner options 6.5 · alt-energy slide.

3. Restore the damage: liming

For lakes and rivers already acidified, there is a repair: liming, adding limestone or lime to the water to neutralise and balance the acidity 6.5 · restoration slide. It uses the same reaction that corrodes buildings, run deliberately: the alkaline lime consumes the acid.

The clean way to structure a solutions answer. Three tiers, from source to cleanup: capture the gases (scrubbers for SO₂, catalytic converters for NOₓ), prevent them by switching to clean energy, and restore what is already damaged by liming. Pair each with the cause or effect it addresses, and the answer looks engineered rather than listed.

Reference

📖 Glossary

Acid rain
A mixture of deposited material, wet and dry, from the atmosphere, with more than normal amounts of nitric and sulfuric acid. Coined 1852 by Robert Angus Smith.
Acid deposition
The broader term: acid rain plus acid snow, acid hail and acid fog. Rain is only one carrier.
Wet deposition
Acids falling in precipitation, as rain, snow, sleet, fog or mist.
Dry deposition
Acids falling as dry particles, dust and smoke that stick to surfaces; about 50% of the acidic pollutants.
Sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄)
Formed when sulfur oxides (SOₓ) meet water vapour. The sulfur half of acid rain.
Nitric acid (HNO₃)
Formed when nitrogen oxides (NOₓ) meet water vapour. The nitrogen half of acid rain.
SOₓ / NOₓ
Sulfur oxides and nitrogen oxides, the two gases that become the two acids.
Anthropogenic
Caused by human activity. About 95% of acid deposition is anthropogenic, mainly fossil-fuel burning.
pH scale
The measure of acidity: lower is more acidic. Neutral water is 7; normal rain is 5.6; acid rain is well below.
Leaching
Acid H⁺ ions displacing soil nutrients (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Na⁺) by ion exchange, so the nutrients wash away and soil loses fertility.
Limestone (CaCO₃)
Calcium carbonate, the building stone that acid rain corrodes into calcium sulfate.
Scrubber (flue gas desulfurization)
A smokestack device that chemically removes about 95% of SO₂ from industrial exhaust.
Catalytic converter
A device on vehicles that breaks NOₓ down into harmless nitrogen and oxygen.
Liming
Adding limestone or lime to an acidified lake or river to neutralise the acidity.
Self-test

🎴 Flashcards

Define acid rain.

A mixture of deposited material, wet and dry, from the atmosphere, containing more than normal amounts of nitric and sulfuric acid.

Who coined the term, and when?

The Scottish chemist Robert Angus Smith, in 1852. He is called the father of acid rain (landmark book, 1872).

What are the two acids in acid rain?

Sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) and nitric acid (HNO₃).

Give the two forming reactions.

SOₓ + H₂O → H₂SO₄, and NOₓ + H₂O → HNO₃.

Name and distinguish the two forms of deposition.

Wet deposition falls as rain, snow, sleet, fog or mist. Dry deposition falls as dry particles and dust that stick to surfaces, about 50% of the total.

What is the broader term than acid rain?

Acid deposition, which also includes acid snow, acid hail and acid fog.

What is the pH of normal rain, and why is it not 7?

About 5.6. CO₂ dissolves into rainwater to form carbonic acid, making even clean rain slightly acidic.

Split the causes of acid rain.

Natural (volcanic eruptions emit SOₓ/NOₓ; lightning makes nitric oxide) and anthropogenic (burning fossil fuels in power stations, factories and vehicles).

How dominant are man-made sources?

About 95% of acid deposition; roughly 7 times the SO₂ of natural sources. In the US, ~2/3 of SO₂ and ~1/4 of NOₓ come from fossil-fuel power generation.

How does acid rain damage soil?

Leaching: acid H⁺ ions displace nutrient ions (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Na⁺) by ion exchange, the nutrients wash away, and the soil loses fertility. It also lowers pH and harms soil microbes.

Give the reaction that corrodes limestone buildings.

CaCO₃ + H₂SO₄ → CaSO₄ + H₂O + CO₂. Solid limestone becomes crumbling calcium sulfate.

Which famous monuments are cited as victims?

The Taj Mahal (marble yellowing and degrading), the Statue of Liberty, and St Paul's Cathedral in London.

How does acid rain harm aquatic life?

It acidifies lakes: the spring peeper frog's embryo coils and fails to hatch at pH 4, and lake trout become thin and stop breeding at pH 5.1.

What are the human-health effects?

Reduced visibility causing accidents, and inhaled NOₓ/SO₂ causing lung and heart problems such as bronchitis and asthma.

What do scrubbers do, and how well?

Flue gas desulfurization removes about 95% of SO₂ from industrial smokestack gases.

What do catalytic converters do?

On vehicles, they break NOₓ down into harmless nitrogen and oxygen (and convert CO to CO₂), so no acid-forming gas is released.

Why are scrubbers and catalytic converters different devices?

Sulfur emissions come mainly from coal burning (scrubbers), nitrogen oxides mainly from vehicle combustion (catalytic converters).

Name the clean-energy alternatives given.

Wind, geothermal, solar, hydropower and nuclear, plus fuel cells, natural gas and batteries.

How do you repair an already-acidified lake?

Liming: adding limestone or lime to neutralise and balance the acidity.

Before the paper

🎯 Exam focus

  • Anchor everything on the two acids. Sulfuric (from SOₓ) and nitric (from NOₓ). Write both reactions, SOₓ + H₂O → H₂SO₄ and NOₓ + H₂O → HNO₃, and the rest of the answer hangs off them.
  • Always give both forms of deposition, wet and dry, and note dry deposition is about half the total. Candidates who only say "acidic rainfall" miss marks.
  • Split causes cleanly into natural and anthropogenic, then quantify: ~95% anthropogenic, ~7 times the natural SO₂, and the EPA's 2/3 SO₂ and 1/4 NOₓ from power generation.
  • Cover all four kinds of damage, and give the mechanism, not just the label: leaching of soil nutrients by ion exchange, CaCO₃ + H₂SO₄ corrosion of stone, lake acidification killing fish and frogs, and respiratory and cardiac harm.
  • Keep the CaCO₃ equation and a monument ready. The Taj Mahal or the Statue of Liberty makes the stone-damage point concrete and memorable.
  • Structure solutions in three tiers: capture (scrubbers 95% SO₂, catalytic converters for NOₓ), prevent (clean energy), restore (liming). Match each control to a specific cause or effect.
  • Use pH numbers for precision: neutral 7, normal rain 5.6 (carbonic acid), acid rain down to 4.2, aquatic life failing at pH 4 to 5.1.
