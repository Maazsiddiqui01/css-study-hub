Accountability & the Public Policy Cycle

Who watches the power-holders, and how policy is actually made. The four kinds of accountability and Pakistan's institutions that enforce them, then public policy from problem to evaluation.

Power without accountability is the root of the corruption in the last chapter. This one is the answer: the auditors, committees and courts that hold power to account, and the disciplined cycle by which good policy is built. ⚖️

⭐ The five-minute version

  • Accountability is the obligation to give an account. Public servants are power-holders (political, financial, administrative), so they must answer for their duties and the resources entrusted to them. That is why they are held to a higher standard than an ordinary person.
  • There are four types: political (to voters and the media), judicial/legal (to the courts), administrative (to auditors, inspectors and the internal hierarchy), and professional (to a profession's own standards).
  • The institutions that enforce it: the Auditor General of Pakistan (AGP) audits public accounts; the Public Accounts Committee (PAC) scrutinises the AGP's reports in parliament; the NAB, FIA, provincial anti-corruption establishments and the Ombudsman investigate and check wrongdoing, with the media as an informal watchdog.
  • Judicial review runs mainly through Article 184(3), the Supreme Court's original jurisdiction on questions of public importance touching fundamental rights, the basis of suo motu action. Note the 26th and 27th Amendments have changed this landscape.
  • Public policy is "the actions of government and the intentions that determine those actions" (Cochran), or, in the famous shorter version, "what government chooses to do or not to do" (Dye). Inaction is also policy.
  • The policy cycle has a fixed order. It starts with problem identification, never policy formulation, and ends with evaluation, never implementation. Stages: problem identification, agenda setting, formulation, adoption, implementation, evaluation.
  • A flaw in implementation is a failure of governance, since governance just is the implementation of public policy. Implementation works best when backed by law.
1 The frame

📋 What accountability is, and its four types

Class 4

Accountability is the obligation of public servants and civil servants to present an account of their duties, their activities and the resources given to them by the government or by virtue of their office Class 4 · 09:01. In short: what did you do with the power and money you were trusted with, and why.

Why power-holders are held higher. The teacher's rationale: civil servants and public officials are power-holders who possess political, financial and administrative power, so their accountability is far greater than that of an ordinary citizen Class 4 · 09:01. The more power an office carries, the heavier its duty to answer for it. This is the moral logic behind every institution in the next section.

The four types

TypeTo whom, and how
PoliticalTo the voters and the media. Elected representatives answer at the ballot box and in public opinion. Parliamentarians are not immune; a citizen can bring a writ against a law or politician Class 4 · 37:59
Judicial / legalTo the courts, through judicial review (see section 3). Laws or acts against the public interest can be challenged Class 4 · 37:59
AdministrativeTo inspectors, auditors, controllers and the internal reporting hierarchy. The watchdogs who check whether the work is being done. This mirrors regulation theory's identify-and-correct idea Class 4 · 38:31
ProfessionalTo the standards and codes of one's own profession

The link back to the last chapter. Accountability is the structural answer to corruption. Where corruption is the misuse of entrusted power, accountability is the obligation to justify how that power was used, backed by institutions that can check and punish. Frame an anti-corruption answer around these four types and you have both the diagnosis and the cure in one structure.

2 The machinery

🏛️ The institutions of accountability

Class 4

The teacher calls these the institutional part of the government edifice that ensures accountability, the external auditors and watchdogs of the state Class 4 · 58:21. Know each one's role and, where given, its founding law (a favourite for MCQs).

Editorial: which details are added. The lecture names all these institutions and gives two founding dates directly, NAB 1999 and AGP 2001 (both good MCQ facts she flags herself). The other founding laws and role descriptions in the table (FIA Act 1974, the Ombudsman established 1983 to redress "maladministration", the AGP's constitutional Articles) are added here for reference and accuracy; treat them as supporting detail rather than lecture content.

The Public Accounts Committee, in detail

The PAC deserves its own paragraph because it is the hinge between the auditor and the parliament. It is a legislative body within the National and Provincial Assemblies Class 4 · 01:01:40, and its objective is to uphold transparency and accountability across all public bodies and constitutional institutions Class 4 · 01:01:57. The chain is: the AGP audits and produces reports; the PAC examines those reports, summons officials to answer for irregularities, and reports its findings back to the legislature, so the public can see how each department spent its money Class 4 · 01:04:10.

Editorial: two precisions on the PAC. At the federal level the PAC is not purely a National Assembly body: it has 29 members, 23 from the National Assembly and 6 from the Senate, chaired by an NA member. And it questions the principal accounting officers (the secretaries of ministries and departments), not "representatives" loosely. The AGP is the auditor; the PAC is the parliamentary forum that acts on the audit. Keeping that division clear is a common way to earn or lose a mark.

Correction and currency: NAB's mandate has changed, and you must be current. NAB was created by the National Accountability Ordinance 1999, which the teacher rightly flags for MCQs. But its powers have been through a legal rollercoaster you should know as of 2026. The 2022 amendments narrowed NAB sharply: jurisdiction limited to corruption of Rs 500 million and above, and the chairman's and prosecutor-general's terms cut to three years. In September 2023 the Supreme Court struck those amendments down; then in September 2024, on intra-court appeals, it reversed itself and upheld the amendments, so they stand. The appellate architecture is now shifting to the new Federal Constitutional Court created by the 26th Amendment. The line to write: NAB operates under its amended 1999 law (Rs 500m threshold, three-year terms), upheld by the Supreme Court in 2024.

How to improve accountability (the "remedies" half). The teacher's solution list, for the standard "how to strengthen accountability" question Class 4 · 01:07:03: establish and empower anti-corruption institutions (like NAB) at local and provincial levels; legislative reforms; strengthen institutional capacity; judicial reforms; implement freedom-of-information laws; and roll out e-governance, which ties this chapter back to the last one.

3 The courts

⚖️ Judicial review and Article 184(3)

Class 4

Legal accountability runs through judicial review: the power of the courts to review an act or law you believe is against the constitution or against the public interest Class 4 · 48:44. The teacher anchors it to one article.

Article 184(3). This provision gives the Supreme Court original jurisdiction to make orders enforcing fundamental rights where a question of public importance arises Class 4 · 48:44. It is the constitutional basis of the Court's suo motu jurisdiction and of public-interest litigation. Under it, the judiciary can hold the executive and legislature to account when a law or action threatens the public's rights.

Editorial: judicial review is wider than 184(3), and the ground is shifting. Two accuracy points the teacher gestures at. First, 184(3) is one vehicle, specifically the Supreme Court's original fundamental-rights/suo motu jurisdiction. Judicial review of laws and executive action also rests on Article 8 (any law repugnant to fundamental rights is void) and the High Courts' writ jurisdiction under Article 199. Second, and importantly for currency: the 26th Amendment (October 2024) routed 184(3) original jurisdiction to newly created Constitutional Benches (Article 191A), and the teacher warns that the 26th and 27th Amendments change the dynamics of judicial review, so any judicial-review answer should note them Class 4 · 45:55. Write "judicial review, chiefly through Article 184(3)" rather than treating the two as identical.

The debate to be ready for. Judicial review is where accountability meets the separation of powers, and it is contested: welcomed as a check on executive abuse, criticised as judicial overreach when the Court steps into policy. A strong answer presents both, then locates the recent amendments as parliament's response to that tension. That combination, the doctrine plus the live constitutional debate, is exactly what a governance examiner rewards.

4 Policy defined

📜 What public policy is

Class 4

The second half of the chapter turns from checking power to using it: public policy. The definition on the deck: public policy is the actions of the government and the intentions that determine those actions Class 4 · 01:46:11. Policy is action plus the goal behind it.

Correction: get the two definitions and their authors right. The lecture blends two famous definitions and it is worth separating them, because the paper may ask who said what. The "actions of government and the intentions that determine those actions" formulation is Clarke Cochran. The shorter, more famous line the teacher praises as "brilliant", "what government chooses to do or not to do", is Thomas Dye (Understanding Public Policy, 1972), not Cochran. Cite Cochran for actions-and-intentions and Dye for to-do-or-not-to-do.

Inaction is policy too

The most useful idea in Dye's definition is that choosing not to act is itself a policy Class 4 · 01:47:37. If the government does not seriously address the education or healthcare crisis, that neglect signals a policy of not prioritising it, whatever officials say Class 4 · 01:48:03. And because the state deliberates before it acts (or declines to), its inaction cannot be dismissed as an oversight the way an individual's might be Class 4 · 01:48:34. Read a government's silences as policy.

What form a policy takes

A policy takes many forms beyond a document headed "Policy". It can be a rule, a law, a regulation, a decision, or even a verdict or ruling of the Supreme Court, and often a combination Class 4 · 01:52:04. If there is no written money-laundering policy but the courts consistently rule against it, the state's effective policy on money laundering can be read from those rulings Class 4 · 01:53:09.

Three defining features of a public policy. The deck gives three, worth memorising Class 4 · 01:54:16. It is goal-oriented: there is always an intended outcome. It is not a discrete (secret) decision: unlike a defence or nuclear-security decision, which should be discreet, public policy must be known so citizens can plan (a petroleum policy, for instance, affects everyone's budgeting). And it emerges in response to public demand to particular problems, because its whole aim is public service delivery and addressing people's concerns Class 4 · 01:55:29.

Editorial: the evaluation criteria for a policy. The deck's "goals of public policy", efficiency, equity and political practicality, are, in the standard literature, the criteria used to evaluate a policy rather than goals in themselves. Efficiency weighs benefits against costs; equity asks whether the process and outcome are fair (rights-based or redistributive); political practicality (feasibility) asks whether it can actually be enacted. The full list in the literature also includes effectiveness, administrative feasibility, liberty and legality, so treat these three as a core subset.

5 The cycle

🔁 The policy cycle

Class 4

The policy cycle is the stage-by-stage model of how a policy is made, and the teacher is emphatic about its order, because getting it wrong costs real marks.

The rule she repeats three times: the order is fixed. The cycle starts with problem identification, never with policy formulation Class 4 · 01:42:59, and it ends with policy evaluation, never with implementation Class 4 · 01:43:39. She warns that students who start at formulation or stop at implementation lose "six to seven marks" on a 20-mark question Class 4 · 01:45:35. This is the single most exam-critical fact in the chapter.

#StageWhat happens
1Problem identificationA public need or problem is recognised. The cycle always begins here
2Agenda settingOut of many problems, choosing which ones the government will actually take up Class 4 · 02:04:54
3Policy formulationDesigning the proposed courses of action and alternatives
4Policy adoption (legitimation)A chosen solution is endorsed and legitimised. The teacher describes this stage as advocacy and "marketing" the policy through an advocacy coalition Class 4 · 02:10:18
5ImplementationCivil servants and administrative bodies carry it out
6EvaluationJudging whether the policy met its goals. The cycle always ends here

Editorial: whose model this is. The stages-heuristic policy cycle traces to Harold Lasswell, who first broke policy into stages, with the familiar textbook version associated with James Anderson. Crediting "Lasswell and Anderson" is the safe attribution. The point of the model is not that real policy is this tidy, but that it gives you a checklist to structure both analysis and answers.

Answer it with an example threaded through. The teacher's strong advice: do not explain the cycle abstractly, walk a real problem through it and name the administrative body at each stage Class 4 · 02:16:41. Take food insecurity or a flood: identify the problem, set it on the agenda, formulate options, adopt one, then implementation falls to a named body (for a flood, the NDMA), and finally evaluate. Attaching real bodies and a real problem to each stage is what turns a memorised list into a marks-winning answer.

6 Delivery

🚚 Types of policy, and implementation

Class 4

Two last pieces complete the chapter: the kinds of public policy, and how implementation, the stage where policy meets reality, actually works.

Types of public policy

TypeWhat it doesExample
ConstituentSets up government agencies or institutions and defines their rules and conduct Class 4 · 02:21:02Creating the FATF Secretariat
DistributiveDistributes funds or benefits to people Class 4 · 02:22:46A subsidy or a tax holiday
RegulatoryRegulates behaviour with rules and restrictionsPenalising reckless driving (a speeding ticket) Class 4 · 02:23:59
RedistributiveShifts resources from one group to anotherLand reforms (the teacher's Kerala example) Class 4 · 02:25:17

Implementation, and why it decides everything

Implementation is carried out by civil servants and administrative bodies, and the teacher makes it the stage where governance is won or lost Class 4 · 02:12:40. Two rules to hold:

  • Back it with law. Implementation is far more effective when a policy is backed by an act or law, because violators can then be penalised and the policy actually bites Class 4 · 01:53:44.
  • A flaw in implementation is a governance failure. Since governance simply is the implementation of public policy, poor implementation is by definition a failure of governance, even if the policy itself was well designed Class 4 · 02:15:21.

Editorial: the two classic implementation models. An examiner rewards the top-down vs bottom-up distinction, which the lecture does not cover but which strengthens any implementation answer. The top-down model (Pressman and Wildavsky's Implementation, 1973; Sabatier and Mazmanian) sees implementation as executing a clear central decision downward, for instance the State Bank issuing a directive that all banks must follow. The bottom-up model (Lipsky's Street-Level Bureaucracy, 1980; Hjern) argues that the front-line officials who actually deliver a service shape the policy in practice, so implementation must account for their discretion. Naming both, and noting Sabatier later sought a synthesis, shows command of the topic.

Design failure vs implementation failure. When a policy misses its goals, the teacher insists you diagnose which failed: was the policy design wrong (the problem misidentified, the contents flawed), or was the implementation wrong Class 4 · 02:15:21? The distinction matters because the remedy differs: a design failure needs a better policy, an implementation failure needs better administration. A good evaluation answer always separates the two.

F Recall

🎴 Flashcards

Define accountability, and why are power-holders held higher?

The obligation to give an account of one's duties, activities and entrusted resources. Public servants hold political, financial and administrative power, so their accountability is greater than an ordinary person's.

Name the four types of accountability.

Political (to voters and media), judicial/legal (to courts), administrative (to auditors and the internal hierarchy), professional (to professional standards).

What does the AGP do, and when was its ordinance?

The Auditor General audits federal and provincial public accounts and reports whether money was properly spent. AGP Ordinance 2001; constitutional basis Articles 168 to 171.

What is the PAC, and how does it relate to the AGP?

A parliamentary committee (federal: 23 NA + 6 Senate) that scrutinises the AGP's audit reports and reports to the legislature. The AGP audits; the PAC acts on the audit.

When was NAB created, and what is its current status?

National Accountability Ordinance 1999. Its 2022 amendments (Rs 500m threshold, 3-year terms) were struck down in 2023, then upheld by the Supreme Court in 2024, so they stand; appeals are moving to the Federal Constitutional Court.

What is judicial review, and which article anchors it?

The courts' power to review a law or act against the constitution or public interest. Chiefly Article 184(3) (SC original jurisdiction on public-importance fundamental-rights questions, and suo motu); also Articles 8 and 199. The 26th/27th Amendments have changed the landscape.

Give the two famous definitions of public policy and their authors.

Cochran: "the actions of government and the intentions that determine those actions." Dye: "what government chooses to do or not to do." Inaction is policy too.

What are the three defining features of a public policy?

Goal-oriented; not a discrete (secret) decision, unlike a defence decision; and it emerges in response to public demand to particular problems.

State the policy cycle, and the two order rules.

Problem identification → agenda setting → formulation → adoption → implementation → evaluation. It always STARTS with problem identification (never formulation) and ENDS with evaluation (never implementation).

What are the four types of public policy?

Constituent (sets up agencies, e.g. FATF Secretariat), distributive (subsidy, tax holiday), regulatory (restricts behaviour), redistributive (shifts resources).

Why is a flaw in implementation a governance failure?

Because governance simply is the implementation of public policy, so poor implementation is by definition a failure of governance, even if the policy was well designed.

Top-down vs bottom-up implementation?

Top-down (Pressman & Wildavsky; Sabatier & Mazmanian): execute a clear central decision downward. Bottom-up (Lipsky; Hjern): front-line street-level officials shape the policy in practice.

X Strategy

🎯 Exam focus

The marks you cannot afford to drop

  • The policy cycle order. Start at problem identification, end at evaluation. The teacher says students routinely lose six or seven marks here. Learn it cold.
  • The founding dates. NAB Ordinance 1999, AGP Ordinance 2001. Classic MCQ material.
  • Cochran vs Dye. Do not attribute "to do or not to do" to Cochran; that is Dye.
  • NAB's current status. The 2022 amendments were upheld in 2024. An outdated answer here reads as unprepared.

How to build an accountability answer

  • Define accountability and give the four types.
  • Populate each type with its institution: political (media, elections), administrative (AGP, PAC, internal audit), legal (courts, 184(3)), plus NAB, FIA and the Ombudsman across them.
  • Add the remedies: empower anti-corruption bodies, legislative and judicial reform, freedom-of-information laws, e-governance.
  • Note the live debate: judicial review versus overreach, and the 26th/27th Amendments.

How to build a policy answer

  • Define policy (Cochran and Dye), stress that inaction is policy.
  • Walk the six-stage cycle with a single real example (a flood, food insecurity) and a named body at each stage (NDMA for the flood).
  • Distinguish design failure from implementation failure, and note top-down vs bottom-up implementation.
  • Close on the governance link: a policy is only as good as its implementation, and implementation backed by law is what makes a policy bite.

The one line to remember. This chapter is the state holding itself to account, then putting power to work. Accountability without good policy is empty, and policy without accountability is dangerous; the well-governed state needs both, the watchdogs of the first half and the disciplined cycle of the second.
