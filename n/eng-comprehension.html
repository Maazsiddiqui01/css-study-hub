⭐ The five-minute version

  • What it is. Comprehension gives you a passage and five questions on it, four marks each, 20 marks in all. You read it closely and answer each question in your own words. In Sir Danish's exam plan it gets about the first 35 minutes of the paper.
  • The golden rule. Answer in your own words, and keep them clear, simple and direct. The examiner is checking that you understood the passage and can express it yourself, not that you can lift its sentences.
  • The process is a refinement. Understand the point the passage is making, put it in a plain first attempt, then refine it: cut anything unrelated, remove redundant synonyms, and make sure a reader who has not seen the passage would follow you.
  • Most questions stay inside the text. "What does the writer mean by...", "What is the difference between X and Y", "What is implied": for all of these you report the passage's own meaning, reworded. You do not add your opinion.
  • One kind of question lets you out. When you are asked directly, "Do you agree?", "Justify your view", you may step out of the text and give your own opinion, drawn from your own observation. Even then, stay close to the bounds the passage set.
  • Keep answers roughly uniform in length and, for the exam, keep them short and to the point: a comprehension answer is worth only a few marks, so cut extra, unrelated lines rather than padding.
  • A tip that is CSS-specific: do not import outside references or quotations (a book title, a famous example) into an opinion answer. What works in the GRE can read as vague or off-point here. Keep it clear and self-contained.
1 The rule

🔑 What comprehension really tests

Composition

Comprehension looks easy and is quietly demanding. The passage is on the page, so the facts are given to you. What is being marked is whether you can show you understood by saying the point back in your own English, clearly.

Sir Danish keeps pulling every answer toward the same target: a phrase that is direct, simple and to the point, so that it is obvious you have understood the idea and absorbed it into your own words Composition · 04:11. The refining he does with students is almost always in that direction: shorter, plainer, clearer.

The reader test. The best single check he offers: write so that the reader understands what you are writing Composition · 29:04. You may know exactly what you mean, but the answer only earns marks if it lands with someone who has your script and not your head. If a sentence would confuse a fresh reader, it is not finished.

Think first, then simplify. A practical piece of advice he gives more than once: it is fine to think the idea first in Urdu, or whatever language you think in, and then work at making it easy for the reader to understand in English Composition · 29:34. The thinking and the expressing are two jobs. Get the idea straight, then dress it in the plainest English you can.

2 The text

🎨 The passage in front of us

Composition

The whole class works one passage, an essay arguing that beauty is not a luxury but an essential part of life, and that the ability to feel it belongs to everyone, a capacity common to all people rather than the preserve of professional artists.

The passage's argument, in outline. Art has "ebbed away out of our ordinary life," practised no longer by ordinary workmen but only by a few painters and sculptors, because we no longer recognise the aesthetic activity of the spirit so common to all men. The writer's central claim is the sentence the questions keep returning to: "beauty is not an ornament to life or to the things made by man; it is an essential part of both." And his parallel: "as actions empty of moral activity are distasteful to us, so should objects be that are empty of aesthetic activity."

Editorial: know your source. This passage is from the English essayist Arthur Clutton-Brock (1868 to 1924), an art critic for The Times, and comes from his short book The Ultimate Belief (1916). You do not need the attribution to answer the questions, but recognising the register helps: it is a reflective, argumentative essay, so its questions are about meaning and implication, not about facts and figures. Read for the argument, not for data.

Why the source barely matters for the method. The passage changes every year. What does not change is the kind of thing an argumentative passage asks: what a sentence means, what is implied, what the difference is between two ideas the writer contrasts, and whether you agree. The four worked answers below are templates for those question types, whatever the next passage is about.

3 The process

🔄 The answering process: understand, refine, draft

Composition

Every answer in the class is built the same way, in stages, and watching a rough attempt turn into a clean one is the real lesson.

StageWhat you do
1. UnderstandWork out the actual point the question is asking about. If it quotes a sentence, decide what that sentence means before writing anything.
2. First attemptPut the meaning down in your own words, even if it is rough. He praises students for the attempt itself: "that practice actually would be more rewarding than the actual answer" Composition · 11:35.
3. RefineSimplify. Cut anything unrelated. Remove a second word that just repeats the first. Make it clearer for the reader.
4. Final answerThe clean, direct version that a fresh reader would understand at once.

Two refining habits he corrects constantly. First, do not stack synonyms: "when you said fundamental, you do not need another synonym in the same sentence to express the same thing" Composition · 39:03. One precise word is stronger than two loose ones. Second, stop when the point is made: he repeatedly tells students a trailing extra sentence is "going to be extra. If you stop the work after that, that would be sufficient" Composition · 18:32. A comprehension answer does not need a flourish at the end.

Process over product, for now. His encouragement to a student who struggled: the point of practice is to learn and understand the process, because "once you follow the process, you are more likely to answer in a right direction" Composition · 12:02. A rough answer produced by the right method is worth more, at this stage, than a lucky clean one.

4 Worked answers

✅ Four questions, built in front of you

Composition

Four of the passage's questions, each showing the same move: find the meaning, then say it plainly. The wording below follows the model answers arrived at in class.

Q1 · What is implied in the text?

The refined answer lands on the passage's central message: the writer laments the decline of aesthetic values in daily life, and blames society for failing to recognise beauty as an essential human and spiritual activity, rather than the exclusive domain of professional artists Composition · 07:11. Note how it names the writer's feeling (lament), his target (society), and his claim (beauty is essential and universal), all in one sentence.

Q2 · What is the difference between an ordinary man and an artist?

The ideal shape here, he explains, is one line on the fundamental difference, then one line on each side Composition · 20:47. The model answer: the fundamental difference lies in their perception of beauty. An artist is conscious of the beauty around him in the universe and transmits it through everything he makes; an ordinary man perceives beauty only as an external object and makes little effort to appreciate the aesthetics in the things around him Composition · 21:14.

Q3 · How can we make our lives beautiful and charming?

Drawn straight from the passage's own prescription: we can make our lives beautiful by integrating beauty into our work, surroundings and daily activities, by cultivating an appreciation of aesthetic values, and by developing the habit of recognising beauty in nature for spiritual rejuvenation Composition · 31:05. The passage's line behind this: when a man makes anything he should make it beautiful for its own sake, and beauty should be treated as essential, not as a luxury.

Q4 · What does the writer mean by "beauty is not an ornament to life"?

This is the classic "what does the writer mean" question, and the answer is an interpretation, not a repetition. Most people wrongly regard beauty as a luxury decoration, something external or optional. The writer means the opposite: beauty should not be treated as a decorative luxury added to appease our material wants. It is an essential element of human life that elevates our moral and spiritual existence Composition · 42:46.

The pattern across all four. Each answer (a) names what the passage says, (b) in the student's own words, (c) in one or two clean sentences, (d) with no quotation and no personal opinion. That is the comprehension answer in its default form. Only the fifth question type, below, breaks the "no opinion" rule.

5 The exception

💬 The opinion question: when you may step out

Composition

The last question on the passage was different: "Do art and beauty affect our practical life and morals? Justify whether you agree or disagree." This is the one place comprehension lets you leave the text.

The rule. In this rare circumstance, when the question directly asks for your view, you are allowed to step out from the text and bring your own opinion into the answer Composition · 46:17. When the examiner addresses you directly, you can give your view, and you may rely on your own personal observation to support it Composition · 51:38.

The model answer both agrees and grounds itself in the passage's own logic: I strongly agree that art and beauty affect our life and morality. Beauty shapes our aesthetic sense and contributes to our moral and spiritual growth; the absence of beauty in daily life leads to spiritual dissatisfaction and social decline Composition · 59:49. It takes a clear side and justifies it, which is exactly what "justify whether you agree" asks.

The CSS-specific warning: do not import outside references. A student wanted to support an opinion with an external example (a medical student inspired by a Kafka story), on the logic that GRE advice rewards outside references. Sir Danish pushes back firmly: CSS and the GRE are different, and here an unexplained outside reference introduces vagueness and ambiguity rather than strength Composition · 55:49. A reader cannot tell whether "a medical student chose the field because of a book" supports your point or undercuts it. So avoid quoting external references; if you use a general idea of your own, phrase it plainly without a name-drop Composition · 54:35.

But with a strongly one-sided passage, do not take the opposite stance. This is where the note has to be careful, because Sir Danish gives a permission and then immediately qualifies it. Yes, "you can take the liberty to disagree" Composition · 01:04:35, but his very next line is "it is not advisable to disagree at this stage" Composition · 01:04:55, and about this overwhelmingly one-sided art-and-beauty passage specifically, he says it is not advisable to take an altogether opposite stance Composition · 01:16:24. So the safe reading of his advice: you are technically permitted to disagree, but when the passage argues hard in one direction, play safe and stay within the bounds it set Composition · 57:46.

The reason is refreshingly honest about the exam: the goal of this question is to earn its three or four marks, not to win an argument Composition · 01:04:00. Marks come from a clear, justified stance, cleanly expressed, and for a lopsided passage the low-risk clear stance is usually the one that agrees with it and justifies why.

6 On the page

📐 Presentation and length

Composition

Two habits of presentation come up, and both are about respecting the small mark value of each answer.

Keep answer lengths roughly uniform. Carried over from an earlier class: the answers to the different comprehension questions should be of roughly the same length Composition · 43:18. A wildly long answer next to a one-line one looks unbalanced and usually means the long one has padding.

Answer from the exam's point of view: short. When you feel an answer is running long, remember what it is worth. A comprehension answer carries only a few marks, so the exam-smart move is a short, tight answer that cuts the extra, unrelated points rather than a sprawling one Composition · 44:40. Say the point, justify it if asked, and stop.

The time budget. In the whole-paper plan, comprehension gets roughly the first 35 minutes of the three-hour paper. Five short, clean answers in that window is the target, which is another reason to keep each one tight. Do not let comprehension eat into the time the précis will need.

The habits that earn the marks

  • Own words, always. If a whole phrase is lifted from the passage, reword it.
  • Clear, simple, direct. Short sentences a fresh reader follows at once.
  • No redundant synonyms, no trailing extra line. Say the point and stop.
  • Roughly uniform, short answers. Each is worth only a few marks.
  • Opinion only when invited, and even then stay near the passage's bounds and skip outside references.
  • Watch the clock: about 35 minutes for the whole section, so you protect the précis time.

The one line to remember. A comprehension answer is a small act of translation: from the writer's words into yours, from his meaning into a sentence a stranger could follow. Do that cleanly and briefly, question after question, and the section is straightforward marks. The only trap is the opinion question, where the freedom to leave the text tempts people into vague, unsupported, or over-long answers. Take a side, justify it in a line or two, and move on.
