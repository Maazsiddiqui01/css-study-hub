⭐ The five-minute version

  • Read the command, not the punctuation alone. A question mark often signals an argumentative demand, but a "how" question may simply ask for mechanisms. Explicit alternatives such as "success or failure" or "X or Y" require a choice.
  • Take a definite position. The argumentative essay defends one answer. Its arguments may come from several dimensions, but the stance must remain stable from introduction to conclusion.
  • Address the entire phrasing. When the prompt names two alternatives, defend the chosen one and incorporate the other through refutation. Ignoring the second phrase leaves part of the question unanswered.
  • Give your case first. After the introduction and any needed exposition, develop the main heading that proves your stance through several strong, varied arguments.
  • Refute under a separate heading. State the opposing claim accurately, explain its rationale, answer it directly, justify the rebuttal with evidence, and close by reaffirming your original stance.
  • Keep one refutation unit in one paragraph. The opponent's claim, its explanation, your rebuttal, evidence and concluding statement belong together so the reader can see the contest clearly.
  • Do not manufacture a debate. Refutation is compulsory when the prompt itself presents alternatives. In a looser prompt, include it only when a genuine opposing side, a direct counter and supporting evidence exist.
  • Finish every paragraph with a link back. The concluding sentence should use that paragraph's counterargument to strengthen the exact stance and wording of the topic.

2 Commit without narrowing

🎯 Choose one stance

00:00-05:24

An argumentative essay needs a definitive position and a defence of that position 00:00:57. Unity of stance means the answer stays the same throughout the paper, even while the essay draws arguments from political, economic, social, technological and other dimensions 00:01:26.

Unity of stance

One answer controls the thesis, main arguments, rebuttals, paragraph conclusions and final conclusion.

Diversity of arguments

Different domains supply independent reasons for the same answer, avoiding a one-dimensional case.

Unity does not mean silence about the other side

If the topic names both sides, the writer must incorporate both while protecting one stance 00:04:01. The opposing side appears as a claim to be tested and rebutted, rather than as a second conclusion. This is how the essay answers the whole prompt without becoming a neutral list.

Use the prompt's exact degree

The live critique warns against replacing a word such as blunder with a stronger word such as disaster 00:34:41. A different label can alter the proposition being defended. Stay close to the question's nouns, alternatives and level of certainty.

One-line test. Complete "This essay argues that..." in one unambiguous sentence. If two incompatible conclusions appear, the stance has not been chosen.

3 Design the argument

🗺️ Architect the whole essay

04:01-08:19

The argumentative structure adds one distinctive element to the familiar essay: a deliberate refutation section after the writer's main case. The exact headings change with the stance, but the logic stays stable.

Course outline pattern

SectionPurposeIllustrative phrasing from the class prompt

Balance by purpose. Refutation is usually shorter than the main case. The aim is to remove the strongest doubt from the reader's mind, not to give both positions equal argumentative weight.

4 Meet the opposition

⚖️ Build the refutation section

04:56-09:20

When a prompt explicitly supplies two alternatives, writing only the chosen side does not fully address the topic 00:04:56. The refutation section incorporates the other side, demonstrates that the writer understands its best case, and explains why the chosen stance remains stronger.

One subheading, one contested claim

  1. Name the proponent's argument. State what supporters of the other side actually claim.
  2. Give the counterargument. Identify the precise flaw, limit or missing condition.
  3. Supply justification. Use a verified example, case, comparison or statistic that supports the counterargument.
  4. Close the unit. Show how defeating this claim strengthens the original stance.

In the Single National Curriculum demonstration, the opposing claim is that one curriculum advances educational equality. The counterargument says that a curriculum alone cannot erase the public-private divide without financial and human resources 00:06:58 00:07:27. The writing lesson is the relationship among claim, answer and evidence; the note does not adopt the classroom example as a policy verdict.

Refutation is not description

A pair of headings titled "why it is a success" and "why it is a failure" merely describes both sides. Argumentative structure chooses one side as the main case and places the other inside a refutation heading, where each opposing reason receives a rebuttal 00:48:33 00:50:32.

5 Control the paragraph

🧩 Write one rebuttal paragraph

08:19-14:24

Each numbered refutation point becomes one paragraph, not a series of separate paragraphs for the opponent, rebuttal and evidence 00:08:19 00:09:20. Keeping the contest together reduces repetition and lets the reader follow the answer.

The five moves

MoveFunctionCourse direction
1. Opposing claimIntroduce what proponents believe.Use a formal signal such as "Supporters contend..." or "Some hold the view..." 00:09:48.
2. ExplanationShow the rationale behind that belief.Give it one or two fair sentences before answering 00:09:48.
3. RebuttalState the direct counterargument.Shift clearly: "However, this claim overlooks..."
4. EvidenceJustify the counterargument.The evidence supports your rebuttal, not the opposing claim 00:10:18.
5. Concluding statementReconnect the proven point to the essay's stance.Use the counterargument to reaffirm the exact position 00:10:47.

Paragraph logic in compact form

  1. They argue X because Y.
  2. However, X fails to account for Z.
  3. Verified evidence demonstrates Z.
  4. Therefore, Z reinforces this essay's position that...

Fairness before force. Explain the opposing rationale before rebutting it. Opening immediately with "although" and the writer's answer can collapse the opponent's claim, explanation and rebuttal into one confused sentence 00:28:45 00:29:28.

7 Use mature judgment

🤔 Decide when to refute

41:28-01:21:52

The lecture distinguishes mandatory refutation from optional refutation. The decision begins with the wording of the prompt, then tests whether a real and supportable opposing case exists.

When refutation is compulsory

  • The prompt explicitly writes both alternatives, such as "success or failure" or "a route to stability or a recipe for disaster" 00:53:20 01:11:32.
  • The writer must choose one alternative while visibly addressing the other.
  • Omitting the second phrase would leave the question only partially answered 01:12:17.

When it is a judgment call

A prompt may ask a direct question without naming the alternative. Then the writer asks whether the issue contains a strong debate and whether the opposing side can be represented honestly. The teacher warns that a forced, weak refutation can reduce the impression created by an otherwise strong essay 01:06:36 01:07:30.

The three-part evidence test

1. Real claim

Can you state an argument the opposing side genuinely makes, rather than one invented for convenience?

2. Direct counter

Do you have an answer that meets that specific claim instead of drifting to a neighbouring issue?

3. Evidence

Can a verified example, case or statistic support the counterargument 01:20:56 01:21:24?

Do not create symmetry that the topic lacks. The class treats a populism-and-democracy prompt as an example where the teacher considers the opposing case too weak to require a forced refutation 01:05:44 01:06:10. This is a course judgment about that prompt, not a verified scholarly conclusion about all theories of populism.

Avoid repetition during brainstorming

Brainstorm the main side and the refutation separately. Select the strongest six or seven reasons for the main case, then test the remaining material for direct use against opposing claims 01:26:54 01:27:41. An argument that fits both sides should be assigned to the place where it performs the clearest job.

End-to-end exam workflow

  1. Translate the prompt into a plain instruction.
  2. Choose the stance and repeat the exact degree of the topic in a one-line thesis.
  3. Brainstorm the main case across several dimensions.
  4. Decide whether refutation is mandatory from the phrasing or justified by a genuine debate.
  5. Brainstorm opposing claims separately and pair each with a direct counter and verified evidence.
  6. Write visible headings for the main case and the opposing claim.
  7. Draft one five-move paragraph per refutation point.
  8. Audit every conclusion for a paragraph-specific link to the same stance.

Final test. Cover the title and read the concluding sentence of every body paragraph. Together, they should reveal one consistent answer to the original wording.

Revision sheet

🎯 Exam focus

The complete architecture

  1. Interpret: identify whether the prompt asks for judgment, choice or explanation.
  2. Commit: state one position in the topic's exact terms.
  3. Prove: develop the main case through diversified arguments and verified evidence.
  4. Refute: when required, answer two or three genuine opposing claims.
  5. Reaffirm: make every paragraph conclusion strengthen the same stance.

Refutation paragraph template

Sentence jobStarter
Opposing claim"Supporters of X contend that..."
Rationale"Their position rests on..."
Rebuttal"However, this argument overlooks..."
Evidence"This limitation is demonstrated by..."
Link back"Therefore, [proven counter] reinforces the view that [exact stance]."

Common mistakes

  • Treating every question mark as an argumentative command.
  • Writing both alternatives descriptively without choosing a position.
  • Ignoring the second alternative named in the prompt.
  • Changing the prompt's degree or key word while drafting.
  • Inventing a weak opposing claim merely to fill a refutation heading.
  • Placing the opposing claim, explanation and rebuttal in separate paragraphs.
  • Giving evidence for the opponent rather than for the counterargument.
  • Ending with a suggestion, generic summary or bare repetition of the stance.
  • Reusing an unsourced classroom statistic.
