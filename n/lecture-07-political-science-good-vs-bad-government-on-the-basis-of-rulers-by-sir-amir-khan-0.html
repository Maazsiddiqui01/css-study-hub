⭐ The five-minute version

  • Aristotle is the father of political science 00:01:44, because he studied the state systematically, by observation and experiment. It is a social science, not a natural science, but it is a science.
  • His ideal state is built on moderation. Population neither too large (law cannot be implemented) nor too small (it cannot defend itself). Territory the same. A sea route for trade, but not sitting on the coast where it can be attacked. Society split into citizens and slaves, six tasks divided between them, state-controlled public education, and rule under a constitution where nobody is above the law.
  • Aristotle against Plato. Same topics, opposite temperament. Plato's state is a utopia; Aristotle's is realistic and practicable 00:26:26. Plato puts rulers above the law and strips them of property; Aristotle insists on rule of law and says abolishing property is simply not in human nature. Hence the exam line: a great but not a grateful student of Plato.
  • Criticise him on exactly two counts 00:24:16: gender discrimination (education for male citizens only) and racial discrimination (his defence of slavery). Everything else in his state is workable.
  • The classification is his most important contribution 00:27:16, and it runs on two variables: the number of rulers and whether the form is good or bad. One: monarchy or tyranny. Few: aristocracy or oligarchy. Many: polity or democracy. The good forms serve the interest of society; the bad ones serve the ruler's self-interest.
  • Polity is the best form 00:37:17. It works because every society holds two forces, quality (the educated elite, who have the ruling ideas) and quantity (the lower class, who have the numbers). Rule by either alone is bad government. Balance them by empowering the middle class 00:52:58, which is what polity is.
  • Then the lecture jumps two thousand years. Machiavelli ends the medieval idea that the state is a means to salvation and declares the state an end in itself 01:31:30: build a national army, be feared rather than loved, expand or expire, strike first, and treat only interests as permanent. He is the father of modern political science and the founder of the secular state.
  • Finally the test for a good law. Bentham: pleasure and pain are our two sovereigns, and any law is good if it produces the greatest happiness for the greatest number, judged by consequences and never intentions. Kant is the exact opposite: some principles are absolute whatever the consequences.
1 The ideal state

🏙️ Aristotle's ideal state

00:01 – 00:24

Aristotle developed the subject as a science: the study is systematic, it is observation, it is experimentation. It is social science rather than natural science, but it is science, and that is why he is called the father of political science 00:01:44.

Two contributions matter for the paper: his ideal state, and his classification of government 00:02:19. Take the ideal state first, characteristic by characteristic. The thread running through all of them is moderation: every quantity has to sit between two failures.

Population

Without population a state is just a piece of land, so no population means no state 00:03:13. The question is whether it should be large or small, and Aristotle's answer is neither:

  • Too large, and you face problems in the implementation of law. The law will not be implemented in the true sense 00:04:06.
  • Too small, and the state cannot defend itself 00:04:06.

The lecturer's live example. The big issue of Pakistan is population: there is roughly one policeman for over 200 people, and law implementation is a very large problem here because of the size of the population 00:04:56. That is Aristotle's first failure mode, running in real time.

Two numbers, and one of them is flagged for MCQs. Aristotle's figure for the ideal state is 15,000 to 100,000 00:05:33. Plato's figure is very specific: 5,040, and the lecturer notes that Plato's population often comes in MCQs 00:06:36. Remember both are describing small Greek city states, not Pakistan or India.

Territory

The same rule and the same reason: territory should be neither too large nor too small 00:07:07. The ruler should be able to see the whole state 00:08:05. As the lecturer puts it, Aristotle was not living in Pakistan, India, Russia or China. He was living in a city state, and we pursue what we see 00:08:30.

Location

An ideal state must not be landlocked, like Afghanistan, which has no sea route. It must have a sea route, because that helps the state with exports and imports 00:08:30. But it should not sit very close to the sea, because then it can be attacked from the sea, in the way the Vikings arrived before anyone could prepare 00:09:19.

Division of society

Plato divided society into three classes. Aristotle divides it into two: citizens and slaves 00:10:49. And here he hands you your criticism, because he is supporting slavery 00:11:40.

The six tasks

There are six important tasks in a state, in two groups of three 00:12:35:

Group of tasksWho performs themWhich citizens
Agriculture, art, craftSlavesn/a
Defence
Governing
Religion		CitizensThe young defend the state
The middle-aged govern the state
The old protect the religion of the state

So the split is clean: agriculture, art and craft are the work of slaves; defence, governing and religion are the work of citizens, allocated by age 00:14:01.

Education

Like Plato, Aristotle holds that education is a very important aspect of any state, that education can create a state, and that it must be controlled by the state and must not be private 00:15:32. Education must be public.

Then the second criticism arrives: only male citizens have a right to education 00:15:32. Aristotle holds a traditional view of women 00:16:19.

The two criticisms, and they are the whole of your criticism 00:24:16. Racial discrimination: for Aristotle the Greeks are the superior race and non-Greeks are slaves, and Plato says nothing of the kind 00:17:45. Gender discrimination: he is anti women's rights, because the right to education is for male citizens only 00:18:20. Beyond those two, his state is practicable and realistic rather than idealistic like Plato's.

Constitution and property

These two are where Aristotle turns on his teacher hardest.

  • Constitution. In Plato's ideal state the rulers are above the law. In fact they are the law: whatever they say is law, so there is no need of a constitution and no need to clip their wings 00:19:05. Aristotle says laws and a constitution must be formulated, and nobody is above the law or above the constitution. So Aristotle is talking about equality before law and rule of law, where Plato was talking about absolute rule 00:19:39.
  • Property. Plato said the rulers and the soldiers have no right to private property. Aristotle's reply is flatly practical: you cannot do it, no man agrees to that, it is not in their nature 00:20:23. Why propose something that is not practicable? You may divide property so far that there is neither rich nor poor, but you cannot abolish it 00:21:33.

3 The main contribution

📊 The classification of government

00:27 – 01:00

This is the most important contribution of Aristotle to political science 00:27:16, and it came up in the 2016 paper 00:58:27. He classifies government on two bases: the number of rulers, and good form against bad form. The lecturer tells the class to draw it as a diagram 00:28:09, so draw it.

Number of rulersGood formBad form
OneMonarchyTyranny
Few (5, 50, 100, 200)AristocracyOligarchy
ManyPolityDemocracy

What makes a form good or bad

The test is not the number. The number only names the row; what decides the column is whose interest is served and whether there is law:

  • Monarchy. One person rules through established laws, and he is not promoting his self-interest but the interest of his society 00:34:08.
  • Tyranny. One person rules and is not following any law. His words are laws, and he uses the tactic of fear while ruling 00:34:48.

The same logic runs down the table: aristocracy is the few ruling for society's interest and not corrupt, oligarchy is the few who corrupt themselves and promote their self-interest 00:44:20; polity is the good form of rule by many, and democracy its bad form.

Two MCQs the lecturer says outright will be asked 00:37:17. Best form of government according to Aristotle: polity. Worst form of government according to Aristotle: democracy.

Correction, and this one matters more than any other line in the lecture. "Best is polity" is fine. "Worst is democracy" is not what Aristotle says, and he says the opposite in one sentence of his own text. Politics IV.2: the perversion of the highest form is necessarily the worst, so "tyranny, which is the worst of governments", is furthest from a well-constituted form; oligarchy is little better; and "democracy is the most tolerable of the three."

So among the three bad forms Aristotle ranks them tyranny (worst) → oligarchy → democracy (least bad). Democracy is the most tolerable deviation, not the worst form of government.

What to write. For "best", answer polity, with the distinction that it is the best practicable constitution (IV.11) while the absolutely best would be kingship or aristocracy of the truly virtuous (Book VII). For "worst", answer tyranny. The phrasing that scores either way: "Among the perverted forms Aristotle ranks tyranny the worst and democracy the most tolerable (IV.2), though democracy remains a perverted form, because it rules in the interest of the poor rather than the common good." That concedes the lecturer's real point (democracy is a bad form) without asserting the false claim that it is the worst.

Why democracy is the bad form here

Do not be shocked by that answer, because Aristotle's democracy is not the democracy you are watching today. It is Greek democracy 00:37:51. Democracy means rule of the people, and in Greek civilisation the people really did rule directly, appointing a man judge for a day.

The missing ingredient is education. For democracy the most important thing is education 00:38:33, and without education, democracy is mobocracy, the rule of the mob 00:39:18. The lecturer's picture of it is mob lynching and mob justice: nobody checks whether he did it or not, the crowd simply decides and acts 00:39:50. Greek society was at the top of civilisation, but that education was not widespread enough, and this is why the lecturer places the form at the bottom (see the correction above for what Aristotle's own text actually ranks worst).

The modern translation. What today's newspapers call the Scandinavian democracies, Norway, Denmark and Switzerland, the lecturer says would be called polity, because democracy there is a refined form 00:41:09. The ingredient that refines it is the one Aristotle demanded: education 00:40:31. So Aristotle is not attacking modern democracy; he is attacking an uneducated one.

And the uncomfortable local application 00:59:02. The lecturer turns the same test on Pakistan and answers it himself: where there is awareness and public opinion and everyone knows their role, you get quality, and that is the best form. Where votes go for a road, a pressure pump or a favour, and the electorate enjoys the election without knowing what it is choosing, you are somewhere else on the table 01:00:05. He is applying Aristotle's education condition to the present.

4 How each form dies

🔄 The cycle of governments

00:41 – 00:47

The classification is a snapshot. The cycle is what happens to it over time, and the lecturer says this question needs you to draw a cycle 00:41:09. Every good form decays into its bad twin, and every bad form is overthrown into the next good one.

StepWhat happensResult
1One person rules through established laws, for societyMonarchy 00:41:59
2He abandons law and rules by fearTyranny, which is unstable and will not last long 00:43:27
3A few rise against the tyrant and overthrow him, then rule for society's interest rather than their own, and are not corruptAristocracy, a stable form that lasts a long time 00:43:37
4With the passage of time they corrupt themselves and promote self-interest over society'sOligarchy 00:44:20
5The oligarchs are overthrown, and a form is established where each and every class has its own rulePolity 00:45:12

Read the pattern, not just the sequence. The engine is always the same: a good form is a form that rules for society, and it turns bad the moment its rulers start promoting self-interest. Corruption is what converts each good form into its bad twin, and overthrow is what converts each bad form into the next good one. That single sentence lets you reconstruct the whole cycle in the exam even if you forget the order.

Correction: this cycle is not Aristotle's. It is Polybius'. The sequence above is anacyclosis, from Polybius, Histories Book VI (2nd century BC): monarchy, then tyranny, then aristocracy, then oligarchy, then democracy, then ochlocracy (mob rule), and back to monarchy. The lecture is running Polybius' cycle with Aristotle's six names attached, calling Polybius' good-rule-by-many "polity" and his ochlocracy "democracy". Polybius' own remedy for the cycle is Rome's mixed constitution.

Aristotle has no cycle at all, and this is the part that can really cost you. Politics Book V studies constitutional change and faction causally, not as a fixed sequence, and he states that constitutions "more often change into the opposite form than into the one near them", which directly denies a set order. At V.12 he devotes a passage to attacking Plato's degeneration scheme, noting that a tyranny may turn into another tyranny, or into an oligarchy, or into a democracy. His politics is anti-deterministic: prudence, education, moderation and balance can preserve a constitution.

So: if a question asks whose is the cycle of governments, the answer is Polybius. Plato's own sequence (Republic VIII) is different again: aristocracy, timocracy, oligarchy, democracy, tyranny, ending at tyranny. Learn the cycle for the lecture, but never write it under Aristotle's name, because it attributes to him a thesis he wrote a chapter demolishing.

5 Why polity works

⚖️ Quality, quantity & the middle class

00:47 – 01:00

This is how Aristotle builds polity, and it is the deepest idea in the lecture. According to Aristotle there are two major forces in each and every society: quality and quantity 00:47:28.

ForceWho they areWhat they have
QualityThe elite or upper classEducation, and ideas. They hold the ruling ideas in any society 00:48:28.
QuantityThe lower classOne thing only: numbers. They are great in numbers 00:49:01.

The rule. If a government is ruled by either quality or quantity, it will be a bad government 00:49:35. Neither force is safe on its own.

Left alone, quantity is led by demagogues 00:50:25, who work on resentment (they have wealth, we want them to share it with us) and lead the many into revolution 00:51:25.

The answer: empower the middle class

So you have to balance these two powers 00:52:33. And the way you balance them is to empower the middle class in society 00:52:58. Let quality rule, by all means, but let the middle class be strong enough to hold it in check. That balance is polity, and it is why polity is the best form.

Use the age of the idea. The lecturer makes a point of it: Aristotle is prescribing a strong middle class as the stabiliser of the state two and a half thousand years ago 00:52:58. In an essay on democratic stability, inequality or polarisation, tying a modern argument back to Aristotle's quality-quantity balance is exactly the kind of link that lifts an answer.

6 The state as an end

🦊 Machiavelli & power politics

01:04 – 01:52

Between Aristotle and Machiavelli sits a gap the lecture crosses quickly. Greece was taken by the Romans, Rome became the city people called the City of God, and for more than a thousand years the philosophers of the West did one job: rationalise Christianity. That era is the Middle Ages, also called the medieval or dark ages, and its philosophers are St Augustine and Thomas Aquinas 01:07:42.

The medieval frame is what Machiavelli attacks. In it, a person is born into original sin, and the one purpose of life is redemption and salvation, with the church and the state guiding you there 01:11:24. Hold that thought, because Machiavelli's whole move is aimed at it.

The man and the moment

Machiavelli was from Florence. In his era Italy was divided into five city states, and they were at civil war with each other 01:25:56, with external interference from France, Spain and others feeding the conflict 01:26:39. The lecturer calls it an era of bastards and adventurers 01:29:17. Machiavelli raised his pen and wrote The Prince, a book full of advice for his government, and that book became the theory we call power politics 01:30:10.

Two lines from this section worth memorising. "Every great idea starts out with blasphemy" 01:20:51, which is how the lecturer frames the reception of The Prince. And Aristotle's own line about his teacher, quoted here: "Plato is dear, but truth is dearer" 01:23:52.

The one move that makes him modern

For more than a thousand years the state was used as a means to an end, and that end was salvation and redemption. That is Machiavelli's first target 01:30:33. His answer:

The state is not a means to an end. The state is an end in itself. 01:31:30 It is your only end, your only purpose, your only objective. Protect your state, make it powerful, unify it. Forget about redemption, forget about salvation. That is not your end. Your end is the state 01:31:56.

Everything else follows from that inversion. If the state is the end, then it justifies the means. Lying is bad, but your purpose is not whether you lie: your purpose is the state 01:33:14. Deceiving is bad, but if you deceive for the sake of the state, be a fox 01:33:49. The Bible says thou shalt not kill, but if you kill for the sake of the state, kill millions, and it is justified, so long as it is only for the state 01:34:29. This is what the lecturer means by Machiavelli's immorality: not immorality as such, but immorality which is justified 01:25:11. His three labels for Machiavelli are power politics, realism and immorality 01:24:31.

7 Consequences vs principles

⚗️ Bentham's utilitarianism vs Kant

01:57 – 02:17

Jeremy Bentham is an English philosopher, and the important thing is utilitarianism 01:57:49. It answers a question the whole lecture has been circling: what actually makes a law or an action good?

The two sovereigns

Nature has put the individual under two sovereigns in this world 01:59:13: one is pleasure and the other is pain. They rule over us. Whatever you do, your only purpose is to maximise pleasure and minimise pain. You are always running from the one and running toward the other 01:59:56.

The test

From that, Bentham's standard: any law, any rule, any action is moral, legal, good and just if it produces the greatest happiness for the greatest number of people 02:00:47.

And the hard edge of it: do not look at the intention of an action, look at the consequences 02:01:37. Suppose the action is very bad, a killing, a theft. It is not bad so long as it produces the greatest happiness for the greatest number. There are no absolute principles, no absolute good and no absolute bad. The only thing that makes an action good or bad is its consequences 02:02:12.

The utilitarian calculus

Bentham gives you a calculator to check your actions against 02:11:10. The lecturer walks four of its checks:

CheckThe question it asks
IntensityHow great is the happiness? If it is greater, do it; if not, go for another action 02:11:53.
CertaintyAre you sure that pleasure will actually come? If not, do something you are certain about 02:12:41.
PropinquityCloseness, that is, how near the pleasure is 02:12:41.
ExtentHow many people does it affect? This is the check that stops it being about you: the greatest happiness is for the greatest number, not for yourself 02:15:10.

A vocabulary warning from a past paper 02:13:27. Propinquity has appeared in the paper as a synonym question, where the expected sense was kinship. In Bentham it means closeness or nearness. Know both senses.

Kant, the exact opposite

Immanuel Kant is a German philosopher, and he is in the syllabus. His school runs directly against Bentham's: there are some absolute principles, and you must stay inside them every single time 02:03:29. If killing is bad, it is bad, no matter what the consequence is. You are not supposed to kill, because thou shalt not kill. You are not supposed to steal, because stealing is bad. You are not supposed to lie, because lying is bad 02:04:49.

Bentham (utilitarian)Kant
Are there absolute principles?No. No absolute good, no absolute bad.Yes, and you never leave them.
What do you judge?Consequences only.The principle, whatever the consequences.
Test of a good actionGreatest happiness for the greatest number.Does it obey the absolute principle?

The lecturer's three examples, and they are the fastest way to remember the split.

  • Sholay 02:06:58. Kill two of them yourself and I spare the rest; refuse and I kill all. Kant holds Amitabh Bachchan's feet and begs him not to, because killing is bad even if eighteen thousand are saved. Bentham says kill them, hurry up: greatest happiness for the greatest number 02:08:12.
  • Batman 02:09:03. Batman is Kant. He has the chance to kill the Joker and will not take it, even knowing the Joker will break out and kill again. Bentham would kill him.
  • Robin Hood 02:10:25. He steals, and on Bentham's test he is justified, because of the greatest happiness for the greatest number.
Reference

📖 Glossary

Ideal state
The state as it ought to be. Plato's is a utopia; Aristotle's is built to be practicable.
Landlocked state
A state with no sea route, such as Afghanistan. Aristotle's ideal state must not be one, because a sea route carries exports and imports.
Monarchy
Rule by one person through established laws, promoting the interest of society rather than his own. The good form of rule by one.
Tyranny
Rule by one person following no law: his words are law, and he rules by the tactic of fear. The bad form of rule by one.
Aristocracy
Rule by a few who serve society's interest and are not corrupt. The good form of rule by few, and a stable one.
Oligarchy
Rule by a few who have corrupted themselves and promote their self-interest. The bad form of rule by few.
Polity
The good form of rule by many, in which every class has its own share of rule. Aristotle's best form of government.
Democracy (Aristotle's sense)
The bad form of rule by many. Greek direct democracy, not the modern kind.
Mobocracy
What democracy becomes without education: rule of the mob, with mob lynching and mob justice in place of law.
Quality
The elite or upper class: education and ideas, and so the ruling ideas of a society.
Quantity
The lower class, whose single asset is numbers.
Demagogue
The leader who mobilises quantity on resentment of quality's wealth, and drives it toward revolution.
Power politics
The theory Machiavelli's The Prince became: politics conducted for state power alone.
Immorality (Machiavelli's)
Not immorality as such, but immorality which is justified, because it serves the state.
Mercenary forces
Soldiers who fight for money. Machiavelli rejects them for a national army, which is always patriotic.
Pre-emptive strike
Attacking first because war is inevitable, on the principle that the best defence is offence.
Secular state
A state separated from the church, with no declared religion. Machiavelli is its founder, hence father of modern political science.
Utilitarianism
Bentham's doctrine: an action or law is good if it produces the greatest happiness for the greatest number, judged only by consequences.
The two sovereigns
Pleasure and pain, under which nature has placed the individual, and which explain all action.
Utilitarian calculus
Bentham's calculator for testing an action, including intensity, certainty, propinquity and extent.
Propinquity
Closeness or nearness of the pleasure. Note it has appeared in a past paper as a synonym question with the sense kinship.
Self-test

🎴 Flashcards

Why is Aristotle called the father of political science?

He developed the subject as a science: systematic study, observation and experimentation. Social science rather than natural science, but science.

Aristotle on population size, and why?

Neither too large nor too small. Too large and law cannot be implemented; too small and the state cannot defend itself.

The two population figures (Plato's is the one flagged for MCQs)?

Aristotle: 15,000 to 100,000. Plato: 5,040.

Aristotle on the location of the ideal state?

Not landlocked, because a sea route carries exports and imports; but not very close to the sea either, or it can be attacked from the sea.

How do Plato and Aristotle divide society?

Plato into three classes; Aristotle into two, citizens and slaves.

Aristotle's six tasks, and who does them?

Slaves: agriculture, art, craft. Citizens: defence, governing, religion. Among citizens the young defend, the middle-aged govern, the old protect religion.

Aristotle on education?

Very important, can create a state, must be public and state-controlled rather than private. But only male citizens have the right to it.

On what two grounds do you criticise Aristotle?

Racial discrimination (he supports slavery; Greeks superior, others slaves) and gender discrimination. Beyond those, his state is practicable.

Plato vs Aristotle on law?

Plato's rulers are above the law and are the law, so no constitution is needed: absolute rule. Aristotle demands a constitution, and nobody is above the law: equality before law, rule of law.

Plato vs Aristotle on property?

Plato denies rulers and soldiers private property. Aristotle says that is impracticable and against human nature: you may divide property so there is neither rich nor poor, but you cannot abolish it.

What does "Aristotle was a great but not a grateful student of Plato" ask you to do?

Compare both ideal states: similarities in the topics they cover, differences on law, property and the nature of the state. Plato is utopia, Aristotle is realistic and practicable.

On what two bases does Aristotle classify governments?

The number of rulers, and whether the form is good or bad.

Give the full six-box classification.

One: monarchy (good) / tyranny (bad). Few: aristocracy (good) / oligarchy (bad). Many: polity (good) / democracy (bad).

What actually separates a good form from a bad one?

Not the number but whose interest is served, and whether there is law. Good forms rule through established laws for society's interest; bad forms serve self-interest.

Define monarchy and tyranny in Aristotle's terms.

Monarchy: one person ruling through established laws, promoting society's interest. Tyranny: one person following no law, whose words are law, ruling by fear.

Best and worst forms according to Aristotle?

Best: polity. Worst: democracy.

Why is democracy the bad form for Aristotle?

It is Greek direct democracy, not the modern kind, and it lacks education. Without education democracy is mobocracy: mob lynching and mob justice.

What would Aristotle call Norway, Denmark and Switzerland?

Polity. Their democracy is a refined form, because it has the education Aristotle demanded.

Run the cycle of governments.

Monarchy decays into tyranny (unstable); the few overthrow the tyrant into aristocracy (stable); aristocracy corrupts into oligarchy; the oligarchs are overthrown into polity, where each class has its own rule.

What is the engine of the cycle?

Self-interest. A good form rules for society; corruption turns it into its bad twin; overthrow turns each bad form into the next good one.

What are quality and quantity?

Quality is the elite: education and ideas, the ruling ideas of a society. Quantity is the lower class: numbers, and only numbers.

What happens if either force rules alone, and what is the fix?

Rule by quality alone or quantity alone is bad government; quantity alone follows demagogues into revolution. The fix is to balance them by empowering the middle class, which is polity.

What was Machiavelli attacking?

A thousand years in which the state was a means to an end, that end being salvation and redemption, with the church as guide.

State Machiavelli's central claim.

The state is not a means to an end. The state is an end in itself: your only purpose. Protect it, make it powerful, unify it.

Machiavelli on armies?

Not mercenaries, who fight for money. A strong national army, because it is always patriotic, equipped with modern weapons.

Feared or loved?

Feared. Love brings ruin. Be powerful and be ruthless, and the world respects you.

Machiavelli on peace, expansion and war?

Peace is a delusion; prepare for war in peacetime. The state must expand or expire. War is inevitable, so strike first: the best defence is offence.

Machiavelli on neutrality and alliances?

Neutrality is hypocrisy: do not call yourself neutral when two neighbours go to war. Only your interests are permanent, and the interest that counts is the national interest.

Why is Machiavelli the father of modern political science?

He separates church from state and makes the state its own end, founding the secular state. Aristotle is the father of political science; Machiavelli of modern political science.

What are Bentham's two sovereigns?

Pleasure and pain. Nature has placed the individual under them, and every action seeks to maximise pleasure and minimise pain.

State the utilitarian test.

Any law, rule or action is good, moral, legal and just if it produces the greatest happiness for the greatest number. Judge consequences, never intentions.

Name the checks of the utilitarian calculus covered in class.

Intensity (how great), certainty (is the pleasure sure), propinquity (how close), extent (how many people it affects).

How does Kant oppose Bentham?

Kant holds there are absolute principles binding whatever the consequences: killing, stealing and lying are bad full stop. Bentham holds there are no absolutes, only consequences.

Use Batman to explain the split.

Batman is Kant: he can kill the Joker and refuses, even knowing he will escape and kill again. Bentham would kill him, for the greatest happiness of the greatest number.

  • The classification is his most important contribution, and it was asked in 2016 00:58:27. Two named MCQs come off it: best form is polity, worst form is democracy 00:37:17.

  • Answer "great but not grateful" as a comparison, on rails 00:25:03: character of the state, division of society, law and constitution, property. Same topics, opposite conclusions. Close with utopia against practicable.

  • Quality, quantity and the middle class is your depth card. It explains why polity is best rather than just asserting it, and it transfers straight into essays on inequality, populism and democratic stability.
