⭐ The five-minute version

  • What it is. A 10-mark question that gives you Urdu (ten short sentences, or sometimes a short paragraph) to render into English. It tests whether you can carry a thought across, including idioms and figurative expressions, not whether you know dictionary equivalents.
  • Read first, translate later. Read the whole Urdu passage two, three, four times before translating a single sentence, to grasp its tone and message. "English translation is not just about translating, it's about reading."
  • Translate by sense, not by word. Literal, word-for-word translation produces nonsense (a phrase meaning "I became happy" translated literally becomes "my heart became garden garden"). Carry the meaning, not the words.
  • Rearrange into natural English. Urdu puts the verb last (subject, object, verb); English does not (subject, verb, object). Reorder the words to fit natural English syntax.
  • Go clause by clause, tense by tense. Break a long sentence into its clauses and translate each. Identify each sentence's tense (past, present, future) and keep it.
  • Do not freeze on hard words. Small words cause more trouble than big ones. Guess the right word from context and move on rather than getting stuck.
  • Handle idioms by meaning. You do not need the exact English equivalent of an Urdu proverb; give its overall sense in plain English.
  • Keep the vocabulary simple. Do not reach for complex or archaic English. Clear, correct, natural English scores; showy English that distorts the meaning does not.
1 The skill

🎯 What translation actually tests

Practice

The translation question hands you Urdu and asks for English, and the temptation is to swap words one for one. That is exactly what it is not testing.

Sir Danish is precise about the skill under examination: it tests your ability to express a thought written in Urdu in the English language, and specifically whether you can handle idioms and figurative expressions Practice · 06:14. A thought, not a string of words. The unit that has to survive the crossing is the meaning of the sentence, and everything in the method below serves that.

The format. The FPSC scheme sets it as ten short Urdu sentences to translate into English, chosen to involve structural composition, significant terms, and figurative or idiomatic expressions. Some actual papers instead give a single short Urdu paragraph for the same 10 marks. Prepare for both; the method does not change.

Translation is a reading test wearing a writing test's clothes. His single most repeated line here: "English translation is not just about translating, it's about reading. So try to improve your reading" Practice · 43:10. If you have genuinely understood the Urdu, the English is a matter of tidy expression. If you have not, no amount of good English will rescue a sentence you did not grasp.

2 The first move

👀 Read the whole thing before you write

Practice

The most common mistake is starting to translate sentence one before understanding the passage. The method starts, like précis, with reading.

Read the entire Urdu passage several times, two, three, four times, to grasp the tone and the message, before translating a single sentence Practice · 09:09. Only once the whole thing sits clearly in your head do you begin turning it into English. The passage is a single connected argument; translating sentence one in isolation risks a choice of word that the later sentences then contradict.

Read and understand is "the most important thing in translation." His words. The problem in a bad translation is almost never that "the English is not coming out"; it is that the Urdu was never fully understood Practice · 08:38. Fix the reading and the English follows.

3 The core principle

🔁 Translate by sense, not word for word

Practice

This is the principle everything else hangs on. You are translating meaning, not swapping words, and literal translation is the fastest way to produce nonsense.

His worked warning: an Urdu expression for becoming happy, translated word for word, comes out as "my heart became garden garden" Practice · 08:04. Literally accurate, completely wrong. What you had to carry across was the feeling, "I became happy", not the individual words. Understand what the phrase is doing, then say that in English.

Literal translation breaks the grammar too, well beyond idioms. Urdu and English order their words differently, so a word-for-word carry breaks the structure. Take the sentence "he eats food": in Urdu the pieces arrive as subject, object, verb (he food eats); in English they must be subject, verb, object (he eats food) Practice · 11:57. So you rearrange the words to fit natural English syntax rather than preserving the Urdu order.

The test of a good translation. Read your English sentence back as if it were written by a native speaker. If it sounds like something no English speaker would ever say, you have translated the words and lost the sense. Go back to what the Urdu meant and say that instead.

4 The rules

🧰 The working rules

Practice

Under the "sense not word" principle sit a handful of concrete working rules. Together they are a checklist you can run on any sentence.

RuleWhat it means
Read the whole passage firstTwo to four times, for tone and message, before translating anything Practice · 09:09.
Do not freeze on hard wordsDo not get stuck on an unfamiliar word. Guess its meaning from context and keep moving Practice · 10:30.
Small words are the real trouble"Small words create more trouble than large big words while doing translation" Practice · 11:18. Watch the particles and prepositions, not the long nouns.
Translate clause by clauseA long sentence is several clauses. Identify them, take each separately, translate each, then join Practice · 19:53.
Translate tense by tenseIdentify whether each sentence is past, present or future in the Urdu, and keep that tense in the English Practice · 13:27.

Keep vocabulary simpleNo overly complex or archaic English. Communicate the meaning cleanly Practice · 14:20.

Why "small words" matter more than you would think. A long noun usually has one obvious English equivalent. It is the little connective words, the particles that show relation, cause, time, direction, that carry the grammar of the sentence, and getting one wrong quietly changes the meaning. This is the same lesson the prepositions carry in the grammar section: the small words do the heavy lifting.

5 Worked example

📜 Clause by clause: a passage on Sir Syed Ahmed Khan

Practice

The class translates a dense Urdu passage about Sir Syed Ahmed Khan and his son Syed Mahmood, and it is a perfect demonstration of the clause-by-clause method on a sentence that would be impossible to translate whole.

The Urdu opens with a long, layered sentence. Rather than attack it whole, Sir Danish breaks it at its clauses and takes them one at a time Practice · 19:53:

Urdu clause (sense)English rendering
the phrase for colonialist / imperialist English values"in front of the imperialist English values" (colonialism = imperialism)
the sycophantic and obedient attitude"contrary to the sycophantic and obedient attitude"
that the British rulers had promoted and the people of India had adopted"that the British rulers had propagated and the people of India had adopted during that time"
Sir Syed and his bright-minded son endeavoured..."Sir Syed Ahmed Khan and his most dignified and bright-minded son, Syed Mahmood, endeavoured to adopt an attitude as if they were equal to, and on the same status as, the English"

Assembled, the clauses become one clean English sentence: Contrary to the sycophantic and obedient attitude towards the imperialist English values that the British rulers had propagated and the people of India had adopted during that time, Sir Syed Ahmed Khan and his most dignified and bright-minded son, Syed Mahmood, endeavoured to adopt an attitude as if they were equal to and on the same status as the English Practice · 40:19. A sentence that looked untranslatable becomes routine once it is cut into clauses.

The history behind the passage checks out. The passage refers to a real episode. At the Agra Durbar of 1867, a medal was to be conferred on Sir Syed Ahmed Khan, but he stayed away because Indians had been given seating inferior to the English. The medal was later delivered to him at Aligarh, where he went on to found the Muhammadan Anglo-Oriental College (a school in 1875, a college in 1877), which became Aligarh Muslim University in 1920. His son Syed Mahmood studied in England and became one of the first Indian judges of a High Court. You are translating the Urdu, not asserting the history, but knowing the story helps you choose the right words.

What to copy from this. Never try to hold a long sentence whole. Find its clauses, translate each into clean English, then reassemble, checking that the joined sentence reads naturally. The dense Sir Syed sentence and a simple one submit to exactly the same move.

6 The trap

🪤 Idioms and figurative expressions

Practice

The passage is deliberately seeded with idioms and figurative phrases, because that is where literal translators fall down. The rule for them is the same as the whole method, only sharper.

Translate the proverb by its meaning, not its equivalent. You do not need to know the exact English proverb that matches an Urdu one. Understand what the Urdu proverb or idiom means, and give that overall meaning in plain English Practice · 12:55. Hunting for a perfect English idiom often wastes time and misfires; a clear statement of the sense always lands.

The "my heart became garden garden" example is the whole lesson in miniature Practice · 08:04. The Urdu is a vivid figure for delight. The literal English is meaningless. The right translation, "I became happy" or "I was delighted", throws away the picture and keeps the point. In translation, meaning beats imagery every time.

The mindset for the ten sentences. Expect at least a few idiomatic or figurative lines among the ten, since the paper is built to include them. When you hit one, do not panic and do not go literal. Ask what the sentence means, then write the simplest true English version of that meaning. A plain, correct rendering of an idiom scores; a literal one that reads as nonsense does not.

X Strategy

🎯 Exam focus

The order of operations for the ten sentences

  1. Read all of it first. Even ten separate sentences share a register; read them through before writing.
  2. For each sentence, get the meaning. What is this sentence actually saying? Say it to yourself in plain terms.
  3. Identify the tense and keep it.
  4. If it is long, split it into clauses and take them one by one.
  5. Write natural English in subject-verb-object order, simple vocabulary.
  6. Read it back as a native speaker would. If it sounds wrong, return to the meaning.

The mistakes that cost the marks

MistakeFix

The one line to remember. Translation rewards understanding, not vocabulary. If you have truly read and grasped the Urdu, the English is just tidy expression of a meaning you already hold. So spend your effort on the reading, translate the sense clause by clause, and write the plainest natural English that says the same thing. Ten clean, faithful sentences take the ten marks.
