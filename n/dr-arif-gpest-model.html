⭐ The five-minute version

  • GPEST is the lecturer's essay-planning adaptation. It begins with governance, then examines politics, economy, society and technology or technical matters. Its purpose is to help a writer generate and sort points.
  • Governance means decisions and their execution. In this class it covers policy direction, policy making and implementation. The teacher treats it as wider than the elected government alone.
  • The lenses can suggest a chain. Governance can shape political conditions; politics can affect the economy; economic conditions reach society; and social conditions interact with technology or a topic's technical features.
  • Classify by the substance of the point. Money usually signals economy; effects on people often signal society; a decision, policy or implementation failure points toward governance; and subject-specific operations belong under technical.
  • Words can mislead. "Research culture" sounds social because it contains "culture", yet within a university problem the lecture classifies it as technical because it concerns research and teaching practice.
  • The framework does not write the outline for you. Several points may land in one category. Rearrange them manually so each point prepares the ground for the next.
  • Coherence comes from relationships. The worked example links policy direction, implementation, merit, interference, fees, teaching methods and research culture as a progressive chain instead of seven isolated complaints.
  • Apply the table to each major heading. Scan one heading through the available indicators, keep only productive lenses, then select, merge and order the resulting points.
1 Build the lens

🧠 What the GPEST model is

00:00-05:23

The lecturer presents GPEST as his adaptation of the familiar PEST or PESTEL family of planning frameworks. His change is to place governance at the front so that essay planning begins with policy direction, decision making and implementation 00:00:01.

Governance in the course model

Policy direction

What the responsible authority intends to achieve and where it wants the sector to go.

Policy making

How rules, choices and institutional priorities are formed.

Implementation

Whether the chosen policy is carried through in practice 00:00:46.

The teacher separates governance from the narrow idea of "government". The lens asks who makes a decision, how it is institutionalised, and why execution succeeds or fails. It can therefore reveal governance points inside organisations as well as the state 00:00:01.

Why governance comes first here

The lecture says governance should ideally follow political direction. The teacher reverses that order for Pakistan-oriented essays, arguing that interruptions of elected politics strengthened administrative and establishment structures relative to political institutions 00:00:46 00:04:14. This is the course rationale, not a universal rule.

Correction and exam caution. The lecture states that Pakistan experienced "governance but no politics" for 34 of 79 years 00:02:53. Treat both the count and the absolute wording as a rounded classroom formulation. Official parliamentary histories identify constitutional interruptions beginning on 7 October 1958, 5 July 1977 and 12 October 1999, but a total varies with the chosen end points and with whether military-led civilian phases are included. In an exam, define the period and evidence it instead of quoting 34/79 as a self-explanatory statistic.

The T bucket is deliberately wider than devices or digital systems. "Technical" means points specific to the subject being analysed 00:06:27. In a university essay, teacher training, curriculum and research are technical. In an agriculture essay, seeds, fertilisers and agricultural research can play the same role 00:07:02.

The chain is a prompt, not a compulsory order. The teacher uses G → P → E → S → T to reveal connections. Later he explicitly says the framework will not fit every topic or every point 00:17:35 00:32:38.

3 Diagnose the point

🏷️ Classify an indicator

08:39-14:28

The classroom drill takes problems already gathered for higher education and asks which GPEST indicator best describes each one. The skill lies in identifying what the statement is actually about, rather than reacting to one visible word 00:09:04.

Fast diagnostic cues

  • Follow money: a fee, cost or funding point usually belongs under economy 00:12:03.
  • Follow decisions: policy direction and implementation call for a governance test.
  • Follow people: groups and social outcomes may signal society, but the people affected do not automatically determine the category.
  • Follow the mechanism: teaching, research and curriculum belong under technical when that operation is the centre of the claim.

4 Add judgment

🔄 Rearrange the buckets

14:28-18:19

Classification is an intermediate step. Once the points sit under G, P, E, S and T, the writer must inspect several points inside the same bucket and decide which order gives the strongest logical progression 00:14:57.

What "manually tweak" means

  1. Collect first. Let several points land in the same indicator without forcing a sequence too early.
  2. Find dependency. Ask whether one point is a cause, an implementation failure, evidence, a consequence or a condition for another.
  3. Test both directions. Compare A → B → C with C → B → A and keep the order that explains rather than merely lists.
  4. Write the link aloud. If a sentence cannot explain why B follows A, the connection may be artificial.

Important boundary. The lecture says GPEST is not to be applied mechanically to every point in the final outline 00:17:35. It produces categories and possibilities. Selection and sequence remain the writer's responsibility.

From bucket to topic sentence

The demonstration starts with a broad entry into the governance cluster: "There are several problems in higher education in Pakistan, chief among which is the lack of direction in educational policies" 00:18:19. The sentence opens a chain, but the value of that chain comes from the relationships added after it.

Exam use. Never reproduce G, P, E, S and T as empty headings. Turn the useful material inside them into specific argumentative headings and paragraphs.

5 Watch coherence form

🔗 Higher education worked chain

18:19-28:11

The central worked example rearranges seven complaints about higher education so that each paragraph strengthens the next. The note preserves this as a writing demonstration, not as an independently established diagnosis of every Pakistani university.

MovePointLink to the next move

2Lack of policy implementationNon-merit admissions are offered as evidence of failed implementation 00:19:13.
3Admissions not based on meritThe example links the admission failure to political interference 00:20:22.

Three relationship types

Evidence: non-merit admissions support the implementation claim. Cause and effect: political interference explains the admission problem, which affects students' options. Concession: "despite" links high fees with outdated teaching without claiming that fees cause those methods 00:26:21.

6 Generate material

📋 Brainstorm with a table

28:11-33:15

The method moves upstream from outlining to brainstorming. Draw a table, place the indicators across it, and record points under the lens that best explains each one 00:28:11.

The prism method

The teacher compares the framework to a prism: send one main heading into it and receive a spectrum of possible angles 00:30:30. The analogy captures the right use of the model. One heading is scanned through several lenses, producing candidate material that can later be filtered.

  1. Write one main heading at the top of the page.
  2. Draw the indicator columns beneath it: governance, politics, economy, society and technology or technical.
  3. Ask one question per lens and record whatever relevant point appears.
  4. Leave a lens blank when it yields nothing useful. The model is not applicable in full to every topic 00:32:38.
  5. Repeat for the next major heading on a separate table rather than mixing different sections together.

When the mind feels blank. Replace the vague instruction "brainstorm" with five small prompts: what is the policy issue, the power issue, the money issue, the people issue, and the technical issue? This is the practical relief the lecture offers at 00:29:00.

Keep the worksheet off the final page. The table is a private planning device. The examiner should see selected arguments in a logical outline, not the scaffolding that generated them.

7 Widen the scan

33:15-38:23

Five lenses may leave important material hidden, so the second half of the class widens the brainstorming template to twelve indicators for expository and argumentative essays 00:33:37.

The recoverable indicator set

Core GPEST

Governance; politics; economy; society; technology or technical.

Additional scan

Environment; legal; religion; history; media; emotional; moral or ethical.

The added lenses can reveal ecological conditions, legal gaps, historical roots, religious dimensions, media framing, emotional responses and ethical tensions that the five core categories may miss.

The standard expansion is Legal; PESTEL or PESTLE covers political, economic, social or sociocultural, technological, environmental and legal factors. The added governance, religion, history, media, emotional and moral or ethical lenses belong to this lecturer's extended essay template.

8 Finish the plan

🛠️ Convert analysis into an outline

38:23-42:17

Structured planning makes the first version easier to sequence, but the table still has to be turned into an essay plan. The last stage removes weak material, strengthens links and phrases the survivors as meaningful headings and topic-sentence claims.

End-to-end workflow

  1. Read the exact demand. Identify the major sections the topic requires.
  2. Create one indicator table per section. Causes, effects and remedies are scanned separately.
  3. Generate without forcing. Keep productive lenses and leave irrelevant ones blank.
  4. Classify by substance. Look at the mechanism behind each point, not one misleading word.
  5. Remove duplicates and weak points. Similar ideas may need merging; peripheral ones should go.
  6. Sequence within each category. Use cause, evidence, contrast and consequence to build progression.
  7. Sequence across categories. Use GPEST order when it clarifies the case, and change it when another order is stronger.
  8. Draft topic sentences. Each paragraph should state a specific claim and make its relation to the previous paragraph visible.

Quality-control questions

Relevance

Does this point answer the exact heading being brainstormed?

Classification

What is the point mainly about: a decision, power, money, people or a technical mechanism?

Relationship

Is the link causal, evidential, concessive, sequential or merely superficial?

Coherence

Can one sentence explain why the next paragraph follows the previous one?

Revision sheet

🎯 Exam focus

GPEST on one page

  1. Frame and scan: break the topic into major headings, then run each heading through the useful indicators.
  2. Classify: identify the substance and mechanism of each candidate point.
  3. Filter and relate: remove repetition and weak relevance; mark cause, evidence, contrast, concession and consequence.
  4. Sequence: choose the strongest order within and across the surviving clusters.
  5. Phrase: turn the chosen points into meaningful headings and topic sentences.

Five rapid prompts

  • G: What policy, institution, decision or implementation issue matters?
  • P: What power, interest, actor or interference matters?
  • E: What cost, resource, funding or incentive matters?
  • S: Which people, behaviours, groups or social effects matter?
  • T: What technology or topic-specific mechanism matters?

Common mistakes

  • Using GPEST labels as final essay headings.
  • Forcing every indicator into every topic.
  • Classifying by a visible word instead of the point's substance.
  • Leaving points in brainstorm order or assuming proximity creates causation.
  • Turning a contrast such as high fees and outdated teaching into a causal claim.
  • Scanning causes, effects and remedies in one mixed table.
  • Quoting the rounded military-rule total without definitions or evidence.
